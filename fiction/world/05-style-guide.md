# Writing Tone & Style Guide

## The Aetherfall Fiction Project

This document governs the voice, tone, and craft of all Aetherfall fiction. Every story, scene, and sentence should be measured against the principles here.

---

## Primary Influences

Three authors shape the Aetherfall fiction voice. Each contributes something specific.

**Garth Nix (Abhorsen / Old Kingdom)**
What we take: Prose register. Pacing. Accessibility. The art of making the strange feel tangible without over-explaining it. Nix's gradient between Ancelstierre and the Old Kingdom is the direct ancestor of Aetherfall's zone system -- and his ability to make that gradient *felt* rather than *described* is the model. His prose is clean, propulsive, and trusts the reader. That's our baseline.

**C.S. Friedman (Coldfire Trilogy)**
What we take: Character depth. The consequences of power. Moral complexity that doesn't slide into nihilism. Friedman writes characters who make terrible choices for understandable reasons, and she never flinches from the cost. The Damien Vryce arc -- a good man navigating impossible alliances, paying in pieces of himself -- is the emotional template for our protagonists. Her treatment of how magic and belief shape reality is directly relevant to Aetherfall's cosmology. We also take her understanding of balance: if magic were free, mages would rule everything. The cost is what makes the power meaningful.

**Tad Williams (Memory, Sorrow, and Thorn / Otherland)**
What we take: The lived-in world. Williams builds settings that feel like they existed before page one and will continue after the last page. He rarely does exposition -- instead, the world reveals itself through what characters take for granted, what surprises them, and what they argue about at dinner. His cities have smells. His forests have weather. His politics have history that nobody agrees on. That sense of depth without the burden of explanation is what we're after.

---

## Pacing & Structure

**Model: Conan / Doc Savage / Novella**

These are not epic fantasy novels. They are short, propulsive, self-contained stories that happen to exist in a deep world. Each story should:

- Open in motion or in the immediate aftermath of something happening
- Establish the stakes within the first few pages
- Escalate through complication, not exposition
- Resolve with consequence -- not everything tied up neatly, but the central tension addressed
- Plant at least one seed for a future story

Target length is 8,000-15,000 words per story. Long enough to breathe, short enough to read in a sitting.

Chapters should be short -- 1,500-3,000 words. Cut to a new chapter when the POV shifts or the scene changes. End chapters on tension, not resolution. The reader should always want the next page more than they want to stop.

## Point of View

**Alternating tight third-person (Friedman model)**

Rotate POV across the four party members. Each POV chapter is filtered entirely through that character's perception, knowledge, and emotional state. The reader sees the world through their eyes, hears their internal voice, and is limited to what they know.

This means:

- **Kael's chapters** are observational and tactical. He notices exits, counts opponents, reads body language. His internal voice is dry and resigned. He doesn't think in abstractions -- he thinks in what works and what doesn't.
- **Sera's chapters** are sensory and charged. She feels the Aether before she sees its effects. Her internal landscape is vivid, sometimes overwhelming. She's young enough to still be awed and scared by what she can do.
- **Aldric's chapters** are analytical and quietly anxious. He categorizes, cross-references, notices inconsistencies. His internal voice sounds like a researcher's notebook -- precise, cautious, occasionally surprised by his own courage.
- **Mira's chapters** are social and perceptive. She reads rooms, not books. She notices who's lying, who's afraid, who's in charge. Her internal voice is wry and fast, like someone who learned early that charm is a survival skill.

Not every story needs all four POVs. Some stories may focus on two or three characters, with others in supporting roles. But across the series, every character should get substantial POV time.

## Prose Register

**Nix-level accessibility with Friedman's precision and Williams' sense of place.**

In practice, this means:

- **Sentences are mostly short and declarative.** The default rhythm is subject-verb-object. Complexity earns its place; it isn't the default.
- **Descriptions are concrete and sensory.** Not "the zone was Aetheric" but "the air tasted like copper pennies and the shadows moved wrong." Ground every abstraction in something a body can feel.
- **Psychological interiority is precise.** When we're inside a character's head, the prose tightens. Friedman's gift is making internal conflict feel as urgent as external combat. A character deciding whether to trust someone should be as tense as a firefight.
- **World-building is ambient.** Williams' approach. The world reveals itself through what characters notice, ignore, and take for granted. A character who grew up post-Tear doesn't explain what the Aether is -- she adjusts her jacket against the Aetheric chill the way you'd adjust against wind. A character who remembers "before" might pause for half a beat when something magical happens -- not shock, just the ghost of surprise that never quite went away.

**Avoid:**
- Purple prose. If a sentence is beautiful but doesn't move the story or deepen character, cut it.
- Exposition dumps. If you need to explain something about the world, find a character who would naturally say it, think it, or react to it. If no character would, the reader doesn't need it yet.
- Modern slang or anachronism. The world is 1920s-analog. The language should feel slightly formal by modern standards without being stiff. "Damn" and "hell" are fine. Internet-era phrasing is not.
- Adverb-heavy action prose. "She quickly drew her sword" is weaker than "She drew." Speed lives in sentence length and verb choice, not adverbs.

## Tone: Dark, Gritty, Noir -- With Wonder

**The darkness:** This world is hard. People get hurt. Choices have costs. The Society takes jobs that put them in danger because the alternative is poverty and irrelevance. The Aether is beautiful and it will eat you alive if you're careless. The Engine hums and the people who control it don't care about the ones it displaces. Authority is compromised. Trust is earned in inches and lost in seconds.

**The hope:** These are good people. Not saints -- they'll fight dirty, bend rules, and make deals they're not proud of. But they have a line, and they hold it. When a civilian is in danger, they act. When a job asks them to cross the line, they push back. The Damien Vryce model: idealism tested by a world that doesn't reward it, held onto anyway because the alternative is worse.

**The wonder:** The Aether is terrifying AND magnificent. The first time a character walks into a Wild Zone should feel like stepping into a cathedral made of light and wrong angles. Deep Wild should evoke the Clayr's glacier -- vast, ancient, humbling, dangerous. Galvanic technology has its own wonder -- the first time someone sees an arc gun discharge, the blue-white light and the smell of ozone should land with weight. Wonder and dread are not opposites. They coexist.

**The noir:** Investigation, moral ambiguity, rain on cobblestones, conversations in dim taverns where everyone is lying about something. The Society operates in the gaps between factions, and the gaps are dark. Not every villain is a villain. Not every ally is trustworthy. The noir lives in the spaces between what people say and what they mean.

## The Cardinal Rule

**Never reference game mechanics.**

No dice. No checks. No skill targets. No tier names. No exhaustion points. No reliability percentages. No "Galvanic +3 zone." No spell school names used as jargon. The game mechanics exist behind the curtain. The fiction lives in front of it.

**Translation guide:**

| Game Mechanic | Fiction Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Casting target / skill check | The character attempts something. They succeed or fail based on talent, training, circumstance, and luck. |
| Tier result (Weak/Standard/Strong/Spectacular) | The effect is described by its scale and feel. A weak spell sputters. A spectacular spell reshapes the room. |
| Exhaustion points | Physical cost. Fatigue, nausea, trembling hands, the taste of blood. The body paying for what the mind demanded. |
| Backlash / Wild Effects | Visceral, visible consequences. Eyes glowing. Nosebleeds. The air around the caster going wrong. |
| Reliability / malfunction | The gun clicks empty, or sparks and dies, or bucks in the hand. Described as the weapon's behavior, not a percentage. |
| Zone levels | Felt through the senses. The hum in Galvanic zones. The copper taste in Aetheric zones. The way equipment behaves differently. |
| Wound tiers | Pain. Slowing down. Blood loss. The body failing in specific, described ways. |
| Station / Ledger | Economic reality. What the character can afford. What the Society can't. Where they live. What they wear. |

**The test:** If a sentence could appear in a rulebook, it doesn't belong in the fiction. If a sentence could only exist in a story -- because it's grounded in a specific character's experience, a specific sensory moment, a specific emotional truth -- it belongs.

**Behind-the-scenes mechanics are welcome** as a creative tool. Roll on the Wild Effects table to determine what happens to a character after a bad cast. Use the damage system to decide how badly someone is hurt. Let the mechanics generate surprise that the narrative then makes real. But the reader never sees the roll. They only see the bleeding nose, the trembling hands, the eyes that won't stop glowing.

## Magic in Fiction

Magic in Aetherfall is new, costly, and visceral. Writing it well is critical to the tone.

**Casting should feel physical.**
Magic is not pointing a finger and willing something to happen. It's reaching into something vast and pulling a thread of it through your body. It exhausts. It burns. It leaves marks. A caster after a hard fight should look like someone who ran a marathon in the rain -- shaking, pale, eyes too bright.

**Wild casting is frightening.**
Sera doesn't fully control what comes through. When she reaches for the Aether, it reaches back -- and sometimes it gives her more than she asked for. The sensation should be visceral: heat behind the eyes, a sound like tearing cloth that only she can hear, the feeling of something vast turning its attention toward her. Wild casting is surfing a wave you didn't choose, and the wave has opinions.

**Scholarly casting is precise but not safe.**
Aldric's magic is disciplined -- shaped by study, constrained by understanding. But discipline doesn't eliminate risk. It just makes the risk predictable. He knows exactly how much a healing spell will cost him. He pays it anyway, because the alternative is watching someone die.

**Backlash is identity-altering.**
When the magic goes wrong, it doesn't just hurt. It changes you. Eyes that glow in the dark. Hands that won't stop trembling. A voice that sounds like someone else's. These aren't debuffs -- they're the price of touching something inhuman, and they accumulate. A caster who's been working hard for months looks different from the person they were. Other people notice.

**The Aether has presence.**
In strong Aetheric zones, the air itself feels alive. Not sentient exactly, but aware -- like standing in a river and feeling the current notice you. This is not metaphor. Something beyond the Veil is real, and where the Veil is thin, its presence bleeds through. Characters should feel this as a physical sensation: warmth, pressure, the sense of being watched by something too large to see.

## Technology in Fiction

Galvanic technology is not steampunk aesthetic. It's functional, strange, and contested.

**Firearms are familiar but fragile.**
Revolvers, rifles, shotguns -- these are real, established technology that people understand and trust. But that trust erodes near magic. A revolver that jams when you need it most isn't a dice roll -- it's a betrayal. Write the malfunction as the weapon's failure, not a game mechanic.

**Galvanic weapons are impressive and unreliable.**
Arc pistols, exotic sidearms, resonance devices -- these are bleeding-edge prototypes that most people have never seen. They hit harder than conventional firearms, but they're temperamental, poorly understood, and announce your presence to anyone with Aetheric sensitivity. Owning one is a statement. Relying on one is a risk.

**The Engine has presence too.**
In deep Galvanic zones, the Engine's influence is physical. A hum in the bones. Compasses locking to a direction that isn't north. The feeling of something vast and mechanical turning beneath the surface of the world. It's not alive the way the Aether might be -- but it's not nothing, either.

**Swords still matter.**
A blade works everywhere. In a Deep Wild Zone where guns are paperweights and arc pistols are dead metal, a good sword is the difference between life and death. Characters carry blades not because they're romantic -- because they're practical. In a world where your primary weapon might stop working between one block and the next, a backup that never fails is essential.

## Violence & Consequence

**Violence is real.**
People bleed. Bones break. Wounds don't heal between scenes unless someone pays the magical cost. A character who takes a shotgun blast to the chest is not fighting at full capacity in the next scene -- they're fighting through pain, blood loss, and the growing certainty that they need to end this fast.

**Violence has weight.**
Killing someone should never be casual. Even enemies. Even in self-defense. The Society does what it has to, but the narrative should acknowledge the cost. Not through moralizing -- through the silence after, the way a character's hands shake, the drink they need that evening.

**Injury accumulates across stories.**
Scars, old wounds, the knee that never healed right. Characters carry their history in their bodies. This is continuity that matters -- it makes the world feel real and the stakes feel permanent.

**Maiming is possible.**
If a story calls for it, characters can lose things permanently. A finger. An eye. The steady hands that made them a marksman. This should be rare, dramatic, and irreversible. It should change who the character is, not just what they can do.

## World Exposition: The Williams Principle

**Never explain. Inhabit.**

The reader learns the world the way a tourist learns a city: by walking through it, overhearing conversations, noticing what the locals take for granted, and being confused by things that nobody bothers to explain.

**Techniques:**

- **Assumed knowledge.** Characters know their world. They don't explain what the Aether is to each other. They react to it. "The gradient's shifted again" is dialogue. "The gradient, which is the boundary between zones of Aetheric and Galvanic influence, has shifted" is a textbook.
- **Generational perspective.** A character born after the Tear treats magic as normal. A character who remembers "before" still catches on the strangeness sometimes. These different reactions teach the reader without any character explicitly teaching.
- **Incidental detail.** The world-building lives in the margins. A shopkeeper who sells ammunition and spell components in the same shop. A street sign that lists distance in blocks and zone stability in colored tags. A child who complains that the Galvanic hum gives her a headache. These details are never the point of the scene -- they're the texture of the scene.
- **Contradiction as depth.** Two characters disagree about why the Tear happened. Neither is corrected by the narrative. The reader absorbs that this is a world where the biggest questions have no settled answers. Williams does this constantly -- his characters have different understandings of history, religion, and politics, and the narrative never arbitrates.

## Dialogue

**Register: Hardboiled but human.**

The existing Aetherfall voice callouts -- the Handler, the Scholar, the Street, the Believer -- establish the range. Dialogue should feel grounded in a 1920s-adjacent world: slightly more formal than modern speech, but not stilted. People use contractions. They swear. They trail off. They interrupt each other.

**Character voice matters.**
Kael speaks in short sentences. He doesn't waste words. When he does talk at length, it's because something matters enough to warrant it, and the other characters notice.

Sera speaks in bursts -- fast, emotional, circling back to rephrase things she said wrong the first time. She's young and verbal and hasn't learned to edit herself yet.

Aldric speaks precisely. He chooses words the way he chooses spells -- with care and awareness of their effect. When he's stressed, the precision cracks and the human underneath shows through.

Mira speaks like a negotiator. She mirrors whoever she's talking to -- matching their register, their pace, their assumptions. In private, with the Society, she's warmer and more profane. The gap between her public and private voices tells you who she really is.

## Series Continuity

These stories are interconnected. What happens in one matters in the next. This requires:

- **Consistent character development.** A relationship that deepens in Story 2 doesn't reset in Story 3. An injury from Story 1 is still healing in Story 2.
- **Accumulating world changes.** The faction dynamics in Ashwick shift as the Society acts. A smuggling operation disrupted in one story creates a power vacuum in the next.
- **Planted seeds.** Every story should include at least one detail -- a name, a rumor, a glimpse of something unexplained -- that pays off later. The reader who rereads should find things they missed.
- **Earned callbacks.** References to earlier stories should feel natural, not like fan service. A character mentions a past event because it's relevant to the present, not because the author wants you to remember it.

---

## Final Principle

**The stories come first.**

The world exists to serve the characters. The characters exist to serve the story. The story exists to make the reader feel something -- tension, wonder, dread, affection, the bittersweet satisfaction of a hard job done by decent people in a world that doesn't make it easy.

If a piece of world-building doesn't serve the story at hand, leave it out. If a character moment slows the pace, find a way to deliver it faster. If a beautiful sentence doesn't earn its place, cut it.

The world is vast. The stories are small. That's the point.
