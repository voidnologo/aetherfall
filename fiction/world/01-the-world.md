# The World

## A Setting Reference for Aetherfall Fiction

This document describes the world as it exists for the people who live in it -- not as a game setting, but as reality. Everything here should inform fiction without appearing in it as exposition. The reader of the stories will absorb this world through the characters' assumptions, reactions, and blind spots. The writer needs to know it all so they can choose what to reveal and what to leave in shadow.

---

## Before

Fifty years ago, the world made sense.

It was an industrial world -- steel and coal, telegraph wires and railroad ties, factories and foundries belching smoke into grey skies. Governments held territory through infrastructure: roads, bridges, rail lines, communication networks. The trajectory was clear. More machines. More control. More of everything that humans had built for themselves since they first smelted iron.

It was not a kind world. Labor was cheap and expendable. Colonial powers extracted wealth from the periphery and shipped it home. Organized crime followed the money. The gap between the powerful and the powerless was wide and widening. But it was *ordered*. People understood the shape of things. A factory worker knew what factory workers could expect. A soldier knew what soldiers were for. A scientist knew what science could measure.

Some people believed in something beyond the measurable. Occult societies met in candlelit rooms. Hedge scholars pored over texts that claimed to describe forces outside the material world. Spiritualists held seances for the bored wealthy. These people were tolerated the way eccentrics are always tolerated -- with amusement, occasional alarm, and the comfortable certainty that they were wrong.

They were not wrong.

---

## The Tear

It happened within months. The accounts disagree on exactly how many -- the infrastructure for keeping records was among the first things to fail -- but the consensus is that between the first signs and the full catastrophe, less than a year passed.

The Veil -- a word that came later, borrowed from the occultists who turned out to have been closer to the truth than anyone wanted to admit -- thinned across the world. Not uniformly. Not predictably. But everywhere, all at once, like a dam developing cracks in a hundred places simultaneously.

The Aether poured through.

What followed was not a war or a natural disaster. It was something for which no word yet existed. The world *changed*, in patches, without warning and without mercy.

Entire regions transformed. Settled agricultural heartlands erupted into forests that had never existed -- trees that grew in hours, undergrowth that shifted when you weren't watching, clearings where the air hummed with something that wasn't sound. The farmers who worked those fields went to bed in a world they understood and woke in one they didn't. Some fled. Some couldn't. Some found, to their terror or their wonder, that the new world *spoke to them* -- that they could feel the power in the changed air and shape it with their will.

Cities fractured. Urban districts experienced sudden, violent Aetheric saturation. In the neighborhoods where the Veil thinned most, machines died. Electricity failed. Engines seized. The infrastructure that connected modern life -- power lines, telephone exchanges, water pumps -- went dark. What replaced it was stranger than darkness. The air in these pockets went thick and sweet, smelling of copper and rain. Shadows moved on their own. Things grew in the walls that shouldn't have been able to grow.

People changed. Some individuals discovered they could feel the Aether -- could reach into it, shape it, make it do things that violated every physical law they'd been taught. The first wild casters were untrained and terrified, performing miracles and atrocities in equal measure. A woman who could mend broken bones by touching them. A boy who set a building on fire with his fear. A soldier whose rifle stopped working mid-deployment, who found he could throw men across a room with his mind instead.

The old order buckled. Trade routes crossed zones that machines couldn't navigate. Military supply lines collapsed. Communication networks went intermittent -- telegraphs worked here, failed there, worked again three blocks further on for no discernible reason. The governments that had held territory through infrastructure found that infrastructure was precisely what was failing.

People alive today who are over sixty remember this. Their children, now in their thirties and forties, grew up in the aftermath. Their grandchildren -- the current generation of young adults -- have never known anything else. The Aether, for them, is not a catastrophe. It's weather. It's geography. It's the air they breathe.

## What the Aether Feels Like

This is critical for fiction. The Aether is not an abstraction. It is a physical presence, and characters experience it through their bodies.

**In light Aetheric areas** (neighborhoods near the edge of a zone, streets where the gradient is shallow): The air is slightly thicker than normal. Breathing feels different -- not harder, just *more*, as if the air has more substance than it should. There's a faint taste, metallic and sweet, like licking a copper coin that's been sitting in sugar water. Shadows are slightly too dark. Colors are slightly too vivid. Sensitive people -- casters especially -- feel a low-level alertness, like the moment before a storm breaks, sustained indefinitely. Most people who live in light Aetheric areas stop noticing after a few weeks. Visitors notice immediately.

**In moderate Aetheric areas** (established Wild Zones, Aetheric districts in fractured cities): The physical sensations intensify. The copper taste is constant. The air has weight -- not oppressive, but present, like being in a room with someone very large standing just behind you. Sounds carry differently, echoing in ways that the architecture doesn't explain. Compasses drift. Watches run fast or slow. Plants grow conspicuously well. Animals are either drawn to these areas or avoid them entirely, with no middle ground. People who live here for years develop a sensitivity -- they can feel shifts in the zone's intensity the way a sailor feels weather changes. Some develop the ability to cast. Some don't. Nobody understands why.

**In deep Aetheric areas** (Deep Wild Zones, places where the Veil is barely there): Reality becomes negotiable. Geography shifts -- a path that led north yesterday leads east today. Time stutters. You look at your watch and an hour has passed, or three have, or none. The air isn't just thick -- it's luminous, carrying a faint blue-white glow that has no source. Sounds arrive before their causes. Crystals grow from surfaces -- thin, glassy, pulsing with light like a slow heartbeat. The creatures here are wrong in ways that the mind has trouble processing: too symmetrical, too still, too aware. Expeditions into Deep Wild return with stories that don't quite fit together, because the experience resists being narrated. Some expeditions don't return at all.

**For casters**, the Aether is more intimate. They feel it the way a swimmer feels current -- as a force that both supports and threatens. Reaching into it to cast is like plunging your hands into cold water and pulling something out by feel. The sensation is vivid, physical, and exhausting. Strong Aetheric areas make casting easier but more volatile -- more power available means more power to go wrong. Weak Aetheric areas feel like trying to breathe through cloth. Dead zones -- places dominated by the Engine -- feel like deafness, or a missing limb. Casters who spend time in dead zones describe it as one of the worst experiences of their lives.

## The Engine and Galvanic Technology

Within a decade of the Tear -- maybe less, maybe more, the records from that period are unreliable -- the counter-response appeared.

Nobody agrees on what happened. Three theories compete, and the competition is violent:

**The Immune Response:** The Veil thinned. The world's natural immune system activated. The Engine is the metaphysical equivalent of antibodies -- a force that emerged to fight the Aetheric infection and restore the world to its pre-Tear state. This theory is popular with industrialists, military officers, and anyone who wants the old order back. It makes the Engine righteous. It makes Galvanic technology the cure.

**The Rival Gods:** Both the Veil and the Engine are conscious -- or at least intentional. They are competing paradigms, vast forces with agendas that humans can barely perceive, fighting over the fundamental character of reality. People are caught in the middle, and neither force cares about them. This theory is popular with scholars, mystics, and anyone who's spent too long staring into a zone gradient. It makes both sides equally terrifying.

**The Exploitation Theory:** The Engine isn't natural. Someone cracked the code -- figured out how to weaponize the counter-resonance, built the first Galvanic device, and started selling them. The Aether is real. The Engine is manufactured. And the people who built it are profiting from a catastrophe they didn't cause but are very happy to exploit. This theory is popular with cynics, conspiracy theorists, and anyone who notices that Galvanic manufacturers are among the wealthiest people alive.

All three theories are believed passionately by different people. None has proof. The ambiguity is not a gap in the worldbuilding -- it is the worldbuilding. The biggest question in the world has no answer, and people organize their entire lives around their preferred guess.

**What the Engine feels like:** In Galvanic zones -- factory districts, areas around Galvanic installations, military emplacements -- the Engine's presence is physical. A low hum that you feel in your teeth and your joints more than hear with your ears. Compasses lock to a direction that isn't quite north, as if something magnetic but not geological is pulling at them. The air is dry and clean, almost sterile, with a faint ozone bite like the aftermath of a lightning strike. Machines run smoothly -- too smoothly, with a precision that feels almost eager. Electronics are crisp. Radios are clear. Everything mechanical works the way the textbooks promise it should.

And magic goes quiet. Casters feel it as a pressure -- not painful, but relentless, like swimming against a current that grows stronger the deeper into the zone you go. In moderate Galvanic zones, casting is possible but harder, like trying to speak in a loud room. In deep Galvanic zones, the Aether is simply absent. Reaching for it is like reaching for a hand in the dark and finding nothing there. Casters who enter deep Galvanic zones for the first time describe panic, vertigo, the terrifying sensation of a fundamental sense going dark.

## The Balance

The Aether and the Engine don't coexist. They compete. And the competition is felt in every street, every building, every room where people carry both guns and spell components.

Where the Aether is strong, technology weakens. A revolver that fires clean every time in neutral territory starts to stick, jam, misfire as the Aetheric saturation rises. Not because anything is wrong with the mechanism -- because the mechanism exists in a reality that is questioning whether mechanisms should work at all. Complex technology fails first: electronics, then precision instruments, then firearms, then simple machines. A sword never fails. A crossbow rarely fails. A revolver sometimes fails. An arc gun fails often. The older and simpler the technology, the deeper its roots in the collective human understanding of how things work, and the harder it is for the Aether to shake those roots.

Where the Engine is strong, magic weakens. The mirror image. A caster pushing power through a heavy Galvanic zone is like someone trying to light a match in a gale -- the fuel is there, the intent is there, but the environment is actively working against ignition. The stronger the Galvanic presence, the harder it is to reach the Aether. In deep Galvanic zones, it's not just hard. It's impossible. The Aether isn't weakened -- it's absent, pushed out by a force that refuses to share.

This is why swords still matter. This is why a world with revolvers and arc guns and magic still forges steel blades and trains people to use them. Because a blade works everywhere. It works in the deepest Wild Zone, where guns are paperweights. It works in the loudest Galvanic factory, where magic can't breathe. It works in the gradient between them, where nothing else is reliable. A blade is the only tool in the world that never asks permission from the metaphysics.

## The Gradient

The zones aren't rooms with walls. They're slopes.

Walk from a Galvanic factory district toward an Aetheric quarter and you'll feel the change in increments. The hum fades. The clean ozone smell gives way to something warmer, sweeter. Your watch runs a little faster, then a little slower. The compass drifts. The air gets heavier. By the time you're in the Aetheric zone, the world feels fundamentally different -- not wrong, just *other*. Turn around and walk back and you'll feel it reverse, degree by degree.

This gradient is information. If you know how to read it, the zone map tells you everything: who's operating here, how hard they're pushing, how recently they've been active. A sudden steepening of the gradient means someone is pouring energy into the zone -- a factory cycling up, a caster circle intensifying their rituals, a Galvanic weapon fired recently. The environment is a witness. It cannot be bribed, and it cannot lie.

The gradient is also the most dangerous place in the world. Where two strong zones meet -- an Aetheric pocket pressing against a Galvanic dead zone -- the slope is steep and the environment is unstable. Equipment flickers between working and failing from one step to the next. Spells catch or fizzle unpredictably. Weird phenomena appear at the collision point: lights with wrong-angled shadows, sounds that arrive before their causes, machines that run without power, performing functions nobody designed them for.

The Sootborn -- people who live in the gradient zones -- read this instability the way sailors read weather. They know when a shift is coming by the way the air tastes, the way their skin prickles, the way their teeth ache. Some of them claim to feel the balance itself, directly, without instruments or magic or Galvanic devices. Something in between, they say. Something that doesn't fit either paradigm.

Nobody knows what to make of that yet.

## The World Now

Fifty years after the Tear, the world has not recovered. It has reorganized. Badly, incompletely, and without anyone's permission.

**Governments limp along.** The nations that existed before the Tear still claim their old borders, but claiming and controlling are different things. Entire regions are Wild Zones that no conventional military can hold. Tax collection requires roads, and roads require zone stability, and zone stability requires resources that governments don't have. Some governments have adapted -- forming partnerships with Galvanic manufacturers, establishing zone containment agencies, funding Adventuring Societies to do the work that armies can't. Others have fractured into city-states, regional authorities, or quiet irrelevance.

**The world is fragmented.** Not at war -- splintered. Think neighborhoods, not nations. A factory district fortified by its own Galvanic dead zone. A neighborhood reborn as an Aetheric commune. A prophet's growing settlement in the unclaimed wild. A criminal syndicate running the border zones because nobody else will. These enclaves exist alongside and inside the remnants of the old order, competing for resources, influence, and the loyalty of the people caught between them.

**Prophets are rising.** The Tear shattered the old certainties. In the vacuum, charismatic figures are building movements. Some preach the Aether as revelation. Others preach the Engine as salvation. Still others preach themselves as the only ones who understand how it all fits together. These movements range from genuine spiritual communities to cynical power grabs, and telling the difference is not always easy. The governments that claim jurisdiction over the prophets' lands can't actually enforce it. Some have started negotiating. Some have started hiring Societies.

**Information moves. Truth doesn't.** Telegraph and radio work in neutral and Galvanic zones. Newspapers print. Couriers ride. People know things -- or think they do. But the further from home a report originates, the less anyone can verify it. Rumors travel faster than facts. Propaganda travels faster than rumors. The world is locally informed and globally speculative. Everyone has opinions about the floating islands, the techno-archipelago, the Deep Wild. Nobody has proof.

**The Reclaimed Wild is vast and strange.** Hundreds of miles of formerly settled land -- farms, villages, small towns -- swallowed by the Aether and transformed into something that shouldn't exist. Forests that grow too fast. Creatures that belong in legend. Communities that stayed and adapted, becoming something different from the people who left. The deeper you go, the less the old rules apply. The deepest places have structures -- not ruins, but buildings that no human hand constructed. Nobody who's gone deep enough to see them has come back with a coherent story.

**The Archipelago of Wonders is a rumor that won't die.** Somewhere offshore -- distance and direction change with the telling -- an island chain where technology has reached heights the mainland can only imagine. Or where magic has achieved something beyond casting. Or both, in impossible combination. Ships that venture too far in certain directions encounter zones of extreme saturation, alloys that no known forge produces wash ashore, and the few expeditions that have been sent have returned with contradictory reports or not returned at all.

**Adventure starts at home.** For all the wonders at the edges of the map, the world's instability is local. A zone shift threatening a neighborhood. A smuggler moving artifacts across a gradient. A prophet whose commune is growing too fast. A dead zone that appeared overnight where there shouldn't be one. A letter from a relative in a region that went dark. The weird starts a few blocks from home, and the people who walk into it professionally are Adventuring Societies.

## What People Believe

The world has no consensus. On anything.

The Tear divided more than geography. It divided understanding. People who lived through the same event experienced it as catastrophe or miracle depending on where they stood, what they lost, and what they found growing in the ashes. Fifty years of living with two competing metaphysical forces has not produced agreement. It has produced factions.

**The Restoration** leans toward the view that the Aether is disease and the Engine is cure. Push back the magic. Rebuild the old order. The world was better before, and it can be again. The people who lean this way tend to be the ones who lost the most -- industrialists whose factories went dark, military officers whose weapons stopped working, governments whose territory was consumed by Wild Zones. They're not wrong that the Tear caused immense suffering. They may be wrong that the suffering can be undone by going backward.

**The Awakening** leans toward the view that the Aether is revelation and the Engine is denial. The Veil thinned because it was meant to. Magic is a return to something real and primal that the industrial world had forgotten. The people who lean this way tend to be the ones who found the most -- casters who feel the Aether as a gift, communities in the Wild who've built lives around magical self-sufficiency, scholars who believe understanding is more important than restoration. They're not wrong that the Aether offers wonders. They may be wrong that the wonders come free.

**The Arrangement** is the pragmatist's position. Both forces are here to stay. Stop arguing about which is right and start figuring out how to live in the middle. The people who lean this way tend to be the ones who deal with both forces daily -- city governments trying to hold fractured territory, smugglers who move goods across zone borders, Adventuring Societies that carry swords, guns, and spells because ideology doesn't stop a monster. They're not wrong that pragmatism keeps people alive. They may be wrong that the balance can be managed forever.

Nobody carries a membership card. These aren't organizations. They're tendencies -- philosophical currents that shape how people react, vote, pray, and pick fights. Two people who share a tendency might disagree on every practical question. Two people on opposite sides might find themselves aligned on a specific job, a specific threat, a specific Tuesday afternoon when the zone shifts and someone needs to act.

The world does not reward certainty. The world rewards adaptability, courage, and a willingness to carry a backup weapon.

## The Fog

The further away something is, the less anyone knows about it. This is not a failure of communication -- it's the natural state of a fragmented world where information travels through contested territory and every faction has an interest in controlling the narrative.

What does the character know? What the people around them have told them, what they've read in broadsheets that may or may not be accurate, what they've seen with their own eyes, and what they've inferred from the gaps between these sources. That's it. That's all anyone knows.

The grand mysteries -- what the Engine really is, what lives beyond the Veil, what the floating islands actually contain -- are not secrets being kept from the characters. They're questions that genuinely have no answers, because nobody has gotten close enough to the truth and come back with a coherent report. The fog is real. The mysteries are honest.

Write from inside the fog. Let the characters wonder. Let them argue. Let them be wrong about things. Let the reader be uncertain right alongside them. The wonder lives in the not-knowing, and killing it with definitive answers would be the worst thing the fiction could do.
