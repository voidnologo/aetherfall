# The Local Scene: Ashwick

## The City Where the Stories Happen

Ashwick is not a great city. It never was. Before the Tear, it was a mid-sized industrial town -- textiles and light manufacturing, a rail junction that connected bigger places to each other, the kind of city that existed to be useful rather than important. It had factories that employed most of the working population, a commercial district that served the factories, a residential spread that housed the workers, and a thin layer of professional class -- doctors, lawyers, engineers -- who lived in the nicer neighborhoods and told themselves they were better than the grime suggested.

The Tear changed Ashwick the way it changed everywhere: violently, unevenly, and without asking permission.

Now, fifty years later, Ashwick is a fractured city of roughly forty thousand people, give or take -- nobody's done a reliable census in years, because nobody's authority extends far enough to count. It sits on a zone boundary that probably shouldn't exist: a strong Aetheric pocket has established itself in what was once the old town, pressing up against the Galvanic dead zone that the remaining factories maintain, with a gradient border between them that shifts like a tide. The city is physically intact -- the buildings still stand, the streets still connect -- but it is three different cities wearing one name.

It is, in other words, exactly the kind of place where an Adventuring Society can find work.

---

## The Shape of the City

Ashwick lies in a river valley -- the River Ast, slow and brown, curving through the city's western edge. The terrain is gentle: low hills to the east, flat industrial plain to the west, the river and its marshy floodplain providing a natural boundary on one side. Before the Tear, the city grew outward from its old center in concentric rings of decreasing age: medieval old town at the heart, Georgian and Victorian commercial districts around it, industrial expansion to the west, residential neighborhoods filling the spaces between.

The Tear redrew the map. Now the city is organized by zone, not by age:

**The Factory Quarter (West) -- Galvanic**
Where the old industrial district survived and intensified. The Ashworth Foundry Collective dominates here, running Galvanic component manufacturing from a complex of soot-stained brick buildings that hum day and night. The zone runs moderate to strong Galvanic -- the hum is in your teeth before you're within three blocks. Compasses lock south. Radios are crystal clear. Machines run with eager precision. And magic goes quiet. Casters who walk into the Factory Quarter feel the Aether retreat like water draining from a basin, replaced by something dry and mechanical and relentless.

The streets here are well-maintained -- the Collective pays for that, because functional infrastructure supports functional factories. The workers live in row houses within walking distance of the plants. The air smells of ozone and hot metal. At night, the arc lights along the main avenues are the brightest in the city -- powered by Galvanic generators that never dim. It's impressive. It's efficient. It's also expanding, and the neighborhoods at its eastern edge are starting to feel the pressure: headaches, insomnia, a persistent hum in the walls that won't stop even when the factories cycle down.

**The Veilwright Quarter (East) -- Aetheric**
What was once the old town -- narrow streets, medieval foundations, buildings that predate the industrial era by centuries. When the Tear hit, the Veil thinned here more than anywhere else in Ashwick. The old town went Aetheric and never came back.

The Quarter is beautiful in a way that makes some people uneasy. The streets are cobblestone -- asphalt crumbles in sustained Aetheric zones. The lighting is gas flame and candle, supplemented by the faint luminescence that the Aether itself provides in the thicker patches. Plants grow aggressively: ivy covering whole facades, window boxes rioting with flowers that bloom out of season, trees pushing through pavement cracks with roots that seem to move when nobody's watching. The air smells like copper and rain, even on clear days.

The people here rebuilt around magic. Healers run clinics. Wardsmiths sell protection. Diviners offer their services to anyone who can pay. The economy is barter and service exchange internally, with magical labor sold externally to the rest of the city. The Quarter is self-governing -- the Greycoat Authority's jurisdiction technically extends here, but their equipment fails within blocks of the border, so "jurisdiction" is academic.

The residents are protective of their home to the point of belligerence. They've spent fifty years being told their neighborhood is a disaster area, a containment problem, a slum that should be cleared for the good of the city. They've responded by building a community that works, on its own terms, by its own rules. Outsiders are welcome to visit and spend money. Outsiders are not welcome to stay and tell them how things should be done.

**Harrier Street (The Gradient)**
The border. A single long avenue that runs north-south along the zone boundary, with the Factory Quarter's Galvanic hum pressing from the west and the Veilwright Quarter's Aetheric warmth pressing from the east. Stand in the middle of Harrier Street at sundown and you can feel both forces pulling at you -- the dry, bone-deep hum on your left, the thick, copper-sweet heaviness on your right.

Harrier Street is where the world's central tension is visible at human scale. The shopkeepers here sell ammunition and spell components in the same display cases. The street vendors adjust their prices based on which way the gradient is leaning that day -- Galvanic goods cheaper when the hum is strong, Aetheric goods cheaper when the air tastes like rain. Children who grow up on Harrier Street learn to read the gradient before they learn to read books.

It's also the most dangerous stretch of urban real estate in Ashwick. The gradient shifts with activity -- a spell cast on one side pushes the balance, a factory cycling up on the other pushes it back. Equipment reliability changes from block to block. The Sootborn communities that cluster along Harrier Street have adapted, but visitors trip over the instability constantly: a revolver that worked fine three doors down suddenly sticking, a ward that was strong an hour ago flickering out because the Ashworth night shift just started.

The Lamplighter Syndicate does its best business on Harrier Street. Moving goods across the gradient is what they do, and the gradient is right here, pulsing like a living thing.

**Ashwick Central (Neutral Zone)**
The commercial heart of the city -- what remains of the old commercial district, carefully maintained in zone neutrality by the Greycoat Authority because neutral ground is where trade happens. The train station still functions here. The telegraph office. The post office. The banks. The kind of infrastructure that requires both technology and the absence of interference to operate.

Central is where the factions meet. Not formally -- there are no summit halls or treaty tables. They meet in taverns, in coffee houses, in the offices of lawyers who handle disputes between parties that can't acknowledge each other publicly. The economy of Ashwick flows through Central because Central is the only place where Galvanic currency, Aetheric barter, and old-fashioned cash all have value simultaneously.

The Greycoat Authority's headquarters is here -- a tired-looking building that was once the city hall and now serves as the seat of whatever government Ashwick still has. The Authority spends most of its diminishing resources keeping Central neutral: monitoring zone balance, persuading (or paying) both sides to keep their accumulation below detection thresholds, and running the constabulary that patrols the few blocks where their authority is recognized.

**The Residential Sprawl (Mixed)**
Everything else. The neighborhoods between the defined zones, where ordinary people live ordinary lives in a world that changed under their feet. Some blocks lean slightly Galvanic -- near enough to the factories for the hum to be background noise. Some lean slightly Aetheric -- close enough to the Quarter for the air to taste sweet on damp mornings. Most are genuinely neutral, too far from either source to be affected.

These neighborhoods are where the city's forty thousand people actually live. They work in the factories or the Quarter or Central. They send their children to schools that teach reading, arithmetic, and "zone awareness" as a standard subject. They argue about politics at kitchen tables and in corner pubs. They are, overwhelmingly, ordinary people navigating a world that asks extraordinary adaptation.

**Beyond the City**
Ashwick has no walls, but it has edges. Beyond the residential sprawl, the city gives way to what it always gave way to: farmland, river floodplain, and the low hills that roll east toward the horizon.

The surrounding country is ordinary. Small towns dot the valley -- market villages, coaching stops, farming communities that send produce to Ashwick by cart and receive manufactured goods in return. The River Ast carries barge traffic south toward the coast and the larger port cities. The rail line runs west, connecting Ashwick to other industrial centers inland. Roads go in every direction, though not all are equally reliable.

The complication is northeast. A broad stretch of Wild land -- not deep, but wide and persistent -- cuts across what was once the main highway to the eastern cities. Travel that way is still possible by longer routes or with a guide who knows the safe passages, but it's slower, more expensive, and it gives Ashwick a slightly inward character. News from the east arrives late and garbled. Eastern goods carry a markup. The city's politics have developed with a degree of independence from whatever the capitals are arguing about this season.

But Ashwick is not cut off. Merchants arrive daily. Families visit relatives in the outlying towns and come back with gossip and preserves. Workers travel to other cities for seasonal labor and return with stories and opinions. The train station in Central -- the busiest building in the city -- is proof that the world beyond Ashwick is real, connected, and considerably larger than any single city's concerns. Ashwick is a small city in a big world, not a fortress in a wasteland.

## History

### Before the Tear

Ashwick was founded as a market town at the river crossing -- the Ast was fordable here before the bridge was built, and trade naturally pooled. It grew slowly for centuries, the way market towns do: a church, a guild hall, a cluster of medieval streets that still form the bones of the Veilwright Quarter. The old town's buildings are stone and timber, built to last, and they've outlasted everything that came after.

Industrialization hit Ashwick in the mid-to-late pre-Tear era -- roughly a century before the Tear, in the world's terms. Textile mills first, then light manufacturing, then heavier industry as the rail junction brought raw materials and carried finished goods away. The factory district grew west of the old town, eating farmland and turning it into brick and iron. The worker housing spread north and south. Ashwick became what it was designed to be: a cog in the industrial machine, not glamorous but functional.

### The Tear in Ashwick

The Tear hit Ashwick in two waves.

The first wave was the Aetheric saturation of the old town. Within weeks, the medieval district went wild -- machines failed, the air changed, the old stone buildings that had stood for centuries suddenly felt *alive* in ways that the newer structures around them didn't. People panicked. Some fled to the factory district, where the machines still worked. Some stayed, whether from stubbornness, poverty, or a dawning sense that the changed air was offering something.

The second wave came months later, when the Engine emerged. The factories -- which had been struggling, half-powered, trying to run on coal and steam while their more sophisticated equipment failed -- suddenly roared back to life. Galvanic resonance flooded the industrial quarter. The machines didn't just recover; they *improved*, running with a precision and efficiency that the pre-Tear technology had never achieved. The factory owners, who had been watching their livelihoods dissolve, found themselves holding the most valuable thing in the new world: working industry in a dead zone that magic couldn't touch.

The two waves created the two poles. The old town became the Veilwright Quarter. The factory district became the Galvanic industrial zone. And between them, on a street that happened to sit on the boundary, the gradient established itself -- Harrier Street, named for the family that had run the general store on the corner for three generations and refused to leave regardless of what the metaphysics were doing.

### The Fifty Years Since

Ashwick has spent five decades not quite falling apart and not quite coming together. The Greycoat Authority -- originally the city's civil servants and constabulary, who kept working after the elected officials fled or became irrelevant -- holds the nominal center. The Ashworth Collective controls the industrial quarter. The Veilwright Quarter governs itself. The Lamplighter Syndicate controls the border traffic. Smaller factions, organizations, and ambitious individuals fill the gaps.

There have been no wars. There have been confrontations: a zone expansion that pushed too far, a smuggling operation that got too visible, a political crisis when the last elected mayor died and nobody could agree on a replacement. These crises have been resolved through negotiation, threat, compromise, and the occasional application of violence that everyone subsequently agreed to pretend hadn't happened.

Ashwick is stable the way a house of cards is stable. All the pieces are in balance. None of them are secure.

## The Major Players

### The Greycoat Authority

What passes for a city government. The Greycoats are career bureaucrats and constables who kept showing up to work after the world broke, because someone had to keep the water running and the streets lit. They wear grey coats -- literally, the old civil service uniform that became their identity -- and they claim jurisdiction over all of Ashwick. In practice, their authority extends to Central and a few blocks in each direction.

**Commissioner Hale Dupont** runs the Authority. He's sixty, silver-haired, and exhausted in a way that goes deeper than tired. He was a young constable when the Tear hit and he's been holding things together ever since. He believes in civic duty the way some people believe in gods -- as a foundational truth that doesn't require evidence. He is also aware that the deals keeping the Authority solvent are corroding the institution from within. He takes money from the Ashworth Collective for favorable zoning decisions. He looks the other way on Lamplighter operations in exchange for a cut. He hates himself for it. He sees no alternative.

**Inspector Renna Laine** is the Authority's best investigator and the handler most likely to deal with Adventuring Societies. She's mid-forties, sharp-eyed, ink-stained, and brutally pragmatic. She cares about results, not ideology. She hired the Society for their first job and she'll hire them again, because they delivered and because she doesn't have the bodies to do the work herself. She trusts them exactly as far as their track record extends, which is to say: provisionally.

### The Ashworth Foundry Collective

The industrial power. The Collective consolidated three factory districts after the Tear and now controls Galvanic component manufacturing -- not finished weapons, but the capacitors, resonance coils, and stabilizers that everyone else needs. This gives them leverage that borders on monopoly.

**Aldric Voss** is the Chief Operations Director -- former military logistics, converted to industry after the Tear broke the army he'd served. He believes in the Restoration cause genuinely: the world was better before, the Engine is the path back, and industry is the engine (lowercase) of civilization. He's also beginning to suspect that the Collective's board cares more about market dominance than ideology. He drinks too much. He asks uncomfortable questions at board meetings. He is, in his own quiet way, becoming a problem for the people who sign his checks.

The Collective is deliberately expanding its Galvanic dead zone -- pushing the boundary eastward, into the residential neighborhoods between the factories and the Veilwright Quarter. The official story is industrial necessity. The truth is strategic: every block that goes Galvanic is a block where magic can't work, where the Quarter's influence recedes, and where the Collective's control is absolute. The people living in the expansion zone are experiencing headaches, insomnia, and a persistent hum that doesn't stop. The Collective calls these "adjustment symptoms." The people experiencing them have less polite names.

### The Veilwright Quarter

The Aetheric community. Self-governing, self-sufficient, and self-righteous about both. The Quarter has no formal leader -- its governance is a tangle of neighborhood councils, guild-like associations of practitioners (healers, wardsmiths, diviners), and the informal authority of the people who've been here longest and cast best.

**Maren Elia** is the closest thing to a leader -- a healer who came to the Quarter as a refugee during the Tear, discovered she could cast, and built the clinic that became the community's anchor. She's in her sixties now, grey-haired and stubborn, with a reputation for telling outsiders exactly what she thinks of their opinions about her home. She's tired. She's been defending the Quarter's right to exist for fifty years and she's sick of explaining to people that a neighborhood where magic works is not a disaster area.

What the Quarter doesn't advertise: a small circle of senior casters has been performing sustained rituals to maintain the zone's strength, preventing the natural decay that would bring the Aetheric saturation back toward neutral. They believe -- probably correctly -- that a strong zone is the only thing protecting the Quarter from being bulldozed by Restorationist interests. But the rituals are pushing the zone deeper than it's ever been, and some of the effects are getting harder to control. The crystals growing from walls are thicker this year. The shadows move more than they used to. Something beyond the Veil has started paying attention.

### The Lamplighter Syndicate

Organized crime, Ashwick chapter. The Syndicate runs smuggling across the zone borders -- Galvanic components into the Quarter (where they're unavailable), magical goods into the Factory Quarter (where they're contraband), and anything profitable through the gradient to whoever's buying. They also run protection, gambling, and information brokerage, but the smuggling is the backbone.

**The Lamplighter** is the local boss, and nobody knows who they are. Not their face, not their name, not whether they're one person or a rotating identity. The Syndicate's lieutenants take orders through intermediaries. The organization runs on compartmentalization and plausible deniability.

**Corrigan Fels** is the fixer most visible to people like the Society. Former Adventuring Society member himself -- he "retired" into the Syndicate after his Society's patron betrayed them, a story he tells with charm and without details. He's the person you talk to when you need something moved, someone found, or a problem solved quietly. He has a code about violence: he prefers bribery, blackmail, or misdirection. He'll kill if cornered, but he considers it a professional failure.

The Syndicate doesn't want the city's status quo to change. A fragmented, balkanized Ashwick with porous zone borders is the perfect operating environment. Any faction that threatens to stabilize things -- whether by expanding Galvanic dead zones or consolidating Aetheric sovereignty -- threatens the Syndicate's business model. This makes them natural opponents of both the Ashworth Collective and the Veilwright Quarter, and natural allies of anyone who wants to keep things messy.

### The Gradient Scholars

The academics. An independent research institute that set up shop on the zone border because that's where the most interesting phenomena occur. They study the Aether, the Engine, the gradient between them, and anything else that doesn't fit the existing theories. Their campus is a converted textile mill on the northeast edge of the gradient, with laboratories that straddle the zone boundary -- one wing in light Galvanic territory, one wing in light Aetheric, the connecting corridor a perpetual science experiment.

**Dr. Isavel Crane** runs field research -- fifties, sharp, impatient with politics, fascinated by everything else. She lost her university in the Tear and rebuilt from scratch. She authorized a Deep Wild expedition six months ago. The team sent one final report describing structures in the deep forest that appeared to be responding to their presence -- not hostile, but communicative. Then they went silent. Crane has classified the report and hasn't slept well since.

The Scholars fund Adventuring Societies for artifact recovery, zone cartography, and specimen collection. They're one of the few institutions producing formally educated casters. They publish a journal that circulates among anyone literate enough to read it. Their bias is toward understanding rather than exploitation, which makes them Awakening-leaning in the eyes of the Restoration and dangerously naive in the eyes of the Arrangement.

### The Ironveil Circle

Religion. The local diocese of a faith that predates the Tear -- a church that ran hospitals, schools, and orphanages when the world was orderly and continues to run them now that it isn't. Their cathedral sits on the north side of Central, and their network of sanctuaries extends into every district, maintained in careful neutrality through a combination of Galvanic dampeners and Aetheric warding.

**Prelate Ansel Rourke** leads the diocese. He's seventy-odd, silver-haired, and patient to the point of infuriating. He has spent two decades holding the Ironveil Circle together through compromise, delay, and strategic ambiguity. He knows the split is coming and he's trying to make it happen after he's dead.

The split: the Ironveil Circle is theologically divided. One faction -- the Engine Orthodox -- believes the Aether is spiritual corruption, demonic in nature, to be suppressed by the faithful. The other -- the Veil Contemplatives -- believes the Tear was divine revelation, a thinning of the barrier between the mortal and the sacred. Both factions have members in positions of power. Both fund their own Adventuring Societies with contradictory mandates. The institutional facade of unity is growing thinner by the year.

### The Sootborn

Not an organization. A community -- or a network of communities, clustered along the gradient zones where nobody else wants to live. The Sootborn are the people of the border: the ones who stayed when the zone shifted, or moved in because the margins were the only place they could afford, or were born here and never knew anything else.

**Rill** is a guide -- no surname offered, early thirties, wiry and fast-talking. She can read the zone balance of any room she walks into within seconds. She hires out to Adventuring Societies, researchers, and anyone else who needs to cross the gradient without dying. She has the sensitivity -- the ability to feel the balance shifting directly, without instruments -- and she will deny it to anyone who asks.

The Sootborn's value is practical: they know the gradient better than anyone. Their vulnerability is political: they have no patron, no faction backing, no institutional support. When the zones shift, they're the first displaced and the last helped.

## Landmarks

### The Wet Ember

A tavern on the neutral side of Ashwick -- the kind of place where the Galvanic hum and the Aetheric prickle cancel each other out. Gas lamps, not electric, not magical. Warm wood and pipe smoke. The fire is coal, and it's always burning.

The Ember is neutral ground by tradition and reputation. Factory workers and Quarter residents drink in the same room, which is remarkable and deliberate. The owner -- **Hesper Brack**, a broad-shouldered woman in her fifties who lost her arm in a factory accident and learned to pour with the other -- enforces neutrality with the force of personality that comes from throwing a grown man through a window once and never having to do it again.

The Ember is where Greycoat inspectors brief Adventuring Societies. Where Syndicate fixers make offers. Where rumors start, deals close, and alliances form over warm beer and cold calculation. It is, for the Society, something between an office and a confessional.

### The Decommissioned Ashworth Foundry

A hulking brick-and-iron structure on the edge of the Galvanic quarter. Smokestacks cold, windows dark, loading bay doors chained shut -- but the chains are new. Officially decommissioned two years ago. Unofficially, the tunnel network beneath it connects to the old drainage system that runs under the zone border, making it the Lamplighter Syndicate's preferred route for moving large shipments.

The foundry sits in a moderate Galvanic zone -- the hum is present, casting is harder, guns are reliable. But the tunnels beneath it tell a different story. The Aether has been bleeding through the old drainage tunnels for months or years, warping the environment: crystalline growths on the walls, air that tastes of copper and sweetness, a blue-white luminescence with no source. The zone contrast between the foundry above and the tunnels below is sharp and disorienting.

The Society's first job happened here. They'll be back. The tunnels go deeper than anyone has mapped, and the anomalous readings that drew Fen Harker's attention haven't been explained.

### The Gradient Scholars Campus

A converted textile mill on the northeast edge of the gradient. Three buildings connected by enclosed walkways -- one wing in light Galvanic territory, one in light Aetheric, the corridor between them a controlled gradient that the researchers use as a permanent laboratory. Instruments line the walls of the connecting corridor, measuring accumulation, drift, interference patterns, and phenomena that don't have names yet.

The campus is open to visitors by appointment. It has a library -- one of the best in Ashwick, if you're interested in topics like "pre-Tear occult texts" or "theoretical zonography." It also has a security problem: the Scholars' collection of Aetheric artifacts and recovered specimens makes it a target for theft, and their research produces knowledge that multiple factions would prefer to control.

### The Ironveil Cathedral

St. Ansel's -- named for a pre-Tear saint, not the current Prelate, though the coincidence makes Rourke uncomfortable. A stone edifice on the north side of Central, built in the medieval period and expanded repeatedly since. The cathedral is maintained in careful zone neutrality, its interior protected by both Galvanic dampeners embedded in the modern wing and Aetheric wards carved into the ancient stonework.

The hospital annex runs off the north transept. The orphanage occupies the old chapter house. The soup kitchen operates from the undercroft. These services are faction-neutral by policy and by necessity -- the Ironveil Circle can't afford to turn anyone away, and the city can't afford to lose the Circle's infrastructure.

### The Sootborn Market

Not a fixed location. The Sootborn run an informal market that moves along the gradient, setting up wherever the zone balance is most stable on a given day. Stalls sell gradient-specific goods: instruments calibrated for zone navigation, clothing treated to resist Aetheric particulate, foods preserved using techniques that only work in the border zones. Information is traded as freely as goods -- who's moving through the gradient, what the zones did last night, which blocks are safe today and which aren't.

The market is the best place in Ashwick to hire a zone guide, buy untraceable goods, or hear rumors that haven't reached the broadsheets yet.

## Street-Level Life

What does it feel like to be an ordinary person in Ashwick?

You work. In the Factory Quarter, you work shifts at one of the Collective's plants -- good pay, steady hours, the persistent hum in your bones that you stop noticing after the first year (or don't). In the Quarter, you practice a trade that relies on magic, or you serve the people who do -- cooking, cleaning, building, the mundane work that keeps a community running regardless of metaphysics. In Central, you keep the commercial machinery going: banks, shops, offices, the train station where goods arrive from the coast and the inland cities and crates of Galvanic components leave on the evening freight.

You go home. Your flat is in one of the residential neighborhoods, probably leaning slightly one way or the other. You've learned to ignore the gradient effects -- the watch that runs fast near the bedroom window, the radio that crackles when the Quarter's evening rituals start. Your children learn zone awareness in school. Your elderly parents, if they're still alive, remember a world where none of this existed and shake their heads at what passes for normal.

You worry. About the expansion -- will the Collective push the dead zone far enough to reach your block? About the Quarter -- is the Aetheric saturation getting stronger, and what does that mean for the value of your property? About the Syndicate -- is it safer to pay their protection fee or refuse it? About your children -- your daughter felt the Aether last week, and you don't know whether to be proud or terrified.

You go to the Wet Ember, or a place like it. You drink. You argue about politics with people who disagree with you. You hear rumors about the latest zone shift, the latest faction move, the latest Adventuring Society job gone wrong or right. You go home and bolt the door and try not to think about the fact that the bolt is held in place by the same physical laws that the Aether is slowly questioning.

Ashwick is not a glamorous place. It is a real place, full of real people, navigating a reality that they didn't choose and can't escape. The Society lives here too. They drink in the same taverns, walk the same streets, feel the same gradient. The difference is that when the ordinary people lock their doors and hope the weirdness stays outside, the Society is the weirdness that someone hired to walk in.

## Current Tensions

These are the fault lines that the stories will explore. They're not plot hooks -- they're the baseline conditions of life in Ashwick.

**The Ashworth Expansion.** The Collective is pushing its dead zone eastward. Border neighborhoods are feeling the effects. The Greycoats can't stop it because the Collective pays their bills. The Quarter can't stop it because their authority ends at their zone boundary. The people caught in the middle are organizing, but organizing against the city's largest employer is not a simple proposition.

**The Veilwright Intensification.** The ritual circle is pushing the Quarter's zone deeper. The effects are becoming visible: thicker crystals, darker shadows, air that hums with a frequency that makes newcomers dizzy. The Quarter's leadership doesn't know about the rituals -- or claims not to. The Greycoats suspect something but can't investigate in a zone where their equipment fails. If the rituals are discovered, the political fallout could destabilize the Quarter and give the Restoration interests the excuse they need.

**The Lamplighter Expansion.** The Syndicate's smuggling operation is growing. The Ashwick Job -- the Society's first mission -- disrupted one route, but the Syndicate has others. More concerning: the Lamplighter's artifact stockpiling suggests preparation for something larger than routine smuggling. What the Lamplighter is planning, nobody knows. That nobody knows is, itself, the problem.

**The Greycoat Decline.** The Authority is running out of money, credibility, and options. Commissioner Dupont's deals with the Collective and the Syndicate are becoming harder to hide. If the Authority loses its claim to neutrality, Central loses its status as neutral ground, and the economic machinery of Ashwick seizes up. Dupont knows this. He doesn't know how to stop it.

**The Ironveil Split.** The theological divide inside the Circle is widening. Engine Orthodox and Veil Contemplatives are funding separate Adventuring Societies with contradictory mandates. Prelate Rourke's hold on the institution is weakening. If the Circle splits publicly, the hospital, orphanage, and soup kitchen -- the services that every faction depends on -- will be caught in the crossfire.

**The Anomalous Readings.** Whatever Fen Harker found beneath the old foundry hasn't been explained. The Aetheric bleeding through the tunnels is stronger than it should be, coming from a source that nobody has located. The Gradient Scholars are intensely interested. The Collective is intensely nervous. And the tunnels go deeper than anyone has mapped.
