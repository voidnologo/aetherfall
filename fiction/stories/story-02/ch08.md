# Chapter 8

The nosebleed stopped after ten minutes. Aldric knew because he timed it by Kael's watch, which was the only measure he trusted, even though he didn't trust it much.

He sat on the warm ground of the clearing with his back against a tree at the edge, his notebook open on his knees, and wrote. The pencil was unsteady in his fingers — the Anchor had cost him more than he'd estimated, and the mending work he'd done on Kael's cut inside the structure had taken what was left. His reserves were low. Not empty, but low enough that another significant casting would put him somewhere he didn't want to be.

He wrote anyway. The details had to be captured now, while they were fresh, while the adrenaline was still keeping his observations sharp. Later, his memory would soften the edges, round off the strangeness, domesticate what he'd seen into something his training could accommodate. He didn't want that. He wanted the raw data, uncomfortable as it was.

*Shell: pre-Tear processing mill, identifiable by silhouette and footprint. Grain elevator, roller mill, loading dock. Structurally intact in outline — the Wilds have not collapsed it. Confirmed pre-Tear origin per local testimony (T. at settlement well). Estimated abandoned when the Wilds manifested, ~50 years ago. Exterior material appears remade — concrete and timber replaced in-place by a pale seamless material, original form preserved. A cicatrix rather than a ruin. The mill is the scar the mill became.*

*Interior: bears no relation to the shell. No machinery foundations, no catwalks, no floor-fixture scars, no chute trenches. None of the signatures of a working mill. The interior has been hollowed and remade by something that did not consult the original function. Corridors follow non-standard geometry. Angles internally consistent but do not correspond to Euclidean expectations. The space shifts — slow reconfiguration of layout, as if the interior's floor plan is mutable. Not random: the shifts follow patterns I cannot yet identify.*

*Material (interior): seamless, pale, warm to touch, accepts scoring (knife marks) but resists, luminous veins running through it. Not stone, not metal, not ceramic. Not visibly related to the mill's original building materials. The Aether has not warped the mill — it has replaced it from the inside out.*

*The chamber: circular, high-ceilinged, convergence point for luminous veins. Spatial defense — approach to the central pedestal results in return to point of origin. Not a barrier. A loop. The space between the threshold and the pedestal is folded. No hostile intent detectable.*

He paused. Looked at the structure across the clearing. Looked back at his notebook.

*The container.*

He'd seen it for perhaps ninety seconds. At a distance of thirty feet, through air thick with Aetheric luminescence, under conditions of extreme stress. Ninety seconds was not enough. But Aldric had spent twelve years training his observation, and twelve years of training didn't shut off because you were frightened.

*Container: approximately 60cm x 30cm x 15cm. Dark material — absorbs light rather than reflecting. Surface appears smooth and featureless at distance. Seated on a pedestal of the same material as the structure itself.*

*Critical observation: the container's visual qualities are inconsistent with its environment. The remade interior is luminous, organic in its geometry, saturated with Aetheric resonance. The container is dark, precise, angular. Its proportions suggest manufacture — the kind of exactness that comes from machining, not growth or casting. If I did not know where I was, I would identify it as a Galvanic artifact. The precision, the surface finish, the proportions — these are the qualities of engineered objects.*

*But there is no Engine signature. No hum. No resonance of the kind associated with Galvanic technology. And yet the container sits, unmistakably, at the center of an interior remade by Aetheric force — an interior in which Aetheric and Galvanic qualities are interwoven in the wall material itself.*

*The mill is pre-Tear. The shell confirms that. Pell's grandfather ran it.*

*The interior is not pre-Tear. The interior is whatever has been building itself inside the shell in the decades since the Wilds came in. The container, then, is not necessarily pre-Tear either. It may have been placed, or grown, or assembled in the chamber by whatever agency is remaking the building from within. The timeline is intact. What I cannot yet answer is: what is the agency. What is it building. What is it building toward. And why, after fifty years of work, has it sealed this object behind a lock that nothing at our disposal can reach.*

He stared at the page. Then he closed the notebook and put the pencil in his coat pocket.

He would need to think about this. Not now — now his head ached and his nose had just stopped bleeding and there was still the matter of getting back through the Wilds to the settlement. But eventually, in a quiet room with his references and his cross-indexed notes, he would need to sit with the implications of what he'd seen and follow them wherever they led.

He was not looking forward to that.

"Aldric." Mira was crouching beside him. "How are you?"

"Depleted. Not incapacitated." He assessed himself with the clinical honesty that his training demanded. "I can walk. I can cast minor workings — Detect, possibly Reveal, at reduced range and with elevated cost. Mend is available but I'd rather not unless someone is actively dying. Shield is possible once, maybe, and then I'm done for the day."

"Honest assessment. Thank you." Mira's hand rested briefly on his shoulder — a rare physical gesture from her, and one he recognized as genuine rather than performative. "Can you do a Reveal? Wide sweep. I want to know if we're alone out here."

Aldric considered the cost. Reveal in this saturation would be expensive — not because the Aether would resist it, but because it wouldn't. The spell would leap off his fingers like a dog that had been waiting too long for its walk, and the work would be in dragging it back, narrowing its mouth, refusing to let it swallow every detail of the forest for a mile around and drown him in returns. But Mira was asking because she was thinking about the same thing Kael had been watching for since they left Ashwick — the tire tracks on the road, the rival Society that was somewhere behind them, the fact that they were in the Wilds with confirmation of something extraordinary and no guarantee that the people pursuing the same objective would let that confirmation reach the Covenant.

He cast Reveal.

The spell went out like a pulse — invisible to the others, but to Aldric it was like throwing a stone into still water and watching the ripples not just travel but accelerate. The Aether didn't resist. The Aether *leapt*. The spell went wider than he'd fed it, faster, reaching further in the first second than he had tried to reach at all, and the returns came back all at once — a flood of data that crested against the inside of his skull and tried to deposit itself there in a single unstructured heap. He clamped down. He narrowed the spell's mouth mid-cast, the way a glassblower narrows a neck, forcing the range back into usefulness and letting the excess bleed off into the saturated air. Each yard of width he shed cost him a throb behind his eyes like a thunderstorm arriving.

The clearing was clear. The forest immediately around them was clear. The structure behind them was a blind spot — the Aetheric density inside it was so extreme that the Reveal slid off it like water off glass.

But to the west — toward the settlement, toward the path they'd taken in — the Reveal found something. Human presences. Four, maybe five — the returns were muddled by the Aetheric interference, the shapes blurring in and out of definition. But they were there. Armed — the spell couldn't literally see weapons, but the presences were moving with the tight, purposeful formation of people who expected to use them. They were between the clearing and the settlement, and they were moving east.

Toward them.

The headache crested. Aldric let the spell collapse and put his hand to his temple, pressing against the pain. His vision swam. He breathed through it, the way he'd been taught — three breaths, slow, let the cost settle, don't fight it.

"We're not alone," he said. His voice came out thinner than he intended. "Four or five individuals, armed, moving east through the Wilds between us and the settlement. Formation movement. Not casual."

Kael's knife was already in his hand. "Distance?"

"Difficult to estimate. The spatial distortion — maybe a mile. Maybe less. The Reveal wanted to keep going. I had to pull it back. The returns I got are clear out to about that distance and then they're lost in what I was bleeding off."

"Ironwork," Mira said. Not a question.

"The Collective dispatched them to find the same site. They've been ahead of us or behind us since the road out of Ashwick. Tire tracks past the waypoint, then something that pushed further than the village man thought wise. They found the settlement, found our trail, followed us in." Aldric wiped his nose — no blood this time, but the reflex was there. "And they know that we found it. If we get back to Ashwick, the Covenant knows. The site is confirmed. The Collective loses any advantage of secrecy."

The implication sat in the clearing like a fifth person. Out here, in the Wilds, beyond any authority's jurisdiction, beyond any witness's reach, a rival Society could do whatever it determined was necessary to prevent that confirmation from reaching the city. And the Collective's investment in secrecy was substantial enough to justify substantial measures.

"We can't go back the way we came," Kael said. He was scanning the tree line, reading the terrain with the eyes of a man who'd spent eighteen years learning how to assess threats. "They'll be on our path. We need an alternate route."

"There may not be one," Aldric said. "Sera navigated by Aetheric current. The current flows in specific directions. Finding a different path through the distortion without her original route —"

"I can do it." Sera was on her feet, her eyes bright, her hands steady. Of all of them, she was the least depleted — the Wilds' saturation was feeding her even as it drained the rest. "The current has multiple channels. I followed the strongest one in. I can find a different one going out. It'll be harder. Longer, maybe. But I can do it."

Mira looked at Kael. Kael looked at the tree line.

"Then we go now," he said. "Different route. Fast as the forest lets us."

They gathered their gear. Aldric shouldered his satchel, feeling the weight of the notebook against his ribs — the observations, the sketches, the questions he didn't yet have the tools to answer. Evidence that someone wanted to ensure never reached the city.

He was not a fighter. He was a scholar who'd ended up in the field because his funding was cut and a woman he respected had offered him a job that used his skills. He carried a walking stick, not a weapon, and the most dangerous thing about him was information.

Right now, that was dangerous enough.

"Stay close to me," Sera said. She was looking into the forest, her head tilted, reading the invisible currents. "The route I'm finding goes north first, then curves back west. It's longer. But it avoids the direct line."

"Do it," Mira said.

They left the clearing, and the structure watched them go with its luminous, patient indifference, and somewhere in the forest between them and safety, armed people were walking toward the place they'd just left, and the Wilds stretched in every direction like a puzzle that kept rearranging its pieces.

Aldric walked, and wrote nothing, and carried everything.
