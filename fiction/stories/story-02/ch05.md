# Chapter 5

They left the settlement at dawn and were lost within an hour.

Not lost the way you got lost in a city — taking a wrong turn, missing a street sign, ending up three blocks from where you intended. Lost the way you got lost in a dream, where the error wasn't in your navigation but in the assumption that navigation was possible.

Mira tracked it as carefully as she could. They entered the forest heading east, following a gap in the undergrowth that might have been a game trail or might have been nothing. Sera walked point — a reversal of the usual order that Kael accepted without comment, because in here, Sera's sense was the only compass that worked. The actual compass on her neck had stopped moving entirely, its needle pointing at a fixed spot that wasn't any direction Mira recognized.

The trees were enormous. That was the first thing. Not just tall — broad, their trunks smooth and silver-grey, their roots spreading across the forest floor like the fingers of buried hands. The canopy was high enough that the space beneath felt like a cathedral: vaulted, dim, filled with a light that was green and gold and faintly luminous. The undergrowth was thick but not impassable — ferns, moss, low bushes with leaves that were darker than they should have been, everything growing with an enthusiasm that bordered on aggression. The air was warm and heavy and tasted of copper and soil and something sweet that Mira couldn't name.

Kael's watch said they'd been walking for forty minutes when the rock appeared.

It was distinctive — a tall, narrow stone standing upright at the base of a ridge, pale grey, its surface worn smooth by something that wasn't weather. Aldric had identified it from Thom's descriptions as a likely landmark. They passed it, noting its position, and continued east.

Twenty minutes later, the rock was ahead of them again.

Mira stopped. "We've been here."

"We haven't turned," Kael said. He'd been tracking their course by pace count and the position of what light filtered through the canopy. His voice was flat — not arguing, just reporting.

"Sera?"

Sera was frowning, her head tilted, her hands open at her sides the way they went when she was reading the air. "The current shifted. I felt it — it moved, like a river changing course. The direction I was following is different now."

"Different how?"

"It's still east. But east is — it's not where it was."

Mira looked at the rock. It was the same rock. The same shape, the same pale stone, the same smooth surface. It was standing exactly where it had been standing half an hour ago, which meant either they'd walked in a circle without any of them noticing, or the forest had rearranged itself around them.

Neither option was encouraging.

"Adjust and continue," Mira said. "Sera, follow the current as it is now. Kael, mark trees."

Kael drew his knife and scored a cross into the nearest trunk. The bark parted easily, revealing pale wood beneath. He looked at the mark, nodded, and they moved on.

The marks helped. Not because the forest respected them — twice more in the next hour, a marked tree appeared ahead of them when it should have been behind — but because the pattern of the marks told them when the forest had shifted. When a mark appeared in the wrong place, they knew the terrain had moved, and Sera could adjust her sense to compensate.

It was slow. It was exhausting. And the deeper they went, the worse it got.

Time was the cruelest trick. Mira checked Kael's watch at intervals and found that the intervals didn't match her internal sense. A stretch that felt like twenty minutes was five by the watch. A stretch that felt like five was forty. She stopped checking after the third discrepancy because the dissonance between felt time and measured time was worse than not knowing.

Kael's revolver was the first equipment casualty. He checked it at what Mira estimated was the two-hour mark — or the four-hour mark, or the one-hour mark — and found the action sluggish, the cylinder resisting rotation. Not jammed. Reluctant, as if the mechanism had developed opinions about whether it wanted to function. He holstered it without expression and moved his knife to his primary hand.

"My casting is harder too," Aldric said quietly. He'd been attempting small workings — a Detect here, a Reveal there, trying to map the Aetheric landscape. The spells took more out of him than they should have, leaving him grey-faced and sweating. "Not the way I expected. It isn't that the power resists. It's that it doesn't. Every cantrip comes in at full strength when I want a whisper. A Detect I meant to cast at six feet went out to fifteen and brought back twice as much as I could hold in my head at once. It's like trying to write with a pen that's too full of ink. Everything bleeds. The effort isn't in pushing — it's in holding the spell down to a useful size."

Sera, by contrast, was incandescent. Not literally — though Mira wouldn't have been surprised. The flush on her face wasn't exertion. Her eyes were bright, her movements fluid, and when she reached for the Aether to test their direction, Mira could see the effect — a ripple in the air around her hands, visible for a fraction of a second, like heat haze in the shape of something intentional.

"I can feel the structure," Sera said. They were resting on a fallen trunk that was wider than the kitchen table in the flat. "It's — it's a pull. Not strong, but constant. Like a stone at the bottom of a stream. The current flows around it."

"How far?"

Sera closed her eyes. The ripple effect came again, stronger this time, and Mira felt the hair on her arms rise. "Close. Maybe an hour. But —"

"But time doesn't mean what it should in here."

"Right."

Mira looked at her team. Kael was watchful and steady, his knife in hand, scanning the forest with the mechanical patience of a man who'd decided that unpredictable terrain was just another kind of beat to walk. Aldric was tired but determined, his satchel clutched against his body, his notebook already half-filled with observations. Sera was alive in a way that Mira had never seen her — connected to the environment in a way that was clearly powerful and clearly not entirely under her control.

They were operating on guesswork and instinct in a place that didn't follow rules. They had no guide, no map, no reliable sense of time or distance. Their conventional equipment was failing by degrees. And somewhere behind them — back through the shifting forest, back past the settlement, back on the road — Ironwork Solutions was either following their trail or making their own way to the same destination.

This was the part of the job that no briefing prepared you for. The part where the plan dissolved and all you had left was the people you'd chosen and the bet you'd made that they were enough.

"We keep going," Mira said. "Stay close. Mark everything."

The clearing appeared an hour later, or three hours later, or sometime in the middle of the afternoon if the light filtering through the canopy could be trusted, which it couldn't.

They pushed through a line of undergrowth that had been thickening for the last stretch — ferns chest-high, with fronds that trembled at their passage as if startled — and stepped into open space. The canopy above thinned, and grey light poured down into a clearing perhaps two hundred yards across. The ground was flat and bare — not the soft forest floor of leaf litter and moss, but hard, smooth, a surface that looked like packed earth but felt, when Mira knelt and pressed her palm to it, like stone. Warm stone. The warmth had no source she could identify.

The structure sat at the clearing's center, and it was, first of all, a mill.

Mira hadn't expected to recognize it. The silhouette was from a previous century of someone else's life — a pair of grain silos rising from a lower block of mill house, a loading bay yawning open on one side, the stub of a smokestack snapped off at half its original height. Fifty years of forest had gnawed at the edges of it. A roof had collapsed on the far side. One of the silo caps was gone, the cylinder beneath it open to the sky. Ivy had made a long assault on the wall that faced them and failed — failed because the wall wasn't anything ivy knew how to climb. The plants had died six feet up and dried into brown rope on pale stone that wasn't stone.

That was the second thing. The shell had been a mill once. The shell was still, in outline, a mill. But the material had been replaced. Not overgrown — *replaced*, the concrete and steel and timber of the original bleached and remade into the seamless pale material they were now looking at, the mill's shape preserved and the mill's substance taken away. The silos were the same diameter as they had always been. The loading bay opened onto the same rectangle of ground where the wagons used to back up to the chutes. Only the stuff of the thing was wrong.

Sera had stopped walking. She was staring at the structure with an expression that Mira couldn't read. Not fear. Not awe. Something more private than either.

The openings — the loading bay, a doorway in the mill house, a gap where a window had been knocked out — were dark in a way that the early afternoon didn't explain. The light that should have been spilling into them simply wasn't.

"That's it," Aldric said. His voice was barely above a whisper. "That's — somebody built this. A long time ago. People did this. Put up the silos. Milled grain here. And then —" He was fumbling for his notebook with hands that had gone clumsy. "And then something else started building inside it. Something's still building inside it. Look at the walls. That's not the original material. That's not *any* original material."

The Aether here was visible. Not the faint luminescence of the deeper forest — actual light, pale blue-white, drifting like dust motes in the still air around the structure, thickest where the shell had been remade most completely.

Kael said nothing. He was looking at the openings in the structure, counting them, measuring the angles, doing the thing he always did when presented with a space he might need to fight in or escape from. His knife was in his hand. The revolver was in its holster, useless.

Mira looked at the structure, and the structure — in the way of things that shouldn't be alive but somehow occupied space with intention — looked back.

"We go in," she said. "Stay together. Mark your entry. If anything feels wrong, we get out. No arguments, no second chances."

They walked toward the pale shape in the clearing, and the Aetheric light drifted around them like snow.
