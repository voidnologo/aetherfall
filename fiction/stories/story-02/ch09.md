# Chapter 9

They heard Ironwork before they saw them.

Voices in the forest — muffled by the dense foliage, distorted by the Aether's tendency to play games with sound, but unmistakably human. Commands in a clipped cadence. The crunch of boots on undergrowth. A metallic sound that Kael identified without thinking: a rifle bolt cycling.

They'd been moving for what Kael estimated was an hour, though the estimate was worthless. Sera had led them north from the clearing, following an Aetheric current that wound through the forest like a river through floodplain — not straight, not fast, but navigable. The trees were thick enough to provide concealment. The undergrowth was dense enough to muffle their passage. But the Wilds' spatial distortion worked against secrecy the same way it worked against navigation: a sound that should have been half a mile behind them was suddenly close, and then far, and then close again, and the direction kept shifting.

"Stop." Kael held up a fist. The others froze.

He listened. The voices were to the south — between them and the direct route back to the settlement. Three distinct voices, maybe four. Moving with the discipline of trained operators, but frustrated — the forest was fighting them the same way it had fought the Society, and their formation was ragged.

"They're on our original path," Sera whispered. "Following the current I used coming in. They must have found our markers — Kael's knife marks on the trees."

"Can they track us on this route?"

"Not by the marks. But if they have anyone with Aetheric sensitivity, they could feel the current I'm following now."

Kael processed this. Ironwork Solutions was a Galvanic-leaning outfit — the Collective's preferred Society, equipped with Engine-enhanced gear, trained for zone operations. They might not have a caster. But "might not" was not a number Kael was willing to bet four lives on.

"Keep moving. Faster."

They moved. The forest shifted around them — the slow, dreaming rearrangement that had become familiar enough to navigate but not familiar enough to trust. A ridge appeared to their left that hadn't been there minutes ago. A stream crossed their path, shallow and cold, its water clear as glass and tasting faintly of metal. They crossed it and kept going.

The first shot came from the south.

Not aimed at them — aimed at the forest, at a target the shooter couldn't see, fired in frustration or as a signal. The sound was wrong. Not the clean crack of a rifle in open air — a dull, stuttering bark, the sound of a firearm fighting its own mechanism in Aetheric saturation. The bullet went somewhere. Probably not where it was aimed.

The second shot was closer.

Kael pulled the revolver and checked it. The action was still sluggish — the cylinder turned, but grudgingly, like a wheel in mud. He cocked it manually, feeling the mechanism resist. In these conditions, the revolver was a single-action proposition: cock, aim, fire, cock again. No fanning. No rapid follow-up. Each shot was a commitment.

"Kael." Mira was beside him, her arc pistol in hand. The Galvanic weapon was worse off than his revolver — the crystal chamber that powered it was dim, the hum that usually accompanied its charge barely audible. "The arc pistol is marginal. How's the revolver?"

"One shot at a time. The action's too slow for anything else."

"I've got the derringer." She pulled the holdout from her boot — a two-shot mechanical pistol, simple enough to function in heavy saturation. "Two rounds. Make them count."

"There are four of them."

"Then we'd better not miss."

A shout from the south — closer now, disturbingly close, the Wilds' spatial compression bringing Ironwork's voices to conversational distance even though they might still be a hundred yards away. Or fifty. Or twenty. The forest offered no guarantees about anything, including how much space separated you from the people trying to kill you.

Sera said, "I can push them back."

Kael looked at her. Her eyes were bright — too bright, the blue-white ring around her irises visible even in the forest's dim green light. The Aether here was feeding her, filling her reserves while it drained everyone else's. She looked alive in a way that was beautiful and wrong, like a flame that was burning too hot.

"Do it controlled," Kael said. "Nothing you can't rein in."

"I'll try."

"Don't try. Do."

Her jaw tightened. She nodded.

They heard them before they saw them. Then they saw them.

Ironwork Solutions came through the undergrowth in a loose file — four operators in reinforced coats, carrying rifles and sidearms with the easy competence of people who used them regularly. Their gear was good. Their formation was professional. Their faces were set with the grim focus of people who had a job to do and didn't intend to be talked out of it.

The lead operator saw Kael at the same moment Kael saw him. There was a beat — less than a second, the gap between recognition and action — and then everything happened at once.

Kael fired. The revolver barked once, the sound flat and muffled in the saturated air. The shot was true — eighteen years of marksmanship didn't evaporate because the mechanism was reluctant. The lead operator staggered, hit in the shoulder, his rifle dropping from nerveless fingers.

Sera threw force.

She didn't shout. She didn't gesture dramatically. She extended one hand, palm out, and the air between her and the Ironwork line detonated. Not an explosion — a concussion. A wave of pure kinetic energy that tore through the undergrowth and hit the three standing operators like a wall of moving air. They went down — thrown backward, their formation shattered, one of them hitting a tree trunk with a sound that Kael heard through the Aetheric distortion with sickening clarity.

The force she'd thrown was more than she'd intended. Kael could see it in her face — the flash of something that wasn't triumph. The undergrowth where the wave had passed was flattened in a cone, ferns bent to the ground, branches snapped, the air shimmering with residual energy. One of the operators wasn't moving. The others were struggling to rise, disoriented, bleeding.

No time to assess. "Move!"

They ran. Sera led, navigating by instinct and Aetheric sense, ducking through gaps in the undergrowth that seemed to open before her and close behind. Kael ran rearguard, the revolver cocked and ready, his knife in his off hand. Behind them, shouting — Ironwork recovering, regrouping, one voice rising above the others in a command to pursue.

A shot cracked through the trees. The sound was wrong again — muffled, distorted — but the bullet was real enough. It hit a tree six inches from Aldric's head, spraying bark chips into the air. Aldric flinched but didn't stop.

The forest shifted.

The ridge that had been to their left was suddenly ahead of them, cutting across their path like a wall. Sera veered right without breaking stride, following a current Kael couldn't feel. The trees thickened. The light dimmed. Behind them, the shouting grew more distant — then suddenly closer — then distant again. The Wilds were folding the space between pursuer and pursued, and the result was chaos. Neither side could maintain a consistent distance. The forest was shuffling them like cards.

"Shield!" Aldric's voice, strained. The spell went up behind them — a wall of invisible force, not solid but resistant, like trying to walk through heavy water. It wouldn't stop a bullet. It would slow a person. Aldric stumbled with the cost of it, and Mira caught his arm and kept him moving.

Another shot. This one went wide — way wide, the trajectory bent by something, the Aetheric saturation doing to firearms what it always did, what it had been doing for fifty years in every Wild Zone in the world. Making them unreliable. Making them liars.

But they were still firing. One shot in five might fly true, and one true shot was all it took.

Kael stopped.

He stopped because someone had to, and because stopping was what he'd been trained to do — not running, not retreating, but turning to face what was behind him and buying the people ahead of him enough time to get clear. He pressed his back against a tree trunk wide enough to cover his silhouette. He raised the revolver. He cocked it.

An Ironwork operator appeared through the ferns — running, rifle up, twenty yards out and closing. The rifle was jamming — Kael could see the operator working the bolt with desperate speed, trying to force a round into a chamber that didn't want to cooperate.

Kael shot him in the leg.

The revolver coughed. The operator went down, clutching his thigh, his rifle clattering into the undergrowth. Behind him, a second operator appeared, saw Kael's position, and fired.

The shot hit the tree. Bark exploded next to Kael's ear. He ducked, cocked the revolver, leaned out, and fired again. Wide. The Aether was pulling at the bullet the way it pulled at everything mechanical — not a lot, not enough to explain at close range, but at twenty yards through saturated air, the deviation was real.

The operator fired again. The rifle jammed.

Kael didn't wait. He ran — backward at first, keeping the tree between himself and the operator, then turning and sprinting north, following the sounds of the others crashing through the undergrowth ahead. Behind him, the operator swore — a sound that was viciously human in a forest full of things that weren't.

The forest helped.

Not deliberately. Not kindly. But the same spatial distortion that had been tormenting them all day decided, in this moment, to fold the space behind Kael like a curtain drawn across a stage. The sounds of pursuit — the crashing, the shouting, the aborted shots — grew distant in a way that had nothing to do with running speed. The ridge reappeared to their left, then behind them, and suddenly the undergrowth thinned and the trees opened and the air smelled different — still Aetheric, still saturated, but lighter. Less dense. The edge of the deep zone.

Sera was ahead of him, pulling Aldric by the sleeve. Mira was beside them, the derringer still in her hand, both shots unfired. Kael caught up and fell into step.

"Everyone whole?"

"Whole enough." Mira's voice was tight. There was blood on Aldric's temple — bark fragments from the near miss, not a wound, but enough to look alarming. Sera's eyes were glowing. Not the faint ring from before — a full luminescence, blue-white, the iris and the sclera both lit from within. She didn't seem to notice. Or she noticed and was choosing not to address it.

"Did you —" Sera started, looking at Kael. At the blood on his knife. At the revolver with three rounds remaining.

"They'll live," Kael said. He didn't know if that was true. The leg shot had been clean, but the operator Sera had thrown into the tree had been very still. "We need to move."

They moved. The forest lightened around them by degrees — the cathedral spaces giving way to something more ordinary, the canopy thinning, the undergrowth losing its aggressive density. The luminescence faded. The copper taste in the air weakened. Kael's revolver loosened in his hand, the action smoothing, the mechanism remembering what it was supposed to do.

They were coming out of the Wilds.

The settlement appeared through the trees as the light shifted from green-gold to grey — the natural grey of an overcast sky, the ordinary light of the ordinary world filtering through an ordinary canopy. The clearing. The buildings. The gardens, still impossibly lush. A thin line of chimney smoke rising from the largest structure.

Kael counted heads one more time. Four. All standing. All moving under their own power, which was more than he'd expected an hour ago.

Sera's eyes were still glowing.

He decided not to mention it yet. There would be time. Right now, the priority was distance between them and whatever was left of Ironwork Solutions in the forest behind them.

They walked into the settlement, and the Sootborn watched them come, and nobody needed to ask what had happened in the Wilds.
