# Chapter 2

The road out of Ashwick ran northeast through a mile of residential sprawl before the buildings gave up and let the land breathe.

Kael marked the transition the way he'd marked a thousand others in his years walking beats — by what changed around him. The row houses thinned to cottages. The cottages thinned to farmsteads. The cobblestones gave way to packed earth, rutted by cart wheels and softened by last week's rain. The factory hum faded first, then the Aetheric sweetness that drifted from the Quarter on morning wind, and then there was just the road and the fields and the grey sky of early spring pressing down on all of it.

He walked point, ten paces ahead of the others. Old habit. A constable walked ahead of his partner because the one in front saw trouble first and the one behind saw what happened when trouble found you. Eighteen years of that had worn the pattern into him deeper than thought.

The countryside was ordinary. That word kept surfacing in Kael's mind like a stone in a plowed field, and he kept turning it over, testing its weight. Ordinary. The fields on either side of the road were planted — early barley, he thought, from the color and the height of the shoots. Hedgerows divided the plots with the stubborn geometry of generations of farmers arguing about boundaries. A stone wall ran along the road's south side, grey and lichen-spotted, built before the Tear by people who expected the wall to outlast them and had been proven right.

Nobody here was fighting over metaphysics. The nearest zone influence was miles behind them. The farmers working these fields worried about rain and prices and the health of their livestock, the same worries that farmers had carried since before anyone alive could remember. There was comfort in that. There was also a warning: the world's normality was the thing that made the zones feel wrong, and they were walking toward the wrongest place any of them had ever been.

They stopped at a coaching inn at midmorning — a two-story stone building at a crossroads, smoke from the chimney, a hand-painted sign showing a hare in flight. The innkeeper was a broad man with a red face who sold them water and bread and directions they didn't need without asking where they were headed. Coaching inn etiquette. People passing through had their own business, and the business of the innkeeper was selling water and bread.

Kael ate standing, watching the road behind them while the others rested. Mira caught his eye.

"Expecting company?"

"Just looking." He finished the bread and dusted his hands. "Fresh tracks on the road this morning. Horses, not a cart. Moving fast, same direction we're going."

"Could be anyone."

"Could be." He didn't elaborate. Mira was smart enough to draw the line from *the Collective dispatched Ironwork* to *fresh hoofprints heading northeast*, and smart enough not to say it out loud in a coaching inn where the walls were thin and the innkeeper had a professional interest in gossip.

They moved on. The road narrowed past the crossroads, becoming a track used more by local farmers than through-travelers. The land rose gently into low hills — nothing dramatic, just the countryside gathering itself into folds. A village appeared and passed: a church, a general store, a cluster of houses around a well. An old woman on her porch watched them go by with the idle interest of someone who had nothing better to observe. A dog followed them for a quarter mile before losing interest.

Kael fell back to walk beside Aldric, who was carrying his satchel with the careful posture of a man transporting something fragile and irreplaceable.

"You've studied the Wilds," Kael said. Not a question.

"Academically. Zone drift patterns, Aetheric accumulation data. I've never been inside a Wild Zone stronger than moderate." Aldric adjusted the satchel strap on his shoulder. "The literature suggests that the edges are navigable. The deeper zones are where the reports become — contradictory."

"Contradictory how?"

"People who go in together come out with different accounts of what they experienced. Not conflicting testimony — genuinely different experiences. As if the zone doesn't present itself consistently to different observers." Aldric paused, the way he did when he was choosing between a precise statement and one that would actually be useful. "The practical concern is navigation. Distance and time don't behave normally in strong Aetheric zones. The more saturated the environment, the less reliable conventional wayfinding becomes."

"Compasses?"

"Useless past moderate saturation. Watches are unreliable. Landmarks shift."

"So we navigate by what?"

Aldric glanced ahead, to where Sera was walking with the slightly elevated posture of someone whose attention was split between the road and something only she could feel. "In the academic literature, the most reliable navigators in Wild Zones are high-sensitivity casters. They can feel the Aetheric current — its direction, its intensity. It's not a compass. It's closer to how a fish navigates a river."

"By feeling which way the water pushes."

"Essentially."

Kael considered this. "So we're relying on Sera."

"In the Wilds, yes. She may be our most important asset."

Kael said nothing. He trusted Sera. He trusted her intentions, her courage, the fundamental decency that she carried like a lantern in a dark room. What he didn't trust was the thing she reached into when she cast — the vast, alien force that gave her power and asked for pieces of her in return. In the Wilds, where that force was strongest, she'd be reaching deeper than she ever had. The Aether would give her more, and it would take more, and the transaction was one that nobody fully understood.

He checked his revolver. The action was smooth and clean. Out here, in neutral territory, it was just a machine — well-maintained, reliable, doing exactly what it was designed to do. In a few hours, or a day, or however long it took to reach the Wilds, that would change. The mechanism would start to question itself, the way all mechanisms did when reality got uncertain.

The knife at the small of his back didn't have that problem. It was steel. It had an edge. It worked the same way in a Dead Zone and a Deep Wild, and it would work the same way in whatever they were walking toward.

The afternoon wore on. The road climbed into the hills, and the hills grew less gentle — steeper folds, rockier soil, the farmland giving way to pasture and then to scrub. The trees thickened. Not Wild — just forest, the kind of woodland that had always been here, oaks and beeches with new leaves pale green against the grey sky. But the trees were taller than they should have been. Kael noticed it the way he noticed everything that didn't fit: a subtle wrongness, an excess of growth that had no agricultural explanation.

Sera had stopped walking. She stood in the middle of the track, head tilted slightly, the compass on its cord lifted from her chest by a hand that wasn't entirely steady.

"Sera," Kael said.

"I can feel it." Her voice was quiet, focused. "The Aether. It's — it's not like the Quarter. It's deeper. Wider." She turned the compass in her fingers. The needle was drifting, not spinning — a slow, uncertain rotation, as if the magnetic north it was meant to point toward had become a suggestion rather than a fact. "We're getting close."

The last village they passed was barely a village — six houses, a shared well, and a ward-post at its boundary. The post was a pine trunk, stripped and carved with symbols that Aldric identified as Aetheric protection wards. Crude work, he said. Folk practice, not scholarly casting. But functional — the kind of thing a community carved when they lived close enough to the Wilds to need a reminder of where the ordinary world ended.

The people in the village watched them pass. Not with hostility. With the flat, assessing gaze of people who lived on a boundary and had learned to take the measure of everyone who crossed it. A man repairing a fence straightened and stared until they were past. A woman in a doorway pulled her child inside and closed the door. Not against the Society specifically. Against whatever the Society's passage implied.

Beyond the village, the road ended. Not gradually — it simply stopped, as if the road-builders had reached a point where continuing seemed unwise and turned around. Ahead, the tree line rose like a wall. The forest beyond was denser than anything they'd passed through, the canopy closed and dark, the undergrowth thick in a way that suggested growth unchecked by any human hand.

And the air was different. Kael couldn't taste it the way Sera could, couldn't feel the Aetheric current the way she described it. But he could feel something — a thickness, a presence, the sense of standing at the edge of a room where something very large was breathing.

"That's it," Sera said. She was staring at the tree line the way a person stares at the ocean from a dock — not at the water, but at what the water implied about the size of the world. "The Wilds."

Kael checked the road behind them one more time. Empty. The hoofprints had continued past the last village, tracking northeast on a parallel path. Whoever was ahead of them was taking a different route to the same destination.

"Let's find the settlement," he said, and started walking toward the trees.
