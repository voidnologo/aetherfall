# Chapter 2

The Society car was a boxy grey saloon that the Covenant's quartermaster had handed Mira at first light along with a fuel chit and the shrugging assurance that it would probably get them as far as they needed. Kael had driven the first stretch out of the city because Kael drove most things when the group was nervous — he preferred a wheel in his hand to a seat he couldn't steer.

The road out of Ashwick ran northeast through a mile of residential sprawl before the buildings gave up and let the land breathe.

Kael marked the transition the way he'd marked a thousand others in his years walking beats — by what changed around the vehicle. The row houses thinned to cottages. The cottages thinned to farmsteads. The cobblestones ran out into macadam, the macadam ran out into packed earth oiled against dust. The factory hum faded first, then the Aetheric sweetness that drifted from the Quarter on morning wind, and then there was just the road and the fields and the grey sky of early spring pressing down on all of it.

Aldric rode beside him with a map flat on his knees, though the map grew less useful by the mile. Mira was in the back seat, one boot on the running board with the window half-down, watching the road behind them in the wing mirror. Sera had the seat behind Kael. She was quiet, her hand on the door frame, her head tilted toward the open window like a dog reading a wind.

The engine was a friendly animal at first. It chewed up the miles east of the city with the steady four-cylinder insistence that had made these cars ubiquitous in the last decade — a machine that did not ask much and did what was asked of it. Kael had never owned one, couldn't have afforded to, but he'd driven patrol wagons on the same frame for years. The pedals fell where his feet expected them.

They stopped at a coaching inn at midmorning — a two-story stone building at a crossroads that had added a filling pump and a pair of oil drums to its original business in the last decade without bothering to repaint its sign. The sign was a hare in flight, the paint weathered into suggestion. The innkeeper was a broad man with a red face who sold them water and bread and gasoline and directions they didn't need without asking where they were headed. Coaching inn etiquette. People passing through had their own business, and the business of the innkeeper was selling water and bread and, now, fuel.

Kael ate standing, watching the road behind them while the others rested. Mira caught his eye.

"Expecting company?"

"Just looking." He finished the bread and dusted his hands. "Fresh tracks on the road this morning. Cart ruts laid over yesterday's rain, and tire tracks over the cart. Wide tread. Moving fast, same direction we're going."

"Could be anyone."

"Could be." He didn't elaborate. Mira was smart enough to draw the line from *the Collective dispatched Ironwork* to *fresh tire tracks heading northeast*, and smart enough not to say it out loud at a filling pump where the walls were thin and the innkeeper had a professional interest in gossip.

They moved on. The road narrowed past the crossroads, becoming a track used more by local farmers than through-travelers. The macadam gave out first, replaced by cracked old paving stones laid a generation ago and never replaced when the frost pulled them apart. The saloon's tires thumped over the gaps. The land rose gently into low hills — nothing dramatic, just the countryside gathering itself into folds. A village appeared and passed: a church, a general store, a cluster of houses around a well. An old woman on her porch watched them go by with the idle interest of someone who had nothing better to observe. A dog ran alongside the wheel for a quarter mile before losing interest.

Past the village, they met the first sign.

It was bolted to an iron post that had rusted down into the color of the soil. The enamel face was bubbled, the blue field faded almost to grey, the black letters still legible if you knew what to look for:

**AETHERIC INFLUENCE AHEAD**
**LEAVE VEHICLES — TURN BACK**
**BY ORDER — INTERIM AUTHORITY**

Aldric looked up from his map.

"Post-Fall," he said. "The Interim Authority put these up in the first years after the Tear, when they still believed the zones could be fenced. The sign is about forty years older than the car we're driving in."

"It's still here," Kael said.

"Yes. Who was going to take it down?"

They drove past. The sign dwindled in the wing mirror and was replaced, a mile further on, by another — knocked sideways by some long-ago collision, its face cracked, the words on it fading into the same uselessness. Then a third, lying flat in the ditch with weeds growing through the post holes. Kael found himself counting them without meaning to. The Authority had put these up in sequence, once, when someone in a city office had believed that a row of signs could hold the shape of the old world against what was coming. The signs had been wrong. So had the belief.

The engine coughed for the first time ten miles past the third sign.

It was a small cough — a stutter, a hiccup in the firing order, the kind of thing a driver might dismiss as dirty fuel. Kael didn't dismiss it. He watched the road and waited. Two minutes later the engine coughed again, and this time the saloon lost a hair of speed before recovering. The needle on the tachometer dropped and climbed and dropped. The ignition timing was drifting in ways that had nothing to do with the carburetor.

"Here we go," Kael said.

"Already?" Sera had been watching the trees by the road. She turned toward the front of the car, her face bright with something that wasn't alarm. "It's — I can feel it."

"Describe."

"Just — there's weight in the air. Not like the Quarter. Heavier. Lower. Something under the ground, if that makes sense."

"It doesn't," Kael said, "but I believe you."

The paving stones ran out. The road became packed earth again, then packed earth rutted into tracks by wooden wheels, then something that was really a farmer's path held open by regular use. The fields beside the road were less well-kept than the ones closer to the city — hedgerows thickening into something that hadn't been pruned in a decade, a stone wall missing two courses on top, a gate gone off its hinges. The air changed in small increments. The trees were taller than they should have been. The birdsong thinned to a few calls Kael didn't recognize, then to silence.

The engine coughed again, harder, and ran rough for a quarter mile before smoothing itself out.

He fell into the pattern without thinking about it — letting the revs fall when the mechanism complained, coaxing the saloon forward on instinct rather than demand. A car was a conversation between the driver and the machine, and the machine had begun to speak another language. Out past the edge, past where the signs had been telling them to turn back for forty years, the conversation would cease to be productive at all.

"You've studied the Wilds," Kael said. Not a question.

"Academically. Zone drift patterns, Aetheric accumulation data. I've never been inside a Wild Zone stronger than moderate." Aldric shifted the satchel on the floor between his feet. "The literature suggests that the edges are navigable. The deeper zones are where the reports become — contradictory."

"Contradictory how?"

"People who go in together come out with different accounts of what they experienced. Not conflicting testimony — genuinely different experiences. As if the zone doesn't present itself consistently to different observers." Aldric paused, the way he did when he was choosing between a precise statement and one that would actually be useful. "The practical concern is navigation. Distance and time don't behave normally in strong Aetheric zones. The more saturated the environment, the less reliable conventional wayfinding becomes."

"Compasses?"

"Useless past moderate saturation. Watches are unreliable. Landmarks shift."

"So we navigate by what?"

Aldric glanced into the back seat, where Sera sat with her head slightly tilted, her attention split between the road ahead and something only she could feel. "In the academic literature, the most reliable navigators in Wild Zones are high-sensitivity casters. They can feel the Aetheric current — its direction, its intensity. It's not a compass. It's closer to how a fish navigates a river."

"By feeling which way the water pushes."

"Essentially."

Kael considered this. "So we're relying on Sera."

"In the Wilds, yes. She may be our most important asset."

Kael said nothing. He trusted Sera. He trusted her intentions, her courage, the fundamental decency that she carried like a lantern in a dark room. What he didn't trust was the thing she reached into when she cast — the vast, alien force that gave her power and asked for pieces of her in return. In the Wilds, where that force was strongest, she'd be reaching deeper than she ever had. The Aether would give her more, and it would take more, and the transaction was one that nobody fully understood.

His revolver was holstered at his hip, and he didn't touch it. Out here, on the last honest stretch of road, it was just a machine — well-maintained, reliable, doing exactly what it was designed to do. The car under his hands was the same kind of machine, for now. In a few hours, or a few miles, or however much further the road allowed, both would change. The mechanisms would start to question themselves, the way all mechanisms did when reality got uncertain.

The knife at the small of his back didn't have that problem. It was steel. It had an edge. It worked the same way in a Dead Zone and a Deep Wild, and it would work the same way in whatever they were driving toward.

The afternoon wore on. The road climbed into the hills, and the hills grew less gentle — steeper folds, rockier soil, the farmland giving way to pasture and then to scrub. The trees thickened. Not Wild — just forest, the kind of woodland that had always been here, oaks and beeches with new leaves pale green against the grey sky. But the trees were taller than they should have been. Kael noticed it the way he noticed everything that didn't fit: a subtle wrongness, an excess of growth that had no agricultural explanation.

The engine coughed three more times in the next hour, each cough worse than the last. The last village appeared at the hour's end — appeared the way a village does when a road has been climbing toward a place it couldn't quite see, rising over a final shoulder of land to reveal the cluster of low houses gathered around a well. Six buildings. A barn. A ward-post at the settlement's edge — a pine trunk stripped of its bark and carved with symbols that Kael didn't recognize.

And a yard full of abandoned cars.

Not a lot. A dozen, maybe fifteen, arranged without pattern in a field beside the road. The nearest had been there for decades — a boxy pre-war thing with its tires rotted to the rims and ivy growing through the floorboards. The furthest was newer, its paint still mostly present, its headlamps reflecting the grey sky. Between the two extremes was every year of motoring since the Tear, each car parked with a kind of patience, as if the owner had meant to come back and then had reconsidered, or hadn't reconsidered, or hadn't been able to.

Kael slowed the saloon and coasted up to the gate. A man in a leather apron stepped out of a shed that was half-forge and half-tool shop, wiping his hands on a rag.

"Going in?" the man said. His accent was flat country.

"That's the idea."

"Most folks park it here. Car won't get you another mile past the post, and if it does, it won't come back." The man glanced at the saloon's grille, then at Kael, then at the three in the car with him. His eyes moved over Sera longer than the others. Folk in a village like this knew how to spot someone who could feel the zone. "Shilling a week for watching. Two shillings extra if you want me to check the engine and the tires. No promises on condition when you come back, but I'll keep the kids and the crows off it."

Kael looked at Mira. Mira nodded.

"We'll take the watching," Kael said. "Not the checking. She'll sit as she is."

"Suit yourself."

They got out. The air at the gate was different — not the slow weight it had carried a few miles back, but something more distinct, a thickness with edges. Kael could feel it against his skin like the first humidity before a storm. Sera stood a pace away from the car with her hand flat on her sternum, breathing slowly. Aldric was looking at the ward-post as if cataloguing a type specimen.

"Crude work," he murmured. "Folk practice. Not scholarly casting. But the symbols are in the right arrangement. Someone here had a grandfather who knew what he was doing."

They transferred what they could carry from the car to their packs. Kael hefted the kit and adjusted the strap on his shoulder. The saloon sat behind them with its engine ticking as it cooled, small and ordinary, already beginning to join the company of the yard.

The villagers watched. Not with hostility. With the flat, assessing gaze of people who lived on a boundary and had learned to take the measure of everyone who crossed it. A man repairing a fence straightened and stared until they were past. A woman in a doorway pulled her child inside and closed the door. Not against the Society specifically. Against whatever the Society's passage implied.

Beyond the village, the road ended. Not gradually — it simply stopped, as if the road-builders had reached a point where continuing seemed unwise and turned around. Ahead, the tree line rose like a wall. The forest beyond was denser than anything they'd passed through, the canopy closed and dark, the undergrowth thick in a way that suggested growth unchecked by any human hand.

And the air was different. Kael couldn't taste it the way Sera could, couldn't feel the Aetheric current the way she described it. But he could feel something — a thickness, a presence, the sense of standing at the edge of a room where something very large was breathing.

"That's it," Sera said. She was staring at the tree line the way a person stares at the ocean from a dock — not at the water, but at what the water implied about the size of the world. "The Wilds."

Kael checked the road behind them one more time. Empty. The tire tracks they'd been following for the better part of the day had continued past the village on a parallel track — not into this yard of rusting vehicles, but further east, onto a road that wasn't quite a road. Whoever was ahead of them was pushing their machine further than the village man thought wise. That was information. Kael filed it.

"Let's find the settlement," he said, and started walking toward the trees.
