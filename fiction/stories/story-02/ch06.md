# Chapter 6

The inside of the structure was worse than the outside.

Not worse the way a dark room was worse than a lit one. Worse the way a word was worse when you couldn't quite remember what it meant — familiar enough to recognize, wrong enough to make your mind itch. The corridors were corridors. The walls were walls. The floor was a floor. But the proportions were built for something that wasn't quite human, and the angles were drawn from a geometry that didn't care about the one Sera had grown up in.

She went first. Not because anyone asked her to — because the Aether inside the structure was so dense and strange that she was the only one who could see.

Not literally. There was light. A pale luminescence that came from the walls themselves, from veins of something running through the seamless material like capillaries in skin. The light pulsed — slowly, irregularly, with a rhythm that wasn't quite a heartbeat but was close enough to make her own pulse try to match it. In that light, the corridors stretched in directions that the human eye could follow but the human mind had trouble accepting. Left became forward became down, not all at once but in increments, the way a river turned — gradual enough that you didn't notice until you were facing a direction you hadn't intended.

"Sera." Kael's voice, behind her. Steady. She was grateful for that. "How are you doing?"

"The Aether is — it's thick in here. But it's different from the forest. In the forest it felt alive. In here it feels..." She reached for the word. "Stored. Like it's been collected and kept. Like a library, but for power instead of books."

"Can you cast?"

She extended her hand and pulled a thread of force. It came easily — too easily, rushing to her fingertips with an eagerness that made her flinch. A ball of pale fire blossomed in her palm, burning blue-white and brighter than she'd intended. The shadows it cast moved independently of the light source, sliding along the walls in patterns that were almost recognizable, almost meaningful.

"Yes," she said. "I can cast."

The structure's interior was a labyrinth, but not a random one. There was a logic to the corridors — Sera could feel it the way she felt the Aetheric current, as a pattern just below the threshold of understanding. Halls branched and reconnected. Rooms opened off the main corridors, empty or nearly so — some contained pedestals, smooth-surfaced and featureless, as if objects had been displayed there and removed. Some contained panels set into the walls: surfaces of a different material, darker, warm to the touch, etched with lines that might have been writing or might have been circuitry or might have been something else entirely.

"Don't touch those," Aldric said. He was behind her, his voice tight with the particular strain of a man who was terrified and fascinated in equal measure. He had his notebook open and was sketching as they walked, his pencil moving in short, precise strokes. "The etched panels — I have no frame of reference. They could be inert. They could be active components. Until I can study them properly —"

"Aldric." Mira, behind him. "Keep up."

They pressed deeper. Kael marked each turn with his knife — a cross on the left wall, scored deep into the pale material. The material resisted more than wood or stone; it took genuine force to leave a mark. But it held. The scoring stayed. That small fact was an anchor in a place that offered few.

The first shift happened ten minutes in. Or five minutes. Or twenty — in here, Sera's time sense was even less reliable than it had been in the forest.

The corridor they were walking through changed. Not dramatically — the walls didn't move, the floor didn't tilt. But the proportions shifted. The ceiling was higher than it had been a moment ago. The corridor was narrower. The turn they were approaching — a left turn that Sera had felt in the Aetheric pattern — was now a T-junction, offering left and right where before there had been only left.

"That wasn't there," Kael said.

"No," Sera agreed.

They looked at each other. Kael's face was the face he always wore when the situation was bad enough to be simple: no uncertainty, no second-guessing, just the flat assessment of a man deciding whether to go forward or go back. He looked at the T-junction, looked behind them at the corridor they'd come through — still there, still marked — and then looked at Sera.

"Which way?"

She closed her eyes. The Aetheric current inside the structure was complex — layered, braided, flowing in multiple directions simultaneously, like a river with currents at different depths moving in different ways. But underneath the complexity, there was a pull. Faint, constant, the gravitational weight of something dense. The artifact.

"Left," she said.

They went left.

The deeper they went, the more the structure changed around them. The shifts were subtle — a room that was larger when they looked back at it than when they'd passed through. A corridor that curved where it had been straight. A doorway that hadn't existed until Sera looked directly at it, and then had always been there, as if the structure was responding to observation itself.

The technology in the walls intensified. The luminous veins were thicker here, brighter, and the panels appeared more frequently — larger, more complex, the etched lines forming patterns that Aldric couldn't stop staring at and couldn't begin to decode. Something behind the walls hummed. Not the Galvanic hum of factory districts — something at a different frequency, felt in the teeth and the sternum, a vibration that lived at the intersection of the mechanical and the magical.

"Both," Aldric breathed. He had stopped walking, his hand pressed flat against a panel, his expression caught between wonder and distress. "The resonance in these walls — it's both. Aetheric and Galvanic. Not competing. Not canceling each other out. Operating together. That shouldn't — the literature says —"

"Aldric," Kael said.

"This changes everything. If these fields can coexist — if they were designed to coexist —"

"*Aldric.* Hand off the wall."

Aldric pulled his hand back as if burned. He stared at his palm. Nothing visible. But Sera had seen the flash — a ripple in the Aether around his hand when he'd touched the panel, as if the structure had tasted him and found the data interesting.

"Don't touch the walls," Kael said. It wasn't a suggestion.

They pushed on. The pull grew stronger. Sera was navigating almost entirely by feel now — the visual layout of the corridors was unreliable, but the Aetheric current didn't lie, and the dense point she was following was getting closer. She could feel it like a stone in her chest, warm and heavy, drawing her forward the way iron was drawn to a lodestone.

The chamber appeared at the end of a corridor that was shorter than it should have been. One moment they were walking through a hall that stretched into luminous distance; the next, the hall ended at an opening — wide, arched, the edges smooth as poured glass — and beyond it, the room.

The chamber was circular. The ceiling was high — higher than any room they'd passed through, rising into darkness that Sera's pale fire couldn't penetrate. The walls were covered in panels, dense with the etched lines, every surface inscribed. The luminous veins converged here, running from the corridors into the chamber like streams feeding a lake, their light concentrated into a glow that filled the room with cold blue-white radiance.

At the center, on a pedestal of the same seamless material, the container sat.

Sera saw it and felt something that she couldn't name — not recognition, not familiarity, but a resonance, as if a tuning fork inside her chest had been struck by the sight of it. The container was small — perhaps two feet long, a foot wide, half a foot deep. Its surface was smooth and dark, the color of deep water, and it caught the chamber's light without reflecting it. It looked solid. It looked *sealed*, in a way that went beyond physical closure — sealed the way a word was sealed in a language you didn't speak, present but inaccessible.

"There," she said, and her voice sounded strange in the chamber — too clear, too close, as if the room had been designed for speech and was surprised to finally hear some.

The others filed in behind her. Aldric made a sound that wasn't a word. Kael was silent, his eyes moving around the chamber with the systematic sweep of a man cataloguing threats. Mira stood at the threshold for a long moment before stepping through, and Sera saw her do the thing she always did — take the measure of the room, the situation, the odds — and come up with a number she didn't like.

"Can you reach it?" Mira asked.

Sera walked toward the pedestal. The Aetheric density in the chamber was extraordinary — the air itself was visible, thick with luminous particulate, and every breath tasted of copper and something older, something that had been sealed in this room for longer than Sera could guess. She reached for the container.

Her hand went through the opening where the container sat. She felt the movement — felt her arm extend, felt her fingers close on air. But when she looked, she was standing at the entrance of the chamber again, six paces back from where she'd been, facing the pedestal as if she'd never moved.

"What —"

She tried again. This time she watched her own feet, stepping deliberately toward the pedestal, counting paces. One. Two. Three. Four. She looked up. She was at the entrance. Six paces back.

"Sera?" Kael's voice was careful.

"I can't reach it. The room — it puts me back."

Aldric tried next. Three steps forward and he was at the entrance again, blinking, his hand still extended toward a pedestal that was ten feet away and might as well have been ten miles. He tried a Reveal — the spell cost him visibly, sweat beading on his forehead, his face going grey — and the results made him sit down heavily on the floor.

"The space between us and the pedestal isn't real space," he said. His voice was thin. "Or it's real space that's been folded. Walking through it returns you to the origin point. It's not a barrier — it's a loop."

Kael tried the direct approach. He walked toward the pedestal with the deliberate stride of a man who did not believe in impossible rooms, and found himself back at the entrance with the rest of them, his knife still in his hand and his expression suggesting that the room had just earned a place on a very short list of things that had managed to surprise him.

"It's not keeping us out," Sera said. She could feel it — the defense, if that's what it was, wasn't hostile. It wasn't even active, in any way she could identify. The space was simply *configured* so that approach was impossible. Like a staircase that only went up, no matter which direction you climbed. "It's just — made that way. The way a lock is made. It's not fighting us. We just don't have the key."

Aldric was staring at the container from across the room, his spectacles reflecting the blue-white light. "The container," he said. "Look at it. The material, the precision. That's not Aetheric. The zone, the forest, the spatial distortion — that's all Veil. But the container itself is — it looks *manufactured*. Machined. It has the qualities of Galvanic engineering, but there's no Engine signature. Or there is, but it's..." He trailed off, his researcher's vocabulary failing him in the face of something his categories couldn't hold. "It shouldn't be here. It doesn't belong in this zone. It doesn't belong in this *era*."

The chamber's light pulsed once, slowly, like a breath.

"We need to go," Kael said. Not with urgency — with the measured certainty of a man who knew when a situation had given him everything it was going to give.

Sera looked at the container one more time. The resonance in her chest was still there — the struck tuning fork, vibrating with something that wasn't sound. She didn't understand it. She wanted to. She wanted to stand in this room until understanding came, until the lock opened, until the sealed thing revealed whatever it was holding.

But Kael was right. They'd found it. They'd confirmed it. And they didn't have the key.

She turned and followed the others back into the corridors, and behind her the chamber pulsed, patient and sealed and very, very old.
