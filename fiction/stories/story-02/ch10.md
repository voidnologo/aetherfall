# Chapter 10

The Sootborn didn't ask what happened. They could see it.

Pell met them at the edge of the settlement with a look that was neither surprise nor sympathy — the flat recognition of a woman who had predicted exactly this and wished she'd been wrong. She looked at Aldric's grey face and the dried blood at his temple. She looked at Kael's revolver, holstered now, three rounds lighter than when he'd left. She looked at Sera's eyes, still carrying a luminescence that had no business being visible in daylight.

"Sit," Pell said. "Eat. Then leave."

They sat. The Sootborn brought food without being asked — the same stew, the same dense bread — and water from the well that tasted sweet and did something to the headache that had been building behind Mira's temples since the clearing. Kindness, but bounded. Hospitality with a perimeter.

"You found it," Pell said. She was standing with her arms folded, watching them eat.

"We found it." Mira kept her voice level. "We couldn't retrieve anything. The structure has defenses we can't breach."

"Good."

Mira looked at her.

"That thing has been sitting in the forest since before any of us were born," Pell said. "Since before the Tear, from what Adi described. It doesn't need your patrons digging at it. It doesn't need the Collective's people prying at it. It needs to be left alone."

"The people in the forest behind us might disagree."

Pell's expression tightened. "The ones who shot at you."

"Ironwork Solutions. Working for the Ashworth Collective. They followed us in, and they didn't come to negotiate." Mira held Pell's gaze. "They'll come back. Once we're gone, once our confirmation reaches the city, every faction in Ashwick is going to know that the site is real. The Collective will send more people. The Covenant will send more people. Others will follow. Your settlement is about to become a staging ground whether you want it to be or not."

The silence that followed was heavy with the thing Mira had just done — told the truth, completely, because Pell deserved it and because the Sootborn needed to prepare for what was coming. Mira was a social operator. She managed information the way other people managed money — carefully, strategically, releasing it in amounts calibrated to achieve specific effects. She didn't give information away for free.

This was free. Pell had earned it by being right about everything, and by feeding them when she didn't have to.

Pell was quiet for a long time. Then she said, "We'll manage. We always have." She unfolded her arms. "Finish your food. The road south is clear — no one's passed through since you left. Your friends from Ironwork came in from the northeast, different approach. They won't beat you back to the road."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Just go."

---

They left within the hour. The walk from the settlement to the road was easier than the walk in — the Wilds ended where the Wilds ended, and beyond the tree line the world snapped back to normal with a suddenness that felt like surfacing from deep water. The air was thin and clean. The sky was grey and ordinary. The trees were trees and nothing more.

Aldric slept on his feet for the first mile, a stumbling half-consciousness that Kael corrected with periodic adjustments to his trajectory, guiding him by the elbow the way you'd steer a boat in a gentle current. By the second mile, he'd woken enough to walk straight, though his face still had the translucent quality of a man who'd spent everything and hadn't started earning it back.

Sera's eyes had dimmed, but not fully. In the forest's fading light, the luminescence was barely visible — a trick of the evening, you'd tell yourself, if you didn't know what you were looking at. By the time they reached the road, it would be invisible to anyone who wasn't checking.

Mira was checking.

The road south was empty, as Pell had promised. The packed earth was solid under their boots, the hedgerows on either side were mundane, and the coaching inn at the crossroads — the one with the hare-in-flight sign — was lit and occupied and smelled of bread and coal smoke. They didn't stop. Speed mattered more than comfort, and the comfort of a coaching inn would dissolve the urgency that was keeping them on their feet.

They walked through the evening and into the night. The countryside was dark and quiet — farmland asleep, the occasional light from a cottage window, the distant bark of a dog. Normal. Ordinary. A world that had nothing to do with sealed structures and impossible containers and the sound of rifle fire in forests that didn't obey the rules.

Mira walked beside Kael and said nothing for a long time. The silence between them was their oldest language — the one they'd spoken on Harrier Street for years before the Society existed, the mutual recognition of two people who were processing the same information and would compare notes when the processing was done.

"They tried to kill us," Mira said finally.

"Yes."

"Not scare us. Not intimidate. Kill."

"Yes."

"That changes things."

Kael was quiet for three steps. "That changes everything. The Collective authorized lethal force against a chartered Society operating on a legitimate commission. If that becomes public, it's an act of war. If it doesn't become public —" He let the sentence hang.

"If it doesn't become public, they got away with it and they'll do it again."

"So it becomes public."

"Carefully," Mira said. "Through the right channels, at the right time, to the right ears. Not a broadsheet story. A quiet conversation with Inspector Laine, and another with Thessaly, and a very careful notation in the Society's records that can be produced if needed."

Kael looked at her sideways. "You're already three moves ahead."

"I'm always three moves ahead. It's the only advantage I have."

---

Dace Rennick found them at the second crossroads, eight miles south of the Wild edge, sitting on the stone wall that lined the road as if he'd been waiting for them specifically, which he probably had.

He was smoking. The thin cigar that he never seemed to finish, its ember a small orange point in the darkness. He was leaning against the wall with the relaxed posture of a man who had nowhere pressing to be, which was always a lie — Dace Rennick's casualness was as constructed as Mira's mirroring, and twice as deliberate.

"Evening," he said. "You look like hell."

"Dace." Mira stopped walking. The others stopped behind her, Kael's hand drifting to his revolver — a reflex, not a threat. "How did you know we'd be on this road?"

"I didn't. I knew you went northeast four days ago on a Covenant commission. I knew Ironwork went northeast two days ago on a Collective commission. I know what's northeast of here." He tapped ash from the cigar. "When two Societies go into the Wilds on competing commissions and one of them comes out looking like they lost a fight, I find that interesting."

"The Margin's interest is noted."

"The Margin's interest is for sale, as always." Dace pushed off the wall and stood. He was a big man — not tall, but broad, carrying weight that looked soft until you noticed how he moved. "What did you find?"

Mira calculated. Dace Rennick was an information broker. Information given to him would spread — controlled, priced, but spread. That could be useful or dangerous, depending on what she released.

She gave him the shape. Not the details.

"The site is real. Pre-Tear structure in the Wilds, approximately two days from the settlement by foot, though the Wilds' spatial distortion makes that estimate unreliable. The structure contains an artifact. The artifact is inaccessible — defended by mechanisms we don't understand and couldn't bypass. Nobody is taking it home."

Dace's eyebrows rose. "Inaccessible."

"Completely. The site itself is the find, not the artifact. And Ironwork Solutions tried to prevent us from bringing that confirmation back. Lethal force, in the Wilds."

She let that sit. Dace was smart enough to hear the implications: the Collective had escalated beyond professional competition into something that would cost them badly if it became known. That information was worth money. That information was worth more than money — it was leverage.

Dace drew on his cigar and regarded her through the smoke. "Lethal force against a chartered Society. The Collective's getting ambitious."

"Or desperate."

"Same thing, in my experience." He paused. "I'll keep this for now. The site confirmation is sellable. The Ironwork business is — delicate. I'll hold it until the price is right, or until holding it becomes more expensive than selling it."

"That's your business."

"It is." He smiled — the easy, charming smile that made you forget, for a moment, that he was a man who'd built a career on knowing things that other people wanted kept quiet. "Safe travels, Mira. You've had an interesting week."

He walked into the darkness, the cigar's ember shrinking to a point and then disappearing, and Mira stood on the road and thought about information and who held it and what it was worth when the world was watching.

---

Ashwick appeared on the horizon at midmorning — chimney smoke first, then rooftops, then the grey sprawl of a city that had never been beautiful and wasn't trying to start. The factories were running. The Galvanic hum reached them before the city's outline resolved, a vibration in the road that Mira felt through her boot soles. The train station's clock tower was visible from two miles out, its face too far away to read but its presence reassuring in the way that all familiar things were reassuring after days of the unfamiliar.

They entered through the northeast road — past the residential sprawl, past the coaching stops where the city's ordinary commerce carried on, past the people who worked and shopped and argued about prices and had no idea that four people were walking in from the Wilds carrying knowledge that would reshape the politics of their city.

The flat on Harrier Street was as they'd left it. Two rooms above the cobbler's shop, the stairs groaning under their weight, the door sticking in the frame the way it always did in damp weather. Inside, the smell of leather and lamp oil. The kitchen table where Kael cleaned his revolver. The corner chair where Aldric read. The window where Sera sat and watched the Quarter.

Home. Such as it was.

Aldric went straight to the corner chair and sat down with the careful deliberation of a man lowering himself into water. He opened his notebook and began transcribing, his pencil moving in the small, precise handwriting that required a magnifying lens to read. Whatever he'd seen in the structure's chamber — whatever conclusion he was building about the container, the timeline, the categories that didn't hold — he was committing it to his records before exhaustion erased the edges.

Kael sat at the kitchen table and disassembled his revolver. He cleaned each component with methodical care, removing the Wilds' residual effects — a faint stiffness in the spring, a barely visible corrosion on the cylinder pin that shouldn't have been there after four days. The gun had failed him, partially. The knife hadn't. He cleaned the knife too.

Sera sat by the window. Her eyes were normal again — or looked normal, the luminescence finally faded to something invisible in daylight. She was writing in her journal. Mira couldn't read her expression. Whatever had happened to Sera in the Wilds — the amplification, the power, the eyes that had glowed like lanterns — she was processing it alone, in the only record she kept, in the handwriting that no one else was allowed to see.

Mira stood at the kitchen table and opened the Ledger.

The Covenant's commission fee was listed in the terms Thessaly had provided. Confirmation of site existence: full payment. Artifact recovery: bonus. They'd earned the first and missed the second. The numbers were good — two months of operating costs, maybe three. Enough to keep the flat, keep the team equipped, keep the Society viable until the next job came through.

She noted the payment. She noted the expenses — supplies, provisions, the food purchased from the Sootborn. She noted the date, the job description, the outcome. She did not note the subtext: that they'd been sent as expendable assets on thin intelligence and had come back with confirmation that would make them indispensable.

The Covenant would want them again. The Collective would want them watched. The Margin would want them informed. Inspector Laine would want a quiet briefing. The Gradient Scholars would want Aldric's notes. Every faction in Ashwick was about to learn that a pre-Tear structure existed in the Wilds, that it contained an artifact no one could reach, and that Ash & Veil Recovery was the Society that found it.

They had been sent because they were available and unimportant. They were coming back as the people who knew where the most significant discovery in fifty years was located, what it looked like inside, and what had happened when they tried to reach it.

The Ledger balanced. The next job would be harder. The job after that would be harder still. And somewhere in the Wilds, in a clearing that the forest was probably already rearranging, a structure that nobody built sat on warm ground, and inside it a container that nobody made rested on a pedestal behind a lock that nobody could open, patient and sealed and waiting.

Mira closed the Ledger and went to make tea. There would be letters to write. Reports to file. Conversations to have, carefully, with precisely the right people at precisely the right time.

Tomorrow.

Tonight, they were home, and the job was done, and that was enough.
