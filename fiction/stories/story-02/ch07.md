# Chapter 7

The structure shifted while they were leaving.

Kael felt it before he saw it — a vibration in the floor, deep and slow, like a ship settling in its berth. Then the corridor they were walking through stretched. Not fast. Not violently. The walls didn't lurch or crack. They simply moved, the way a sleeping person shifts under a blanket, the proportions of the hallway elongating by inches over seconds until the turn ahead — the turn he'd marked with a cross on the left wall — was twice as far away as it had been.

"Move," he said.

They moved. Kael took point, Sera behind him, Aldric third, Mira at the rear. The order of people who could fight in front, people who could navigate next, people who could heal and observe after that, and people who could make decisions about what happened behind them at the back. It was the order they'd learned in the foundry tunnels on their first job, and it had worked then because it was simple and it used everyone's strengths.

The structure didn't care about their strengths.

The first knife mark was where it should have been — a cross on the left wall at the T-junction where Sera had guided them left. They turned right, retracing their path. The second mark was there too, twenty paces further, at a point where the corridor had curved. The third mark was on the wrong wall.

Kael stopped. He'd scored every mark on the left wall, at waist height, deep enough to gouge the pale material. The cross was now on the right wall, at shoulder height, in the same material, at the same depth. Not a different mark. His mark, in the wrong place.

"The walls swapped," Sera said. Her voice was tight. She was reading the Aether in the corridor the way you'd read smoke to find a fire — trying to determine what had moved and what was still true. "The corridor — it's mirrored. Left became right."

"Can you still feel the way out?"

She closed her eyes. The pale fire she'd been carrying guttered, then strengthened, and the shadows on the walls shifted in patterns that Kael deliberately didn't try to interpret. "Yes. The outside is — it's that way." She pointed down the corridor. "The Aether flows toward open air. I can follow it."

"Then forget the marks. Follow the current."

They moved faster. The structure continued to shift around them — the slow, geological rearrangement of a thing that wasn't hostile but wasn't helpful, a building with the metabolism of a living creature and the indifference of stone. Corridors they'd passed through changed: wider here, narrower there, a ceiling dropping by a foot, a floor developing a slope that hadn't existed ten minutes ago. The luminous veins in the walls pulsed faster, as if the structure's heartbeat had quickened.

Kael navigated by Sera's direction and his own instincts. Eighteen years of walking streets had given him an internal compass that didn't depend on magnetic north — a sense of spatial relationships, of distance covered and direction turned, that functioned independently of the tools that were failing around him. In the Greycoat days, he'd been able to find his way through the warren of streets behind the Ashworth Foundry in the dark, blindfolded, working from memory and the count of his steps. This was harder. The space itself was lying to him. But the lie wasn't perfect, and Kael had spent his career catching lies.

"Left here," Sera said.

They turned left into a corridor that hadn't been there on the way in. It was broader than the others, the ceiling higher, the veins in the walls thicker and brighter. Something was different about the air — a smell underneath the Aether's copper sweetness, faint and mechanical, the ghost of ozone. Engine resonance. Not strong enough to suppress the zone, but present, like a bass note under a melody.

"This is new," Aldric said. He was stumbling slightly — the earlier Reveal had cost him, and the structure's shifting wasn't helping his footing. But his eyes were alert behind his spectacles, cataloguing even now. "This corridor wasn't part of the layout we came through. The structure opened a new path."

"Opened, or shifted into place?"

"I don't know. Both? Neither? The distinction assumes the structure follows rules I understand, and it doesn't."

The new corridor led them toward a junction that was larger than any space they'd passed through — an atrium, almost, where six corridors met at a central point. The ceiling rose into darkness. The veins from all six corridors converged here, their light pooling into a brightness that made Kael's eyes ache.

Sera stopped at the edge of the atrium. "Something's wrong."

Kael scanned the space. Six corridors, six potential approaches, six potential exits. The atrium was empty. The floor was the same warm, seamless material. The light was intense but steady. Nothing moved.

"Wrong how?"

"The current." Sera's hands were open at her sides, fingers spread, her head tilted in the listening posture that meant she was reading the Aether at depth. "It's — it's circling. The flow in this room is circular. Like a whirlpool. If we step into it, I'm not sure which corridor we'll come out through."

"Which one leads out?"

She pointed. Third corridor from the left, its opening slightly wider than the others. "That one. I think. The outside air is that direction. But the current between here and there is —" She made a gesture with her hand, a swirling motion. "Scrambled."

Kael looked at the atrium. Looked at the corridor Sera indicated. Thirty feet of open floor between here and there, through a current that might send them anywhere.

"Aldric. Can you do anything about the current?"

Aldric leaned against the corridor wall. He was pale and breathing hard, the cost of the evening's casting written in the grey of his skin and the tremor in his hands. "I could try an Anchor. It's a Warding spell — stabilizes the local Aetheric field, creates a still point. But in this saturation, the cost would be —"

"Can you do it or can't you?"

Aldric straightened. Took off his spectacles, polished them, put them back on. The habitual gesture, the reset. When he spoke, his voice was steady. "I can hold it for about thirty seconds. Long enough for all of us to cross if we move quickly."

"Do it."

Aldric stepped to the atrium's edge. He took a breath, then another. Then he cast.

Sera felt it — the Anchor settling over the atrium like a hand pressed flat on churning water. The circular current in the room shuddered, resisted, and then stilled. The air went flat. The luminous veins dimmed, as if the structure itself was startled by the sudden calm.

Aldric made a sound through his teeth — not a word, just the sound of a man absorbing a cost he'd agreed to pay. His face went white. His hands locked at his sides.

"Go," he said.

Kael went first. Across the atrium floor, through air that was still and dead and tasted of nothing — the Anchor had pushed the Aether back, creating a pocket of something almost neutral in the heart of the saturated structure. The thirty feet took eight seconds. He reached the third corridor and turned.

"Clear. Move."

Mira grabbed Aldric's arm and pulled him forward. Sera followed, reaching back with one hand to steady Aldric when he stumbled. They crossed the atrium in a tight group, Aldric's face rigid with concentration, the Anchor holding, the veins in the walls flickering uncertainly as if the structure was trying to push back against the imposed stillness.

They reached the corridor. Aldric dropped the Anchor.

The effect was immediate. The circular current roared back into the atrium behind them — Sera felt it like a gust of wind, warm and dense, the Aether rushing to fill the space that Aldric's spell had emptied. The veins flared bright. The atrium hummed.

Aldric sat down on the corridor floor, hard. His nose was bleeding — a thin red line from his left nostril, tracking down to his upper lip. He wiped it with the back of his hand and stared at the blood with the expression of a man who knew exactly what it cost and was calculating whether the ledger balanced.

"Aldric." Kael was already coming back for him.

"I'm fine." He wasn't fine. But he was conscious, and he was moving when Kael pulled him to his feet. "The exit. Sera — the exit?"

"This way." Sera could feel it clearly now — the pull of open air, the Aetheric current flowing toward the outside, toward the forest, toward the world that made sense. "Close. Very close."

They ran. The corridor was short — shorter than it should have been, as if the structure was expelling them, the architecture compressing the distance between here and out. The pale light intensified, then shifted — from the cold blue-white of the interior to something warmer, greener, the light of the forest filtering through an opening ahead.

The last hundred feet were the strangest part. The corridor's walls lost their seamlessness in increments as they approached the outside — the pale remade material giving way, in patches, to hints of what had been underneath: a stretch of pitted concrete, a course of brick, a horizontal strip where a steel I-beam had once been embedded and was now only the ghost of one. The shell remembering itself, or the interior forgetting. They ran through the loading bay's rectangle of ground where wagons had once backed up to chutes, over floor that had briefly, impossibly, become a concrete apron with worn-down drainage grates, and then out.

The opening. The gap in the structure's surface where they'd entered, smooth-edged and wide — and framed, now that Kael saw it from the inside rushing out, by a mill's loading bay with its upper lintel still bearing the rust-streaked stub of what had once been a hoist mount. Beyond it, the clearing. Beyond the clearing, the trees. Beyond the trees, the forest, the Wilds, the world.

They stumbled through the opening and into the clearing, and the air hit Sera like a wall — warm, wet, saturated with the Aether of the living forest, so different from the stored, contained power inside the structure that she gasped.

Kael was counting heads before they'd all stopped moving. "Four. We're clear."

Aldric sat down on the warm ground and put his head between his knees. Mira stood at the edge of the clearing, looking back at the structure, her face unreadable. Sera turned to look too.

The mill sat in the clearing, pale and luminous and patient. In outline it was still a mill — the silos, the dock, the broken stub of smokestack. The openings in its surface were dark now — darker than before, as if the interior had dimmed after they left. The veins on the exterior pulsed slowly. The same rhythm as before. Slow as breathing. Slow as a heartbeat. Slow as the shift bell that had once called men to work on this ground, ringing on a schedule nobody alive remembered.

It had let them in. It had shown them what it held. And then it had rearranged itself around them and made them fight to leave.

Not hostile. Not helpful. Just *there*, being what it was, following rules that nobody alive could read.

Sera's hands were still trembling. Not from exhaustion. From the thing she hadn't told the others — that inside the chamber, when the container had pulsed and the resonance had struck her chest, something had answered. Something inside her, in the place where the Aether lived, had recognized the sealed thing in the chamber and had reached for it. Not her will. Not her casting. Something deeper, older, and entirely involuntary.

She hadn't reached the container. But something in her had wanted to, with a hunger that frightened her more than the shifting corridors or the impossible geometry or the fact that they'd nearly been sealed inside the remade guts of a building that had stopped, long ago, being the mill its shell still remembered.

She kept that to herself. She was getting good at that.
