# Chapter 3

The Wilds tasted like the Quarter on its best days, multiplied by ten, and then by something that didn't have a number.

Sera had grown up with the Aether as background noise — the faint copper sweetness that drifted east from the Veilwright Quarter when the wind was right, the prickle on her skin when she passed close to the zone boundary on an errand for her mother. She'd cast in moderate Aetheric areas, felt the power thicken around her hands like warm water, pulled threads of force from air that was rich with something she couldn't name but could always feel.

This was different. The forest beyond the tree line hummed. Not the mechanical hum of the Galvanic quarter — something lower, older, the way the earth might sound if you could press your ear to the bedrock and hear the world breathing. The air was heavy with it. Every breath tasted of copper and green things growing and something underneath both, something mineral and sweet, like the smell of rain on hot stone. The compass on her cord had stopped pretending to work. The needle pointed nowhere and everywhere, turning in slow circles as if confused by an abundance of direction.

The settlement appeared within an hour of entering the forest edge — a clearing where the canopy thinned enough to let in grey afternoon light. Someone had been working here with purpose. Trees had been felled and the stumps smoothed flat, some used as tables or seats. A cluster of buildings stood in a rough circle: four structures of salvaged timber and local stone, their roofs thatched with something that looked like dried fern but grew too thickly for any fern Sera had seen. Gardens flanked the nearest building — gardens that were indecent with growth. Tomato vines heavy with fruit that shouldn't exist in early spring. Herbs standing waist-high. A row of something flowering in deep violet, each bloom the size of Sera's palm.

"Aetheric cultivation," Aldric murmured beside her. "The zone promotes growth. If you know what to plant and how to tend it, you can produce year-round."

"It's beautiful," Sera said.

"It's also evidence of sustained moderate-to-strong Aetheric saturation. The growth patterns are consistent with —"

"It's *beautiful*, Aldric."

He adjusted his spectacles and didn't argue.

A woman met them at the clearing's edge. She was perhaps forty, lean and brown-skinned, her hair pulled back with a strip of cloth. Her hands were dirty from the garden. Her expression was clean: she'd seen them coming and she'd already decided how this would go.

"You're from the city," the woman said. Not a question.

"From Ashwick." Mira stepped forward, easy and open, the version of herself she used for people who had reason to distrust strangers. "My name is Mira Cade. We're Ash & Veil Recovery — an Adventuring Society."

"I know what a Society is." The woman's gaze moved across the four of them, pausing on Kael's holstered revolver and Aldric's satchel before settling on Sera. Something in her expression shifted — recognition, not of Sera specifically, but of what Sera was. The sensitivity. The way Sera was standing with her face slightly upturned, breathing the thick air like a person who'd been underwater and had finally surfaced. "And I know why you're here. Lenna talked."

"We're not here to take anything from your community," Mira said.

"No. You're here to take what's beyond it." The woman folded her arms. "I'm Pell. I speak for this settlement, inasmuch as anyone does. You're welcome to buy water and food. You're welcome to rest. You are not welcome to stay, and we won't guide you into the Wilds."

"We haven't asked for a guide."

"You were about to." Pell's mouth thinned. "Everyone asks. Everyone comes here with their commission papers and their patron money and assumes that because we found it, we owe them a path to it. We don't. We built this place for ourselves, not as a waystation for every faction in Ashwick to use when something shiny turns up in the forest."

Sera felt the tension between Mira's diplomacy and Pell's wall and knew which one would give first. Mira was adaptable. Pell was planted. You didn't uproot a woman like that with charm or money — she'd come to the edge of the Wilds on purpose, and she'd decided what she would and wouldn't give, and the decision was made before anyone from the city arrived to test it.

They bought food — dried fruit, bread dense with seeds, strips of smoked meat that Pell sold at a price that communicated contempt for city economics. The settlement's other residents watched from doorways and garden rows. A man splitting wood stopped and leaned on his axe. Two children sat on a smoothed stump and stared with the unblinking focus that children had before they learned to pretend they weren't looking. An older man by the well raised a hand to Mira — a greeting or a warning, hard to tell.

Sera drifted toward the settlement's eastern edge while the others negotiated provisions. She couldn't help it. The Aether pulled at her the way gravity pulled at water, and the strongest current was east, deeper into the forest. Standing at the last building's corner, she could see where the clearing ended and the Wilds resumed. The trees here were enormous — trunks wide enough that two people couldn't reach around them, bark silvered and smooth, branches starting thirty feet up and spreading into a canopy that filtered the light into something green and luminous. The undergrowth between the trunks was thick with ferns and moss and something that glowed faintly even in daylight, a pale blue-white luminescence that pulsed so slowly you could mistake it for a trick of the light.

It was terrifying. It was the most beautiful thing she'd ever seen.

She stood there until her hands stopped shaking. Not from cold. From the effort of not reaching for the power that saturated the air around her like heat from an open furnace. It would be so easy here. She could feel it — the threads of force that she usually had to search for, thin and elusive in the moderate zones of Ashwick, were thick as rope here. They wound through the air, through the ground, through the trees. If she reached out, they would come to her. They would come eagerly. They would give her more than she could hold.

That was the part that frightened her.

"It's a lot, isn't it?"

Sera startled. The older man from the well had walked up beside her without her noticing — which was itself a sign of how thoroughly the Aether had absorbed her attention. He was perhaps sixty, weathered, with the deep-set eyes of someone who'd spent years squinting into things that weren't entirely visible.

"You can feel it," he said. Not asking.

"Yes."

"How long?"

"Since I was a child. It's never been — it's never been like this."

The old man nodded. He looked east, into the forest, with an expression that was equal parts affection and caution. The way a fisherman looked at the sea. "We came here because it feels like home. Most of us — the ones who have the sense for it. The city was too thin. Too much hum and metal and the sweetness all scraped away by the factories. Here, the air is right." He paused. "The ones who went deeper said the air gets more than right. They said it gets loud."

"Loud?"

"Their word, not mine. I didn't go with them." He scratched his jaw. "They were gone for — well, they said two days. Might have been more. Might have been less. Time's funny past the second ridge. You'll find that out, if you go in."

He looked at her again, and his expression was the same one Pell had worn — recognition of what she was, and something else. Sympathy, maybe. Or concern.

"The young woman who went the farthest — she has your look. The way you stand in the air. She came back quieter than she left. Not hurt. Just — listening to something the rest of us couldn't hear."

He walked away without offering more. Sera watched him go, then turned back to the forest. The luminescence in the undergrowth pulsed, slow as a sleeping heartbeat. The trees swayed in a wind she couldn't feel on her skin.

Somewhere in there, past the second ridge, past the point where time stopped being reliable, something waited in a structure that no one had built. She was going to find it. The knowledge sat in her chest like a coal, warm and certain.

She went back to the others. Mira was talking with Kael in low tones. Aldric was writing in his notebook, sketching something from memory — the child's drawing he'd noticed on the wall of one of the buildings, angular lines that looked like architecture.

"They won't guide us," Mira said as Sera approached.

"I know." Sera looked east. "I can feel the way."

Mira studied her for a moment — the assessing look, the one that weighed what you were offering against what it would cost. Then she nodded.

"We leave at dawn."
