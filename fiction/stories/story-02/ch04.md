# Chapter 4

Aldric catalogued the settlement the way he'd catalogued everything since he was sixteen years old: systematically, completely, and with a persistent anxiety that he was missing the detail that mattered most.

The buildings were well-made. Not professionally constructed — there was no architect's hand in the layout, no surveyor's line in the foundations — but built with the practical competence of people who had done hard work before and understood that a wall needed to be plumb and a roof needed to shed rain. The timber was local, the stone gathered from a streambed nearby, the thatch a variety of bracken fern that Aldric didn't recognize from any botanical text but that clearly thrived in the Aetheric saturation. The gardens were extraordinary. Vitae theory predicted accelerated growth in sustained Aetheric environments, but seeing it in practice — tomatoes fruiting in early spring, herbs growing waist-high, flowers blooming in colors that seemed slightly too saturated to be natural — was something else entirely.

He noted all of this. He also noted what the settlement was missing. No Galvanic equipment of any kind. No electric lights, no mechanical tools more complex than a hand saw and a hammer, no sign of any technology that might push back against the Aetheric environment. These people had committed fully. They'd walked away from everything that hummed with the Engine's resonance and built with their hands and whatever the forest provided.

The evening meal was communal — a long table between two buildings, lit by candles that burned with a steady, too-bright flame. The Sootborn ate together but didn't invite the Society to join. Pell brought them food separately: bowls of stew, thick bread, water from the well that tasted faintly sweet. Professional hospitality. Not warmth.

Aldric ate slowly, watching. The research instinct was difficult to suppress, and tonight he didn't try.

The child's drawing was the first piece. He'd noticed it on the wall of the second building while Mira was talking with Pell — charcoal on rough timber, the angular lines of a shape that was neither house nor tree. It had corners. It had what might have been corridors, rendered with the confident imprecision of a child drawing something they'd seen described rather than something they'd witnessed. The proportions were wrong in a way that nagged at Aldric. Too many right angles. No curves at all.

The second piece came from the communal table. Two women were talking — quietly, but sound carried in the clear air, and Aldric had spent twelve years in academic settings where the most important conversations happened just below conversational volume. One of them mentioned "the clearing past the second ridge." The other made a gesture — a flattening motion with her hand, palm down, as if pressing something shut. Then she noticed Aldric watching and the conversation stopped.

The third piece was the well.

He'd gone to refill his water flask after the meal and found the old man sitting on the stone lip, smoking a pipe that smelled of something herbal and sharp. The old man — Aldric had heard someone call him Thom — regarded him with the quiet patience of a person who expected to be asked questions and had already decided which ones to answer.

"You're the scholar," Thom said.

"I was. I'm with the Society now."

"Same thing with a different coat." Thom drew on his pipe. "You want to know about the place they found."

"If you're willing to tell me."

"I'm willing to tell you what I know, which is less than you want and more than Pell would like." He exhaled smoke that curled upward and seemed to hang too long in the heavy air. "Five of them went in. Explorers — the younger ones, the ones who wanted to see how far the forest went. They were mapping, or trying to. Pell's idea, originally. Know your territory. Good sense."

"How far did they get?"

Thom's expression shifted, and Aldric recognized the look — the face of someone trying to convey an experience that resisted language. "That's the question, isn't it. They said two days. They were gone for — well, my count said three sunrises, but the sun does things in there. Rises in the wrong place. Sets too fast. They packed for a week and came back with most of their food uneaten, which means either they weren't gone as long as they thought or they weren't hungry." He scratched his chin. "Adi — the one with the strongest sense, the young woman — she said the distances were wrong. You could see a ridge and walk toward it for an hour and it wouldn't get closer. Then you'd turn around and it'd be right behind you."

"Spatial distortion," Aldric said. "Consistent with deep Aetheric saturation. The literature describes —"

"I don't care what the literature describes." Thom said it without heat. "I care that five people I know went into the forest healthy and came back strange. Not hurt. Strange. Quieter. Like they'd seen something they were still deciding how to talk about."

Aldric closed his mouth, waited, and listened. It was, he'd found, the most productive research technique available.

"They found a clearing," Thom continued. "Past the second ridge — which doesn't mean what it sounds like, because the ridges move, or the paths do, or something does. The clearing was real. Solid ground, flat, like something had been built there and then unmade. And in the clearing there was a structure."

"What kind of structure?"

"The kind that doesn't belong." Thom looked east, toward the dark tree line. "Adi said it was like a building, but not a building. Made of something smooth. No seams, no joints. Like it was poured or grown, not built. She said it had — she called them hallways. Openings that led inside. They went in."

"How far?"

"Far enough to find something they couldn't take. A room with an object in it — sealed, she said. Sealed behind something she couldn't touch, couldn't push through, couldn't cast through. She said the building changed around them while they were inside. Not fast. Slow, like the walls were breathing. They came out when the way back started looking different from the way in."

Aldric's hand was shaking, and it took him a moment to realize he'd been gripping his pencil so tightly that his knuckles had gone white. He relaxed his fingers and wrote, the letters precise and small, filling the margins of a page that already held observations about the settlement's garden rotation.

"One more thing," he said. "Can you see the structure from here?"

Thom pointed east, toward a low rise at the settlement's edge where the tree line climbed. "Eastern ridge. On a clear morning, before the canopy mist comes in. Adi showed me once through a spyglass. You can see it — a shape in the trees, lighter than the forest around it. Looks close." He drew on his pipe. "It's not."

---

The eastern ridge was a ten-minute walk from the settlement, and Aldric made it in the grey light before dawn. Kael came with him, carrying the binoculars without needing to be asked.

The ridge was modest — a hump of exposed stone above the tree line, offering a view east over the canopy. The forest stretched to the horizon, a sea of green touched with the faint luminescence that seemed to live in the deeper foliage. Mist hung in the hollows between the ridges — and there were ridges, three or four visible from here, rising like waves in the forest floor.

Kael raised the binoculars and scanned east. He was quiet for a long time.

"There," he said, and handed them over.

Aldric looked. Past the first ridge, past the second — or what might have been the second; the ridges blurred together at distance — a shape broke the canopy. Lighter than the surrounding forest, angular where the trees were curved, catching the early light with a faint gleam that could have been stone or metal or something else. It was small at this distance. A cluster of forms, regular and geometric, sitting in the forest like a dropped puzzle piece.

He estimated the distance. Two miles. Perhaps three. An easy walk on flat ground in normal conditions.

Thom's words: *Looks close. It's not.*

"How far?" Kael asked.

"Visually, two to three miles. The Sootborn explorers took what they described as two days to reach it, though their sense of elapsed time is unreliable." Aldric lowered the binoculars. "The spatial distortion in deep Aetheric zones doesn't follow consistent rules. The distance might be functionally ten miles. It might be fifty. It might be two miles that simply takes two days to cross because the space between here and there isn't behaving."

"Can we do it?"

Aldric considered lying. Considered softening the uncertainty, presenting a probability assessment that would make the enterprise sound more calculated and less like a guess. Decided against it, because Kael had asked him a straight question and Kael deserved a straight answer.

"I don't know," he said. "Sera can navigate by Aetheric sense, which is the most reliable method in these conditions. We have landmarks that may or may not hold true. We have a general direction. We don't have a route, a timeline, or any certainty about what we'll find when we get there."

"But you want to go."

"I've spent twelve years studying zones from the outside. Measuring drift patterns. Analyzing data that other people collected." Aldric looked east, at the shape in the trees that shouldn't exist, and felt something he wasn't accustomed to feeling: a desire that had nothing to do with methodology. "Yes. I want to go."

Kael nodded. He took the binoculars back and looked east one more time, and Aldric could see him doing what he always did — reading the terrain, calculating the risks, deciding whether the people behind him would survive what was ahead of them.

"Then we go," Kael said. "Let's tell the others."
