# Chapter 1

Corrigan Fels walked into the Wet Ember like a man who owned the place and was considering selling.

Kael saw him in the mirror behind the bar — a trick of angles he'd mapped his first week as a regular, Hesper's bottles arranged just so, giving him a view of the door without turning his head. Fels came through it wearing a good coat and an expression of mild interest, as if he'd wandered in by accident and was pleasantly surprised by what he'd found. He ordered a drink from Hesper with a smile that she did not return. He paid with a coin that was too large for the tab and didn't ask for change.

Kael's hand moved under the table to the revolver on his thigh. Not drawing. Resting.

"Don't," Mira said quietly, without looking up from her drink. She'd seen it too — not in the mirror. Mira didn't need mirrors. She read rooms the way weather readers watched clouds, and something in the barometric pressure had just dropped.

Fels collected his drink and walked past their table without acknowledgment. He took the booth behind them, settling into the corner seat with his back to the wall — Kael's preferred position, which meant Fels had either cased the place in advance or had the same instincts. Neither option was comforting.

The Wet Ember went on around them. Two factory workers from the Galvanic district were arguing about the prior season's boxing standings near the door, one of them gesturing with a beer that sloshed on the table with each emphatic point. A woman in a frayed shawl was reading a broadsheet by the coal fire, her lips moving silently. Hesper polished a glass with her one good arm and watched the room with the flat attention of a woman who'd seen everything at least twice.

Normal evening. Except for the man in the booth behind them who'd disrupted a Syndicate operation during the Ashwick Job and had apparently decided to discuss it in person, three months later, at their local tavern.

"I think," Fels said, to the air behind their heads, "that we should have a conversation."

Kael didn't turn. Mira did — slowly, one arm draped over the back of her chair, her posture suggesting she'd been expecting this. She hadn't. But Mira's talent was making improvisation look like preparation.

"Mr. Fels." Her voice was pleasant and measured and gave away nothing. "You're a long way from the docks."

"I'm a long way from a lot of things." Fels sipped his drink. "The job your people did for the Greycoats four months ago — the missing researcher, the foundry tunnels. You disrupted a shipping route I spent three years building. Cost me product, money, and a relationship with a supplier who doesn't extend second chances." He smiled. The smile didn't reach past his mouth. "I've been thinking about that."

"We were under contract," Kael said, still facing forward. "The job intersected your operation by coincidence."

"Coincidence." Fels tasted the word like he was testing a vintage. "That's one interpretation. The other interpretation is that a four-person Society with more ambition than sense blundered into a carefully managed enterprise and set fire to it on their way out. The interpretation doesn't much matter. The cost is the same."

The room noise continued around them. The boxing argument reached a point of shouted agreement. Hesper set down a glass and picked up another. Nobody was paying attention to the quiet conversation at the back of the room, which was exactly how conversations like this were meant to go.

Fels produced a thin cigar and lit it with a match struck on the underside of the table. The smell — clove and something darker, imported — reached Kael a moment later. "I know about the mill," Fels said. "I know you went into the Wilds on a Covenant commission and came back with information that several factions would pay handsomely for. I know Inspector Laine has been your contact at the Greycoat Authority, and I know what you told her about the foundry tunnels doesn't match what you actually found down there." He drew on the cigar and exhaled a thin stream of smoke toward the ceiling. "I know the Ashworth Collective tried to kill you in the forest, and I know you didn't report it to anyone who could act on it."

Kael felt the temperature of the conversation change. Not a threat yet. The inventory before the invoice.

"I'm not here to collect on a debt," Fels continued. "You don't owe me anything, in any traditional sense. But I have information about your operations that would interest people you can't afford to have interested. The Ashworth Collective. Ironwork Solutions. Others." He tapped ash from the cigar into the tray on his table. "I'm here to offer you a job. The compensation is that I don't sell what I know to the people who would use it worst."

Mira's expression hadn't changed. Kael could see enough of her profile to read the calculation happening behind it — not whether Fels was bluffing (he wasn't), but what the response options were and which ones left the Society standing when the dust settled.

"What's the job?" she asked.

"Something went missing in the tunnels beneath the Ashworth Foundry. The same tunnels your people walked through during the Harker business. A transport case — sealed, marked, part of a shipment we were moving through the old drainage route. Three weeks ago, the route became impassable. The conditions down there have intensified past anything our runners can handle. Passages that were open have closed. The environment is —" he paused, selecting a word with the care of a man who understood that words were currency — "hostile. We sent people down. They came back with headaches and stories I don't pay them to tell me."

"And you want us to go get it," Kael said.

"You have two people who can handle what's down there. We don't. It's a straightforward retrieval — go in, find the case, bring it back. I'll provide the access point and what intelligence we have about the current conditions." Fels stubbed out the cigar, half-smoked. "When the case is delivered, I'll share something you need: intelligence on the Ashworth Collective's plans regarding the mill site. They're not done with you, and what they're planning next is considerably more ambitious than the last time."

The carrot and the stick, laid on the table together. Professional. Clean. The kind of offer that only sounded reasonable because the alternative was worse.

Kael looked at the mirror behind the bar. Fels's reflection looked back at him — a pleasant face, a good coat, the calm of a man who'd come to deliver terms, not negotiate them.

"I'll tell you something for free," Fels said, standing. He left his glass half-finished — a man who'd said what he came to say and didn't linger. "I don't make threats I can't carry out, and I don't make offers I can't fulfill. You have until tomorrow evening. Send word to the usual channels."

He walked past their table again, not looking at them. The door opened. The factory noise from the street entered and left with him.

The booth behind them was empty. The thin smell of clove smoke lingered.

Kael looked at Mira. She was already thinking three moves ahead — he could see it in the set of her jaw, the way her fingers turned her glass in slow quarter-rotations on the table. Sera and Aldric weren't here tonight — Sera was at the flat working on her journal, Aldric at the public library chasing a citation he'd been muttering about for a week. They'd need to be told. They'd need to decide.

But the decision was already narrowing. Kael could feel it — the way a room narrows when someone bars the exits. Fels hadn't asked. He'd informed. And the information he carried about the Society's operations was a loaded weapon pointed at everything they'd built.

"He's not bluffing," Kael said.

"No," Mira agreed. "He's not."

The coal fire cracked. Hesper served a drink to the woman with the broadsheet. The boxing argument wound down into companionable disagreement. The Wet Ember went on, the way taverns do, indifferent to the small catastrophes happening at the tables in the back.

Kael picked up his drink and found it had gone warm. He drank it anyway. The evening was going to be long, and the taste didn't matter.
