# Chapter 2

The argument started before Mira finished the briefing.

"No." Kael said it flat, the way he said things that weren't negotiable. He was standing by the window with his arms crossed, silhouetted against the grey morning light. He'd been standing since Mira started talking, which meant he'd already decided and was just waiting for his turn to say so. "We're not Syndicate contractors. We don't do jobs for people who open the conversation with blackmail."

"It's not that simple," Mira said.

"It is exactly that simple. This is the line I left the Greycoats to avoid crossing."

Aldric was at the kitchen table with his Ashwick Job notebook open in front of him, the pages covered in his small, precise handwriting — measurements, observations, questions with no answers. He'd been turning pages since Mira mentioned the tunnels. "The conditions Fels described," he said, not looking up. "If the Aetheric activity beneath the foundry has intensified to the point where trained Syndicate runners can't handle it — these are people accustomed to navigating moderate zone environments as part of their trade — that represents a significant escalation from what we documented during the Harker investigation." He turned another page. "The crystalline formations. The ambient luminescence. The readings I took were anomalous but explicable within the existing framework. If those same formations have grown dense enough to close passages and create hostile conditions, we're looking at something comparable to the Wild edges." He paused. "I'd want to go down there regardless of who was asking."

Sera was sitting on the floor near the cold fireplace, her knees drawn up, her compass hanging from its cord around her neck. She hadn't spoken during Mira's account. She was looking at the compass, which was doing the thing it did when she was lost in thought — spinning in slow, lazy rotations that had nothing to do with magnetic north.

"Sera," Mira said.

"I felt something down there." Sera's voice was quiet. Not reluctant — sorting. "The first time, during the Harker job. In the tunnels. A resonance. I didn't understand it then and I've been thinking about it for months and I still don't understand it, but I want to go back." She looked up. "I can't tell you if that's curiosity or if it's the Aether pulling. The distinction might not matter."

Four positions. Four different calculations. Mira weighed them the way she weighed everything — not by who was right, but by what the team could survive.

"Here's what I know," she said. "Fels has information about our operations that he can sell to the Ashworth Collective, Ironwork, or anyone else who'd pay for leverage against a Society that's gotten more visible than we can afford. If we refuse the job, he sells. If we accept, we do a retrieval under duress for a criminal enterprise." She kept her voice level. This was the part that mattered — not the moral argument, which had no clean answer, but the operational reality. "Either way, we take damage. I'm choosing the damage I can manage."

Kael's jaw tightened. He didn't argue further, which was worse than arguing — it meant he'd accepted a thing he hated, and the acceptance would cost him in ways that wouldn't show until later.

Aldric closed his notebook. "What do we know about the current conditions?"

"Fels's intelligence is three weeks old and comes from runners who couldn't handle what they found. The access point is the same storm drain we used during the Harker job — west side of the foundry, behind the industrial debris." Mira pulled a chair from the table and sat. "The foundry is still decommissioned. Still in Galvanic territory — moderate zone. The tunnels beneath it showed early-stage Aetheric bleed the last time we were there. If conditions have intensified past what the Syndicate can manage, we should expect something comparable to a moderate-to-strong Aetheric environment. Underground. Confined. No retreat options if the environment shifts against us."

Aldric took off his spectacles and polished them — the processing gesture. "Then we should discuss resources." He put the spectacles back on and looked at the team with the expression of a man delivering clinical information. "I can sustain perhaps six or seven casts before I'm working on fumes. Healing is expensive — if someone is badly hurt, a proper Mend will take most of what I have left in a single effort. My detection and shielding are cheaper, but they still draw from the same well." He tapped the table once, the way he did when he wanted a point to land. "I mention this so that everyone understands: magic is a finite supply. I will use it where it matters most, and I will be making judgments about what matters most in real time. Those judgments may not always match what feels urgent in the moment."

"Sera," Mira said. "Your reserves?"

Sera tilted her head, considering. "Deeper than his. But less predictable. I burn hotter and I can't always choose how much I spend." The honesty was characteristic — Sera didn't pretend her path gave her control it didn't. "Between us, we have enough for a careful expedition. Not a prolonged one. Not a messy one."

"Then we don't make it messy," Mira said. "In, retrieve the case, out. Aldric documents what he can without stopping to study it. Kael marks our route. Sera navigates if the conditions require it. I'll handle the handoff when we surface."

She stood. The decision was made. The part that came next was the part she didn't share.

Because Mira had edited the briefing.

Not dramatically. Not dishonestly, exactly. She'd conveyed the substance of Fels's proposition — the job, the blackmail, the carrot of Collective intelligence. But she'd softened the edges. The blackmail, in her telling, was a negotiation with teeth. A trade they'd chosen to accept because the alternatives were worse. She hadn't described Fels's tone — the pleasant, professional fury that made the offer feel less like a business proposition and more like a hand closing around their throat. She hadn't repeated his specific inventory of what he knew, the granular detail that proved his information was current and comprehensive.

She'd done this deliberately. The team would perform better believing they had agency in the decision. Morale matters, and morale comes from feeling like you chose your situation rather than being cornered into it. If they thought Mira had negotiated from a position of limited options, they'd operate with confidence. If they knew the Syndicate had them by the throat, they'd operate with fear.

It was the right call. She was almost certain it was the right call.

Kael was watching her. Not obviously — he was checking his revolver, the habitual disassembly-and-reassembly that was to Kael what polishing spectacles was to Aldric. But his eyes moved to her face during the briefing at the moments where her account diverged from what he'd heard Fels say at the Wet Ember. He'd been there. He'd heard the original. He knew what she'd changed.

He didn't call it. His hands moved through the familiar motions of the revolver's mechanism — cylinder out, check chambers, cylinder in, action smooth — and his face gave away nothing except the faint tightness at the corners of his mouth that Mira had learned to read as disagreement held in reserve.

She knew he'd noticed. He knew she knew. The silence between them held the shape of a conversation they weren't having, and the conversation was about whether managing your team's information was the same thing as lying to them.

"Gear check," Mira said. "Lanterns, rope, chalk for marking. Kael, your usual kit. Aldric, field notebook and medical supplies. Sera —"

"I know what I need," Sera said. She was already on her feet, the compass hanging still against her chest, pointing somewhere that wasn't quite north. Her eyes had the brightness they got when the Aether was calling — not the glow, not yet, but the alertness of someone hearing a frequency that nobody else could detect.

They were going back to the foundry. Back to the tunnels. Back underground, where the rules were different and the walls were growing.

Mira opened the Ledger and looked at the numbers. The Covenant payment from the mill job was keeping them solvent — another two months, maybe three if she was careful. Fels wasn't paying in marks. He was paying in information and the absence of catastrophe, which didn't balance a book but kept you alive to balance the next one.

She closed the Ledger. Put it back on the shelf. Picked up her coat.

The flat settled into the focused quiet of preparation — the same choreography as before the mill expedition, the same economy of motion from four people who'd learned to pack for danger without discussing it. But the quality of the silence was different this time. Before the mill, they'd been nervous and excited. Before the tunnels, they were resigned.

Mira didn't let herself think about whether resignation was worse.
