# Chapter 7

Mira chose the coffee house. Not the Wet Ember — the Wet Ember was theirs, and she didn't want Fels in it again. A place in Central instead, where the Greycoat Authority's presence kept the streets civil and where businessmen met to discuss arrangements that needed witnesses but not listeners. Tile floor, zinc counter, the smell of roasted beans and tobacco smoke and the faint mineral sharpness of the city water they used for the press. Normal. Bright. The kind of place where the worst thing that happened was an overcharge on the bill.

Fels was there when they arrived. Just Mira and Kael — Aldric and Sera stayed at the flat. Aldric was writing, his research journal open on the kitchen table, the pen moving with the focused intensity of a man assembling a puzzle from pieces that didn't quite fit. Sera was sleeping. She'd been sleeping for fourteen hours when Kael left, and Aldric had checked on her twice — pulse, pupils, the signs he knew to look for when a caster had spent too much. Not in danger, he'd said. Just empty in a way that sleep slowly refilled. Like a well that needed time and stillness to rise back to its level.

Fourteen hours. The cost of the barrier, the Kinesis casts, the artifact's touch, and the zone transition on exit. Kael had watched her walk back to the flat from the foundry, moving with the careful deliberation of someone navigating ice — each step considered, each motion managed, the body's reserves so depleted that even walking required conscious supervision. She'd made it to her bed and been asleep in minutes. The deepest sleep he'd seen from her. Not peaceful — heavy. The sleep of a body rebuilding what the magic had taken.

Kael checked his revolver at the coffee house door. The habit — reach, feel, assess — and this time the result was different from the tunnels. The mechanism snapped with its usual precision. The cylinder spun clean. The action was crisp and authoritative, the weapon answering his hand the way it always had.

He stood in the doorway for a moment, processing. The revolver worked. Down in the tunnels it hadn't — not jammed, not broken, but sluggish, uncertain, the machinery of it somehow dampened by the same force that had fed Sera's magic and pushed against Aldric's control. Up here, in the Galvanic-leaning air of Central, the weapon was itself again. The engineering the city ran on — the arc lamps, the trams, the factories — was feeding the same physics his revolver depended on.

He'd always known, abstractly, that magic and machinery didn't coexist comfortably. But knowing it as a fact in a briefing document was different from feeling your weapon go reluctant in your hand, from trusting a thing you'd trusted for seven years and having that trust answered with a hesitation. His revolver was environment-dependent. In the same way Sera's casting was environment-dependent. The difference was that Sera knew it — lived with it, navigated around it, accepted it as the cost of her gift. Kael was only now understanding that the cost ran in both directions.

He walked in. The revolver sat on his hip, working and solid and his. He kept his hand near it anyway.

---

Fels sat at a corner table with his thin cigar and a cup of black coffee, and he looked like any prosperous tradesman meeting associates for a morning appointment. His coat was good. His collar was clean. He rose half an inch when Mira approached — a gesture of politeness so minimal it could have been an accident, except nothing Fels did was accidental.

"Ms. Cade. Mr. Dunn." He gestured at the two chairs opposite. The cigar made a small circle in the air — an invitation that was also a choreography. Fels managed physical space the way Mira managed information: every gesture calibrated, every angle chosen.

They sat. Mira ordered coffee. Kael ordered nothing.

"The case was delivered," Fels said. Not a question. He already knew — his crew would have reported within the hour. "My people confirm the seals were intact. I'm satisfied."

He didn't ask what was inside. He didn't ask what they'd seen in the tunnels. The omissions were loud, and Kael heard them clearly. Fels already knew what the case contained — he'd been moving it through those tunnels on purpose — and he didn't want to know what the Society had learned, because knowledge shared was leverage lost. What the Society carried in their heads was their problem. Fels had gotten what he'd paid for.

"The intelligence," Mira said. "As agreed."

Fels tapped ash from his cigar. "The Ashworth Collective is assembling a second expedition to the mill site. Not Ironwork alone — they've contracted two additional Societies, freelance outfits with no patron ties. Hired muscle. The expedition is planned for within the month." He said this the way he said everything — pleasantly, precisely, as if discussing commodity prices. "They intend to breach the mill's defenses with industrial-grade equipment. Field dampeners. Resonance disruptors. Portable dead-zone generators. Heavy tools designed to suppress Aetheric activity by force." He drew on the cigar. "The Collective's working theory is that the artifact inside the mill is Engine-origin. A wild Galvanic manifestation, not Aetheric. Their research division wants it badly enough to militarize the retrieval."

Mira absorbed this without visible reaction, which meant she was reacting extensively behind the mask. Kael watched Fels.

"The artifact in the case," Kael said. "The one you were moving through the tunnels. What is it?"

Fels's smile didn't change. It was a good smile — warm, collegial, the kind of smile that worked at dinner parties and business negotiations and across the table from people you were systematically manipulating. "You completed the job. I've paid the agreed price. Everything else is above your commission." He tapped ash again. The gesture was precise enough to be a punctuation mark. "I'll tell you this for free, because I like you: stop asking about the tunnels. What's down there isn't something you want to be curious about. Not because it's dangerous — because the people who are already curious about it don't share well."

The warning was genuine. Kael recognized it the way he recognized all genuine warnings — by the moment when the performance dropped. Fels's smile thinned. His eyes, which had been doing the warm-and-collegial thing since they'd sat down, went flat for half a second. The real person showed through the surface like a rock showing through shallow water, and the real person was not smiling.

He wasn't protecting the Syndicate's interests. He was protecting the Society from the Syndicate's interests. The distinction mattered, and Kael filed it in the place where he kept the small, important things that people revealed about themselves without meaning to.

Fels finished his coffee. He stood. He left money on the table — enough for their drinks as well, a courtesy that was also a statement about the power dynamic. "A pleasure," he said, and meant it in the way that men like Fels meant things: partially, strategically, and with an awareness that the next meeting might go differently.

He left. The cigar smell lingered. The tile floor was clean and bright under the morning light from the windows.

---

Kael drank the coffee Fels had paid for. It was good coffee. Central had good coffee, the way Central had good everything — the Galvanic infrastructure kept the district running smoothly, the businesses prosperous, the streets well-lit. The hum was constant here, a background frequency that most people didn't notice and Kael had learned to read the way he read rooms. The hum said: *this is where the power is. This is where the machinery works. This is where the world runs on rules you can trust.*

He'd done the job. He'd worked for the Syndicate. The line he'd drawn when he left the Greycoats — the line between what he was willing to do for a paycheck and what he wasn't — had bent. Not broken. Bent. And the bending bothered him more than the breaking would have, because a man who broke knew where he stood. A man who bent had to wonder how far he'd go before he straightened. How many more times the line would flex before it stopped being a line and became a suggestion.

The coffee tasted like coffee. The world didn't end when you compromised. That was the worst part — the ordinariness of it. The way the morning went on, the trams running, the merchants opening their shops, the Greycoats walking their beats, and nothing in the world acknowledging that Kael Dunn had crossed a line he'd sworn not to cross, for reasons that were defensible, in service of people he cared about. The universe didn't care about your moral architecture. It just asked you what you'd do next.

Mira was watching him over the rim of her cup. Waiting. She knew what was coming — she always knew, which was part of the problem.

"You edited the briefing," Kael said. "Yesterday morning. You left out the part where he threatened us."

She didn't flinch. Didn't look away. Mira met confrontation the way she met everything — directly, with the composure of a woman who'd rehearsed every possible version of every possible conversation and was now performing the one she'd prepared for.

"I managed it," she said.

"You filtered it."

"That's the same thing."

"No," Kael said. "It isn't."

The silence between them had a shape. It was the shape of every conversation they hadn't had since the Wet Ember — every moment Kael had watched Mira smooth the edges off a hard truth, every time he'd noticed the gap between what happened and what she reported, every instance where her talent for managing information had slid from asset to liability and back again so quickly that calling it out would have felt petty. It wasn't petty now. It was a pattern, and patterns had consequences, and the consequence of this one was that the team was operating on a version of reality that Mira had curated for them without their knowledge or consent.

He wasn't angry. That was the complicated part. He understood why she'd done it. The team performed better believing they had agency. Morale mattered. Mira's instinct to protect the people around her by controlling what they knew was not malice — it was care, expressed through the only method she trusted. She was good at it. She'd been doing it their entire partnership.

He just needed her to know that he saw it.

Mira set down her cup. "The team needed to function. They needed to feel like this was a decision, not a corner. I made a judgment call about what information served that purpose."

"And you didn't think I'd notice."

"I knew you'd notice." A pause. Measured, the way all of Mira's pauses were measured. "I also knew you'd understand why."

"Understanding isn't the same as agreeing."

She looked at him for a long moment. The coffee house noise filled the space between them — the clink of cups, the murmur of conversations that were not about trust, the hiss of the espresso machine. Normal sounds in a normal place where two people were establishing, for the first time, the precise location of a crack that had been forming since before either of them had names for it.

"I'll keep doing it," Mira said. Not a challenge. A statement of fact, delivered with the honesty that was its own kind of cruelty. "Not because I don't respect you. Because I think it works."

"I know," Kael said. "That's the part that worries me."

Neither of them was wrong. That was the thing about the crack — it wasn't a disagreement about facts. It was a disagreement about methods, and methods lived in the territory where reasonable people could reach different conclusions from the same evidence and both be justified. Mira managed information because management was how she kept people alive. Kael demanded transparency because transparency was how he kept himself honest. These two positions could coexist. They just couldn't coexist comfortably, and the discomfort would grow.

Kael drained his cup. Mira left hers half-full. The money Fels had put on the table was still there, held down by the rim of the saucer — enough for their drinks and a generous tip, an accounting that had no further need of them. They stood. They walked out into the Central morning and back toward Harrier Street, side by side, two people whose trust had not been broken but had been named — and naming a thing, Kael knew, was the first step in deciding what to do with it.

The Galvanic hum from the west. The Aetheric sweetness from the east. The gradient between them, running beneath their feet. And beneath that, deeper still, in tunnels that used to be tunnels and were becoming something else, the luminous veins pulsed in a rhythm that nobody above ground was counting.
