# Chapter 6

The vision was already dissolving.

Sera held onto it the way you hold a dream after waking — cupping it in her mind, trying to see the details before they ran through her fingers like water. She'd seen the tunnels as they would be, not as they were: fully transformed, the ivory corridors extending and branching and connecting, spreading beneath the city like root systems beneath a forest. A network. The growth was directed, each branch reaching toward the others, the convergence points bright with the same bone-bright threads that pulsed in the walls around her.

And at the center — not a center, exactly, but the place all the branches were reaching toward — something deep and vast and patient that she couldn't see clearly. A presence that wasn't a presence. A purpose that she couldn't name. The same quality Aldric had described in the mill, the sense of something made but not by hands, except vaster and older and operating on a timescale that made human planning look like a child drawing in sand.

The details were going. She tried to hold the shape — the network, the convergence, the direction — but the specifics blurred even as she reached for them. What remained was an impression. A sense of scale. And the knowledge, certain as gravity, that what she'd seen was not a possibility but a trajectory. The tunnels were growing toward something. They had been for a very long time.

She kept it to herself.

Not because she wanted to — the instinct to share was strong, the way it always was when she experienced something that rearranged her understanding of the world. Aldric would want to know. He'd want details, observations, the kind of precise description she wasn't sure she could give. Kael would want to know because knowing was how he protected people. Mira would want to know because information was how she protected everyone else.

But she couldn't describe what she'd seen without first understanding it herself, and she didn't understand. The network, the convergence, the presence at the center — these were words she was attaching to sensations that didn't have words yet. If she tried to explain now, in the tunnels, shaking and depleted, she'd get it wrong. She'd reduce something vast to something small, and the small version would become the team's understanding, and the team's understanding would be insufficient.

She also didn't want to see the look on Kael's face when she told him the tunnels were becoming something, and she'd looked into the becoming, and it had looked back.

---

Something had changed.

The change was in her, not the tunnels. The moment she'd ripped herself free from the artifact, the tearing sensation, the violence of the separation — it had left a mark. Nothing physical. It had nothing to do with the exhaustion, or the eye-glow, or the trembling that was finally, slowly, fading from her hands. Something in how she felt the magic.

Before the artifact, reaching for the Aether was instinct. Natural as breathing. The river was always there and she swam in it without thinking about the current, without considering where it went or what lived in the deep. She reached and it answered and the conversation was simple and familiar and hers.

Now she thought about the current.

Now she knew the river went somewhere, and the somewhere was real, and it had noticed her. The vast thing at the convergence point — the presence that wasn't a presence — it hadn't been hostile. It hadn't been welcoming. It had been *aware*, in a way that was neither human nor inhuman but simply other, and the awareness had touched her as casually as she might brush her hand across a wall in passing. The touch had carried the vision. The vision had carried the scale. And the scale had shown her exactly how small she was in relation to what she was swimming in.

The river had a riptide. She'd always known that, abstractly. Now she knew it the way you knew a stove was hot after you'd touched it.

It didn't stop her from casting. But there was a beat now, between the impulse and the reach. A breath. A moment of conscious choice that hadn't been there before, when reaching had been as automatic as blinking. She hated it and was grateful for it in equal measure.

---

The tunnels had shifted.

Subtly. Nothing like the mill's full architectural rearrangement, the corridors reshuffling while you walked them. But enough. A passage they'd come through was narrower, its walls perceptibly closer together. A junction where they'd turned left now had four branches instead of three, the fourth opening into a corridor of dense crystal formations that definitely hadn't existed an hour ago. Kael's knife marks were still on the walls — the ivory substance accepted cuts and held them — but some of the marks had travelled. Inches, not feet. The surfaces they were carved into had shifted, carrying the marks with them the way driftwood carried initials.

"The route has changed," Kael said, his voice flat and professional. Not a question.

"It's growing," Sera said. "Still growing. It shifted while we were down here."

"Can you find the way?"

The beat. The breath. Then she reached — not for a spell, not for power, but for the passive sensitivity that let her feel the tunnel system the way you felt a draft. Direction. Distance. Open and closed. The pathfinding didn't cost the way casting did, and she was grateful for that, because what she had left wouldn't stretch far.

"That way," she said, pointing toward the passage they'd come through. "It's narrower now, but it goes."

It went. For fifty yards. Then it didn't.

The passage they'd used to descend into the deep tunnels — the service access beneath the collapse zone — had narrowed further. The walls had grown inward, the pale material thickening, the gap between them reduced to something Kael could fit through sideways but Aldric, the tallest of them, could not.

Sera looked at the gap. She looked at her hands. She felt the fog — the heaviness in her reserves, the distance between her and the Aether, as if the river had retreated a step and she had to lean further to reach it.

The beat. The breath.

She reached for Kinesis. The power came with the grudging cooperation of something that was tired of being asked. She shaped it toward the wall, pushing, willing the gap to widen. The ivory substance resisted for a moment, then yielded — slowly, like a door on heavy hinges. The gap widened. Aldric could fit.

The cost landed immediately. A deepening of the hollow feeling, the reserves drawing down another notch. Her hands trembled. The tremor was worse than before the cast — additive, building on the fatigue she'd been carrying since the barrier. She flexed her fingers and they responded slowly, as if the signal between her brain and her muscles was traveling through something thick.

"Thank you," Aldric said as he passed through the gap. His eyes moved to her hands, then away. She knew what he was doing. Counting. Measuring. The silent arithmetic of a healer watching someone spend what they couldn't afford.

She appreciated the silence more than she would have appreciated the concern.

---

They moved. The upper tunnels were closer now — she could feel the Galvanic pressure beginning to reassert itself above them, the staircase gradient stepping down. The crystals thinned. The luminescence dimmed. The world was remembering what it was supposed to be.

Then: another obstacle. A section where the ceiling had lowered and crystal formations had grown across the path at head height, a lattice of pale material that blocked the passage like a frozen waterfall.

The beat. The breath. Longer this time.

She reached again. Kinesis, directed at the crystal lattice, pushing it aside the way you'd push aside a curtain. The power came, and she could feel the fog — the reach was longer, the response slower, as if she were shouting down a hallway that had gotten longer since the last time. The crystals shifted. Some of them. Not enough. The gap she'd opened was barely wide enough for Sera herself, let alone the others.

She had to cast again.

The second cast cost her as much as the first even though the effect was smaller. The hollowing deepened. Her ears rang with the thin tone she'd been carrying since the barrier — fainter now, or maybe she was just too tired to hear it properly. The crystals shifted further. The gap widened. Kael went through first, then Mira, then Aldric. Sera went last, and getting through the gap was a physical effort that shouldn't have been, her arms shaking when she pulled herself past the last crystal edge.

Diminishing returns. Her body telling her she was approaching a limit she couldn't see but could feel the way you felt the edge of a cliff in the dark — not by sight but by the quality of the air and the sound of empty space ahead.

Aldric looked at her after the second cast. He didn't speak. But his expression said what his voice didn't: *that's enough.*

She nodded. Not a concession to his authority — he didn't have authority over her casting, and they both knew it. An agreement between two people who understood the math. She was done. Whatever came next, she'd handle it without magic, the way Kael handled everything — with hands and will and the stubborn refusal to let the world dictate terms.

---

At the junction between the deep tunnels and the upper drainage system: three people waiting.

Not Fels. His crew. Armed — a crossbow, a short sword, a truncheon. Professional, the way Fels was professional: clean equipment, calm eyes, the patience of people who were paid to wait and didn't mind the work. A woman with the crossbow led them. She was short and broad-shouldered, her hair pulled back, her expression blank in the way that people trained in violence cultivated blankness. She didn't introduce herself.

"The case," she said.

Mira stepped forward. She showed the transport case — latches reclosed, the Syndicate stamps visible, the broken seals replaced with a loop of twine tied in the knot a courier would use to pledge that the contents had been inspected and the case closed in good faith. The woman saw the twine. Her eyes flicked to Mira's face and back. She didn't ask about it. She didn't ask what they'd found in the tunnels or what condition the deep passages were in. Fels had told her what to collect and nothing else; that was how Fels operated. Information was currency, and you didn't give it to employees.

"Follow us out," the woman said.

They followed. The upper tunnels, the drainage system, the service passages, the familiar geography of infrastructure that still looked like what it was supposed to be. The Syndicate crew moved quickly and without conversation, their route marked with chalk symbols that Sera recognized from the outbound journey. The escort was insurance, not backup. Fels trusted his crew to collect the case. He didn't trust the Society to deliver it without supervision.

The storm drain. The night air of the foundry's west side. The smell of rust and old oil.

The Galvanic hum came back like a door slamming.

Sera flinched. The transition — she'd been dreading it for the last hundred yards, feeling the Aetheric warmth fading in steps as they climbed, the staircase gradient reversing — was as brutal as she'd feared. The Aether went from flood to drought in three steps. The copper taste vanished. Her compass stopped spinning and hung dead. The dry metallic pressure of the Factory Quarter settled over her like a weighted blanket made of static, and the sensation of a fundamental sense being smothered — a sense she'd been relying on for hours, a sense that had guided them through shifting tunnels and growing walls — was physically painful.

Her eyes ached. Her ears rang. The thin tone spiked and then went silent, replaced by the ordinary silence of a city that hummed on frequencies she couldn't normally hear.

Depleted as she was, there was nothing left to buffer the shift. No reserves to cushion the transition. She felt every increment of the smothering, and each one was a small, specific loss.

She bit the inside of her cheek and kept walking.

In the foundry yard, under the grey-orange light of the Factory Quarter's arc lamps, the Syndicate crew took the transport case from Kael's hands. The woman with the crossbow weighed the case briefly, checked the twine knot at the latch — a glance, no comment — tucked the case under one arm, and led her people into the shadow of the loading bay without a word.

The four of them stood in the yard. The arc lamps hummed. The cobblestones were slick with condensation. Somewhere to the east, past the factory district, past the gradient, the Veilwright Quarter breathed its copper air and moved its shadows, and Sera couldn't feel it from here. She was cut off. Dry. Empty in a way that sleep might fix but that tonight, right now, felt permanent.

Mira's expression as she watched the crew leave — the calculation visible for just a moment before the mask returned. The mental arithmetic of what they'd given away versus what they'd kept. They'd handed over the case. They'd kept the knowledge.

Sera's hands had stopped trembling. She didn't know if that was recovery or if she was just too tired to shake.
