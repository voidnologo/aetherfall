# Chapter 3

The Galvanic hum hit Sera two blocks from the foundry.

It always started the same way — a pressure behind her eyes, a dryness in the air that had nothing to do with weather. The Factory Quarter broadcast on a frequency that most people couldn't hear and Sera couldn't ignore. It pushed the Aether down, smothered it, turned the sweet copper current that ran through the Veilwright Quarter into something thin and distant, like trying to hear music through a wall. She hated it here. Not the place — the sensation. The feeling of a fundamental sense being pressed flat, like breathing through gauze that someone kept pulling tighter.

The foundry was as they'd left it months ago. Dark windows. Cold smokestacks against a sky that hadn't decided between clouds and stars. Chained loading bay doors — the chains newer than the last time, she noticed. Someone was maintaining the fiction that this building was merely abandoned.

They went around the west side, single file, through industrial debris that smelled of rust and old oil. Fels's instructions had been precise: the storm drain behind the third retaining wall, concealed by a sheet of corrugated metal that was bolted at the top and swung free at the bottom. Kael went first, knife in hand. Mira second. Aldric third, satchel cinched tight against his body. Sera last, pulling the metal sheet back into place behind her.

The drain was wide enough to walk upright but not wide enough to walk comfortably. The air changed immediately — cooler, damper, the Galvanic hum receding with every step down. The lanterns made the walls orange and close.

Then the Aether came back.

Nothing gradual about it. None of the days-long gradient she'd felt approaching the Wilds, the slow intensification from normal to alive. Here it returned in a lurch — one step, silence; the next, the copper taste flooding her mouth, her compass spinning against her collarbone, the air thickening into something she could almost feel against her skin. The transition was so abrupt she stumbled, catching herself against the tunnel wall. The brick was warm under her palm.

"You alright?" Kael, ahead of her, not turning. Listening.

"Zone shift," she said. "Sharp one."

They kept moving. Ten more steps and the Aether dropped again — gone, like a door slamming. The Galvanic hum pressed back in, drier and closer than on the surface. Sera's teeth ached. Her compass snapped to a hard heading and held — not north, not anywhere that mattered on a map, but some buried line in the foundry's foundations above them. Locked. Certain. The needle committed to its wrong answer with a rigidity that, in its own way, was as informative as the sweet copper current had been. It told her she was in a strong Galvanic pocket the way the fixed stare of a cat told you there was something in the wall.

"The gradient here isn't a slope," she said, because Aldric would want to know and because saying it aloud helped her understand it. "It's a staircase. Sharp transitions, like — like the two forces are fighting for territory inch by inch."

"Contested zone," Aldric murmured, half to himself. She heard his pencil scratching. He was writing while he walked, the field notebook balanced on his forearm. "The readings from the Harker job showed gentle transition. This is fundamentally different."

The upper tunnels were recognizable from that first job — old drainage infrastructure, brick-lined channels, iron grating. The industrial bones of a water system that predated the Tear. But the changes since their last visit were visible in the lantern light. Crystalline growths on the walls, thicker and denser than before, following the tunnel's geometry in patterns that looked less like mineral accretion and more like something planted. The blue-white luminescence from within the crystals was bright enough that the tunnels ahead had a faint ambient glow even before the lanterns reached them.

They came to a section where the ceiling had partially collapsed — old brickwork sagging, rubble choking the passage. Not impassable, but awkward. Kael was already assessing the best route over when Sera reached for the Aether.

It was instinct. A problem she could solve with a shove of force — clear the debris, open the path. She reached the way she always reached: not with her hands, not with words, but with the part of her that had been touching the current since before she had a name for it. The river. The vast invisible thing that ran beneath the world like blood beneath skin. You didn't command it. You opened yourself to it and shaped what came through.

Nothing came through.

She pulled. The Aether responded — barely. A trickle where she'd expected a stream. She pushed the trickle toward the rubble and felt it arrive like a breath against a boulder. The debris shifted. Dust fell. A brick rocked three inches to the left and settled.

That was all. A cough where she'd reached for a shout.

She was standing in a Galvanic pocket. The staircase gradient had dipped without warning, the zone shifting mid-tunnel, and she'd reached for magic in a stretch of air that was pressing it flat. The force she'd summoned was barely worth the effort — a weak shove that accomplished nothing useful. Like trying to light a match in a rainstorm.

Kael glanced back at her. No comment. No judgment. He climbed over the rubble by hand, finding footholds in the broken brickwork with the practiced economy of a man who'd spent years navigating things that were broken. Mira followed. Aldric ducked through a gap, his satchel catching on a protruding brick. Sera went last, pulling herself over the debris with her arms, the physical effort a reminder that she was a slight woman with a belt knife and not much upper body strength, and that magic was not always available to make up the difference.

She swallowed the frustration. It tasted like the Galvanic hum — metallic and flat.

They rounded the next bend and the Aether flooded back. The transition hit her like stepping from a cold room into sunlight — a rush of warmth, the copper taste filling her mouth, her compass spinning so fast the clicking of brass against its housing was audible. Her skin prickled. The crystal formations on the walls blazed with blue-white light, and for a moment the tunnel was so bright the lanterns were redundant.

"Here," she said, because she needed to stop, just for a moment. "Give me a moment."

The others waited. Kael watched the tunnel ahead. Mira watched Sera. Aldric watched his instruments, whatever they were telling him making his eyebrows climb.

Sera closed her eyes and let the Aether wash through her.

This was the part that nobody else understood. Not Aldric, who studied the Aether from the outside, cataloguing its effects with the patient precision of a man describing a river he'd never swum in. Not Kael, who saw it as a tool at best and a danger at worst. Not Mira, who understood it as a resource to be managed. They knew what it did. They didn't know what it felt like.

It felt like standing in a river you couldn't see. The current pushed against you — warm, vast, indifferent to your plans. You could reach into it and pull, and something always came. But the river didn't care what you wanted. It gave you what it gave you. Sometimes a trickle. Sometimes a flood. The skill wasn't in commanding it. The skill was in shaping what arrived.

She'd been doing this since childhood. Before she had words for it, before anyone told her it was called the Aether, before the world decided it was dangerous and wonderful and worth fighting over. When she was small, objects near her would drift when she was angry. Her mother's teacups, sliding across the table. A door slamming when nobody touched it. She'd thought she was haunted. Then she'd thought she was broken. Then Old Ren in the Quarter had found her and told her what she was, and the world rearranged itself around a new truth: the river was always there. She'd just been born knowing how to swim.

She opened her eyes. The tunnel ahead was bright with crystalline light, the blue-white glow pulsing in a rhythm that was almost but not quite regular. Like breathing, if the thing breathing were the size of a building.

"Moving," Kael said. They moved.

---

The collapse announced itself with sound before sight — a groan from the ceiling, deep and structural, followed by a crack that ran through the brickwork like a fault line opening. The crystals along the walls flared. The Aetheric current surged, spiking so sharply that Sera gasped — the zone pocket they'd entered was suddenly, violently alive, the power in the air jumping from strong to something she didn't have a word for.

"Back up!" Kael, already moving toward them, putting himself between the team and the ceiling that was shedding brickwork in chunks. Not a true collapse — the tunnel molting, the old skin shearing away from the crystalline substrate that was replacing it. Debris rained down. A brick struck Aldric's shoulder and he stumbled sideways, catching himself against the wall.

Sera reached.

The Aether was right there. Not the thin trickle from the Galvanic pocket — a flood. A torrent. She pulled for a barrier, wanting a shield across the top of the passage, something to deflect the falling stone long enough to get clear.

What came was not what she reached for.

The power rushed through her faster than she could shape it. It was like cupping water from a fire hose — you got what you wanted and ten times more. The magic answered the way it always answered, on its own terms, and its terms today were *everything*. She felt the barrier forming and tried to hold it to the shape she intended — a flat shield, ceiling-wide, temporary — but the current was too strong and her hands on it too small. The shape escaped her. Expanded. Became something else.

The barrier erupted outward: not a shield across the ceiling but a dome of rippling force that sealed the entire tunnel section around them, floor to ceiling, wall to wall. It did not just stop. It pushed. The shearing ceiling, still in the act of shedding, was slammed upward and held there, the falling brick pinned against a surface that answered the downward weight with its own countering force. The walls bulged outward where the dome met them, the crystalline skin straining, the old brickwork visibly cracked into a wider cavity than it had occupied a second ago. The blue-white energy was visible, humming, casting light that made the crystals on the walls look dull by comparison. Inside the dome the air was still and warm and faintly electric, like the moment before a thunderstorm that would never arrive.

They were safe. Completely, dramatically safe. The dome was beautiful.

The cost hit a second later.

Sera's knees buckled. Not a stumble — a surrender, her legs simply declining to hold her weight. The exhaustion arrived like ice water poured through her veins, a draining so sudden and so deep that for a moment she couldn't tell if she was standing or falling. She was falling. She caught herself on one knee and one palm against the tunnel floor, her arms shaking, and the shaking wasn't fear — it was the body telling the mind that it had just written a check on reserves it didn't have.

She felt it in specific terms: a hollowing. As if the spell had reached into her and taken everything in the top drawer and most of the second. The sensation was not pain but absence — the place where her energy had been was empty now, and the emptiness had weight.

Then the burn.

A flash of heat behind her eyes, sharp enough to make her gasp. Her vision whited out for a half-second — not blindness, but an overexposure, as if something too bright had fired inside her skull. When it cleared, Kael was staring at her.

His expression told her what her eyes were doing before she could feel it. Something in his face shifted — not the concern she was used to seeing, but something older and more complicated. The look of a man watching something he loved do something he couldn't protect it from.

"Your eyes," he said.

She knew. She could feel it now — a heat behind the irises, a brightness she couldn't turn off. Not the faint ring from the Wilds, the one that had become her new normal, the blue-white discoloration she'd been pretending was cosmetic. This was different. Brighter. The kind of glow that was visible in the tunnel's ambient light, that made her eyes into something you'd notice from across a room. Light pouring through stained glass, if the glass were her.

It would fade, she knew — over minutes, maybe hours, the glow receding in increments like embers cooling. Not on a timescale she could watch it happen. The ringing in her ears settled into a high thin tone that nobody else could hear, and her hands wouldn't hold still.

"I'm fine," she said, because that's what you said.

Aldric was watching her. The expression she'd learned to read as professional concern masking personal worry — his eyebrows level, his mouth set, his eyes cataloguing. She knew what he was doing. Measuring. Comparing her to whatever baseline he'd established in his head. Adding this data point to the running tally he thought she didn't know about. She'd rather he yelled at her. Yelling she could argue with. The silent observation just made her feel like a specimen.

The dome held. She could feel it drawing from her — a sustained drain, shallow but constant, like a faucet left running. She could also feel, through the drain, what the dome was doing to the tunnel: the pressure pushing outward against brick and crystal, holding a volume of collapse in suspension. Ceiling, debris, the strained geometry of the walls — all of it pent behind the force.

"I can't just let it go," she said. Her voice was thin. "When I drop this, everything it's holding up comes down."

Kael understood faster than she'd expected. He was already looking past her, lantern raised, scanning the shape of the tunnel beyond the dome's edge — past the bulged section, past the shearing ceiling, to where the walls still held a clean line.

"Twenty feet," he said. "Past the bend. The stone there hasn't moved."

"We go," Mira said. "Sera holds. She drops it when we're clear."

They moved. Kael first, slipping past the dome's curve at the tunnel's edge where the force thinned against the stone — a careful passage along the narrow margin where the barrier met the wall. Mira second, a hand on Aldric's shoulder, steering him through. Aldric third, the satchel clutched against his chest, his eyes on the strained ceiling above them with the horrified professional interest of a man calculating load distributions in a structure that was about to stop existing.

Sera went last. The dome thinned behind her as she passed its perimeter, the energy tapering, the outer edge dissolving in her wake so she could step through without collapsing the whole. It was a trick she couldn't have described — the same instinct that had always told her the shape of a spell, now telling her where the shape could give and where it couldn't. The effort of the partial release alone was a new kind of exhaustion, finer than the initial cost, a muscle she hadn't known she was using.

They reached the stable ground. Kael's lantern lit a section of tunnel that had not been touched by the pressure. Solid floor. Walls that met the ceiling at honest angles. Twenty feet back toward the surface, the dome still glowed — a blister of light in the dark.

"Now," Kael said.

She let it go.

The sound was less a crash than a long, tearing sigh — the held collapse relaxing into itself, the bulged walls exhaling inward, the suspended ceiling resuming its interrupted fall. Brick rained. Crystal shattered in bright arcs that flared and died. A gust of displaced air rolled past them, smelling of dust and the electric copper of spent magic. The dust plume reached the lantern. The light went orange and small.

When it cleared, the tunnel behind them was sealed — a jumble of brickwork and crystalline debris filling the space the dome had held open, the collapse now complete in the geometry the dome had shaped. They weren't going back that way.

"Forward," Kael said. It wasn't a suggestion. It was an acknowledgment.

Sera got to her feet. The world tilted for a moment, then steadied. Everything felt harder — the air heavier, her thoughts moving through something thick. The next time she reached for the Aether, it would be through fog. She knew this the way you knew your legs were heavy after running. Not a decision. A fact.

"I'm walking," she said.

Kael held her gaze for a moment longer. Then he turned, knife in hand, and led them deeper.

---

The tunnels continued. The crystals thickened. The luminescence brightened until Kael capped the lanterns and they walked by the tunnel's own light — blue-white, pulsing, alive. The pale material that had been filling gaps between bricks in the upper tunnels was now replacing the brickwork entirely, seamless and warm. The drainage infrastructure was disappearing. In its place: corridors. Architecture. Something built by no one for a purpose no one understood.

Sera navigated by feel. The pathfinding didn't cost the way casting did — it was passive, a sensitivity rather than an expenditure. She could feel the tunnels ahead the way you could feel the draft from an open window: direction, distance, a sense of what was open and what was closed. The current of the Aether ran through the passages the way water ran through a river system, and she could read the grain of it the way a riverman read current against rock.

The compass at her throat spun in lazy, insistent circles, the brass ticking against its housing. Not guidance. Barometer. It was telling her what the air was already telling her — that the Aether here was strong, unmediated, running thick. She ignored the circles and followed the current.

The fact that she could do this here — underground, beneath a Galvanic factory, in territory that should suppress everything she was — frightened her and didn't surprise her. The rules down here weren't the rules above. The staircase gradient that had frustrated her in the upper tunnels had given way to something deeper and more constant. The Aether here was thick and strange — not the wild vastness of the forest, not the moderate hum of the Quarter. Structured. Purposeful. Like the current in a river that knew exactly where it was going.

She kept her hand off the compass and followed the feel, and tried not to think about what it meant that the Aether down here was less like a river than a road.

A crystal formation on the wall shifted as they passed. Barely perceptible — a slow adjustment, like a sleeper turning in bed. The tunnel was still growing.

Sera watched it and felt the pulse quicken, as if it had noticed them noticing.

They walked on, into the deep.
