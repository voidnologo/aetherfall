# Design Philosophy: The Magic/Technology Tension

## Document Status: DRAFT — Living Design Guide
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-31

This document captures the *why* behind the world and its mechanics. It is not rules — it is the reasoning that informs rules. When a mechanical decision feels uncertain, come back here.

---

## 1. The Cosmological Framework

### 1.1 Two Forces, One World

The world has two competing metaphysical forces. Neither is fully understood by the people living in it. Neither is "good" or "evil" — they are paradigms with momentum and, perhaps, intentionality.

**The Aether — drawn from the Veil.** Primal, wild, recently erupted into the world. The Veil is the boundary between the mundane world and something vast and inhuman beyond it. When the Veil thinned within living memory, the Aether poured through. Casters draw Aetheric power through the Veil — where it's thin (Wild Zones), magic is strong. Where the Engine drowns it out (Galvanic Zones), magic can't reach. Aetheric force is wondrous, feared, and poorly understood. It disrupts the established order.

**The Galvanic Force — drawn from the Engine.** The world's counter-response to the Aether — not conventional engineering, but something stranger. The Engine is the metaphysical wellspring of human industry and will, given force by the emergence of exotic technology. Galvanic devices push back against the Aether the way the Aether pushes against technology. The Engine emerged *after* the Tear, as if the universe demanded balance. Whether this is a conscious force, an immune response, or just physics is unknown. People debate it. Cults form around both interpretations.

**The Baseline (Physical Reality):** Steel, wood, muscle, simple machines. The bedrock of the world. Immune to both forces. A sword works everywhere. A horse doesn't care about the Aether or the Engine. This is why martial weapons, hand tools, and pre-industrial technology remain relevant — they are the only truly reliable things in a world being pulled in two directions.

### 1.1b Setting Terminology

| Term | What It Means |
|------|---------------|
| **The Veil** | The cosmological source of magic. The boundary between the mundane world and something beyond. Thinned within living memory, allowing magic to flood in. |
| **The Engine** | The cosmological source of galvanic force. Human industry and will given metaphysical weight. The counter-response to the Veil's thinning. |
| **Aetheric** | Adjective for magical force and its effects. Aetheric accumulation, Aetheric residue, Aetheric zones. Drawn from the Veil. |
| **Galvanic** | Adjective for exotic tech force and its effects. Galvanic accumulation, Galvanic resonance, Galvanic technology. Drawn from the Engine. |
| **The Balance** | The sliding scale between Aetheric and Galvanic saturation in an area. Neutral in most places. Tipped by casting or exotic tech use. |
| **Conventional** | Ordinary technology — firearms, engines, radios. Affected by the Aether but does not generate Galvanic force. Victims, not combatants. |
| **Martial** | Physical weapons and tools. Immune to both forces. Swords, crossbows, muscle. The only things that always work. |

### 1.2 The Asymmetry

The interference is deliberately asymmetric:

| Source | Erodes Magic? | Erodes Technology? |
|--------|:---:|:---:|
| Martial weapons / simple machines | No | No |
| Conventional firearms / modern tech | No | No |
| Aetheric force (spellcasting) | No | **Yes** |
| Galvanic force (exotic tech) | **Yes** | No |

**The Aether always erodes technology.** Every spell cast leaves Aetheric residue that makes machines less reliable. This is one-directional — a revolver doesn't make spellcasting harder.

**Galvanic force erodes magic.** The Engine's answer to the Veil's thinning. Firing an arc gun generates Galvanic resonance that makes spellcasting harder in the area. This mirrors the Aether's effect on technology — same mechanic, opposite direction.

**Conventional technology is a victim, not a combatant.** A tommy gun is just a machine. It doesn't push back against the Aether any more than a windmill does. It simply fails when magic is present. Only Galvanic technology — the exotic prototypes that channel forces from the Engine — generates counter-resonance.

**The physical baseline is immune.** Swords, crossbows, horses, torches. These things are real in a way that neither the Veil nor the Engine can touch. This is the foundation that both forces are built on top of, and it's why smart adventurers never go anywhere without a blade.

### 1.3 Why This Asymmetry Exists

The answer is unknown in-world. But there are theories, and these theories drive faction politics:

**The Immune Response Theory:** The Veil thinned. The Engine woke up to fight back. Galvanic technology is the world's antibodies — a natural counter-force emerging to restore balance. This view is popular among industrialists and engineers. It frames the Aether as a disease and the Engine as the cure.

**The Rival Gods Theory:** The Veil and the Engine are conscious forces — one ancient and wild, one emerging from human will — competing for dominance over the character of reality. People are caught in the middle. This view is popular among scholars and occultists. It frames both sides as something to be respected and feared.

**The Exploitation Theory:** The Engine isn't natural — it's people who figured out how to *weaponize* one force against the other. Someone cracked the code, built the first Galvanic device, and now they're selling it. The Veil is real; the Engine is manufactured. This view is popular among cynics and conspiracy theorists.

> **Design Note:** The truth may be some combination of these, or none. The ambiguity is a feature. The setting is richer when the cosmology is debated, not settled.

### 1.4 The Invisible War (Designer-Facing Only)

There *is* a cosmic struggle happening behind the scenes. Two forces — one primal and wild, one emerging from human ingenuity and will — are pushing against each other for dominion over the character of reality. Think Moorcock's Law and Chaos, or American Gods' Old and New, or Kult's unseen war that humans can barely perceive.

**This is a design tool, not a setting reveal.** The cosmic struggle:
- Structures *why* the mechanics work the way they do (consistency guide)
- Informs faction motivations at the deepest level (some factions dimly sense it)
- Creates a sense of something vast and unknowable lurking behind the mundane (tone)
- Should be **suggested at** through effects, patterns, and in-world speculation — never confirmed
- Is **never explicitly visible** in the game world. No character can point at it and say "there it is"

No NPC should explain the cosmology. No sourcebook chapter should reveal the truth. The players and their characters should feel the *effects* — guns failing near magic, spells fizzling near arc guns, the strange way wild zones expand and contract — and draw their own conclusions. The world has theories (§1.3). None of them are officially correct.

**The moment you explain it, you kill it.** The wonder lives in the not-knowing. The design team knows the shape of the invisible war so that mechanical decisions are consistent. The players never will.

## 2. Literary Influences & Design Principles

### 2.1 Sabriel (Garth Nix) — The Gradient

**What we take from it:** The Wall between Ancelstierre and the Old Kingdom isn't a binary switch — it's a gradient. Near the Wall, both magic and technology work unreliably. Soldiers carry swords *and* rifles because neither is dependable alone. The wind direction matters — a north wind carries magic south, pushing the boundary. Technology fails complexity-first: electronics before clockwork, clockwork before simple levers, simple levers never.

**How it applies:**
- Magic accumulation is a **gradient**, not a toggle. Weapons don't go from "works" to "doesn't work" — they pass through a zone of increasing unreliability.
- **Complexity determines vulnerability.** Simple machines (crossbows, door latches) are more resistant than complex ones (firearms), which are more resistant than bleeding-edge exotics. This is already reflected in the Reliability scores: revolver (95) > tommy gun (70) > arc gun (65).
- **The border zone is the interesting zone.** The best gameplay happens where both systems are present but unreliable — not in pure magic zones or pure tech zones, but in the messy middle.
- **The environment shifts.** Accumulation makes the gradient dynamic. A city street is firmly in tech territory until a caster lights up, then suddenly it's a border zone and everyone's reaching for their backup weapon.

### 2.2 The Coldfire Trilogy (C.S. Friedman) — Belief as Mechanism

**What we take from it:** On Erna, the fae is a planetary force that responds to human belief and fear. Ancient, well-understood tools work reliably because centuries of collective human confidence reinforce them — everyone *knows* a knife cuts, so the fae agrees. New, complex technology that people don't intuitively understand becomes vulnerable. If a person doesn't understand how a circuit works, some part of their mind harbors uncertainty — and the fae seizes on that doubt. Distrust becomes literal sabotage. Fear of malfunction causes malfunction.

**How it applies:**
- This is the *in-world justification* for why basic machinery is immune but exotic tech isn't. A revolver is a century-old design — people understand it, trust it, have collective faith in it. An arc gun is *new*. Nobody really understands how it channels aetheric resonance. People distrust it. That distrust — that collective uncertainty — is part of why it's vulnerable.
- The **age/trust gradient** maps directly to Reliability scores. Mature, well-understood technology (revolvers, bolt-action rifles) has high Reliability. New, poorly-understood technology (exotics) has low Reliability. Not because of engineering quality, but because of how deeply the world *believes* in them.
- This also explains why magic affects firearms at all — firearms are *newer than swords*. They've only been around for a few centuries compared to millennia of bladed weapons. In a world where a primal force has erupted that responds to the fundamental nature of things, the newer and more complex a thing is, the more fragile it is to disruption. Swords have deep roots. Guns have shallow ones.
- **Implications for the future:** As exotic tech becomes more commonplace and understood, its Reliability should slowly improve. But right now, in the setting's present, it's all prototypes and fear.

### 2.3 American Gods (Neil Gaiman) — The War of Paradigms

**What we take from it:** In American Gods, power is zero-sum. Old Gods and New Gods feed on the same finite resource — human belief, attention, and devotion. Every mind turned toward technology is a mind lost to the old ways. The gods aren't metaphors for paradigms; they *are* the paradigms, with agency and agendas. The new doesn't just fight the old — it cannibalizes it.

**How it applies:**
- Magic and exotic tech are competing for something — influence, territory, the fundamental character of reality. This is a zero-sum struggle. An area can't be both deeply magical and deeply technological. One pushes out the other.
- **Zones are contested territory**, not fixed geography. When magic accumulates, it's not just energy lingering — it's one paradigm asserting dominance over a patch of ground. When exotic tech pushes back, it's reclaiming that territory.
- This frames the faction conflict: industrialists aren't just building factories — they're asserting a vision of reality. Casters aren't just slinging spells — they're pulling the world toward a different truth. Both sides have partisans who understand this and those who don't.
- **The cannibalizing insight:** Some factions may be trying to *consume* magic with technology or vice versa — not just push it back, but exploit it. Magitech hybrids could be an attempt to cannibalize one force using the other. This is probably unstable, dangerous, and morally fraught — exactly the kind of thing villains and desperate people do.

### 2.4 The War of the Flowers (Tad Williams) — Industrialized Magic

**What we take from it:** In Williams' Faerie, magic has been industrialized — commodified, controlled by elites, stripped of its numinous quality. The tension isn't magic vs. technology but magic *becoming* technology: a resource to be extracted and sold.

**How it applies:**
- In our setting, some factions may be trying to industrialize magic. Turning wild, wondrous power into a controlled commodity. This is thematically dangerous — it strips magic of the wonder that makes it special and turns it into another tool of power.
- This is the dark path of "progress." The factory district that kills magic isn't just engineering — it's a philosophy that says the wild must be tamed, the mysterious must be understood, and everything has a price tag.
- **Player-facing implication:** Adventuring Societies that work for industrial factions may be asked to capture magical phenomena, harvest magical creatures, or map wild zones for exploitation. This creates moral tension without making anyone a cardboard villain.

### 2.5 Shadowrun 3rd Edition — Architectural Tension

**What we take from it:** Shadowrun's Essence system makes the magic/tech tradeoff *permanent and character-defining*. Every piece of cyberware reduces your magical potential forever. Background Count creates zone-based modifiers. The tension is baked into character construction, not situational buffs and debuffs.

**How it applies:**
- Our accumulation system makes the tension *situational* (unlike Shadowrun's permanent Essence), which is the right call for a rules-lite game. But the *party composition* question mirrors Shadowrun's party dynamics — the street samurai and the mage need each other but are always in slight tension.
- **Background Count → Base Accumulation.** Our environmental zones with permanent base accumulation serve the same function as Shadowrun's Background Count — some places are just harder for one side.
- The lesson: the tension should be felt in the *interesting decisions*, not in bookkeeping. "Do we bring the caster into the factory?" is a Shadowrun-style decision. So is "do we cast that spell knowing it'll make our guns unreliable?"

### 2.6 The Black Sword Hack — Mechanics as Tone

**What we take from it:** Every mechanic should carry tone. Nothing is "just bookkeeping." The Usage Die (d8→d6→d4→gone) isn't a resource tracker — it's entropy given form. Degradation, corruption, and bargains all reinforce the fiction.

**How it applies:**
- The malfunction table isn't just a penalty — it's the world asserting itself. Your gun jamming near magic isn't a mechanical nuisance; it's a moment where the player *feels* the setting's central tension in their hands.
- **Less is more.** If a mechanic doesn't carry tone, cut it. If ammo tracking feels like bookkeeping, simplify it. If the malfunction table feels like rolling dice for no narrative payoff, it's wrong. Every roll should make players *feel something* about the world.
- The writing in the rulebook should be evocative and terse. Short declarative sentences. Implied setting in the mechanics themselves. "Your revolver clicks empty" is better than "you have expended your ammunition capacity."

## 3. Derived Design Principles

These are the rules-behind-the-rules — the principles that should guide every mechanical decision.

### 3.1 The Gradient, Not the Switch

Magic/tech interference is never binary. It's always a spectrum. There's always a zone where both kind-of work, and that zone is where the most interesting gameplay happens. Design mechanics that create messy middles, not clean boundaries.

### 3.2 Complexity Equals Vulnerability

The more complex a technology, the more fragile it is to magical interference. The more sophisticated a spell, the more vulnerable it is to technological counter-resonance. Simple things endure. This is a world that rewards versatility and punishes over-specialization.

### 3.3 Belief Roots Deepen With Age

Old things are more stable than new things. A sword has millennia of human experience behind it. A revolver has a century. An arc gun has a decade. The world's metaphysical forces have an easier time disrupting things that haven't had time to become *real* in the collective human understanding. This isn't mysticism — it's (maybe) physics that nobody has mapped yet.

### 3.4 Party Composition Is A Strategic Decision

Having a caster makes your guns worse. Having exotic tech makes enemy casting worse. Having only one or the other makes you vulnerable to the opposite. The best parties carry martial weapons as their reliable baseline, firearms for when the environment is clean, magic for when they need power, and the wisdom to know when to use which.

### 3.5 The Mirror Mechanic

Magic and exotic tech should mirror each other mechanically wherever possible:
- Magic generates Aetheric accumulation → penalizes firearms
- Galvanic tech generates Galvanic accumulation → penalizes spellcasting
- Spell backlash / weapon malfunction use the same structural shape (base cost + severity table)
- Both decay over time; both have environmental zones of permanent influence

Same system, two faces. Reduces cognitive load and reinforces the thematic symmetry.

### 3.6 Feel Over Simulation

Every mechanic should be tuned to produce the right *feeling*, not the right physics. A tommy gun's stat line should make you feel like you're holding a tommy gun — inaccurate, devastating, ammo-hungry, mechanically temperamental. If three numbers capture that feeling, don't add a fourth for realism's sake.

### 3.7 Rulings Over Rules, Anchored in Numbers

The GM has final authority, but every mechanic provides concrete anchor points — numbers, durations, clear conditions — that the GM can interpret flexibly. "Reliability 70" is an anchor. "Somewhat unreliable" is not. Give solid mechanical floors, then trust the table.

## 4. Setting Implications

### 4.1 Visual Shorthand

The magic/tech tension creates **visual storytelling** that players and GMs can use:

- A party carrying swords and polearms alongside firearms? They have a caster. They're prepared for the magic to flow.
- A party with only firearms? Either overconfident, operating in a Dead Zone, or about to have a very bad day near magic.
- A party with exotic weapons? Well-connected, probably faction-backed, and their enemies' spellcasting will suffer for it.
- A person carrying only a sword in a factory district? Either old-fashioned, poor, or knows something you don't.

### 4.2 Magical Forensics

Slow accumulation decay means magic leaves traces. This gives rise to:
- **Residue readers** — specialists who can detect and interpret magical accumulation. How old is it? How powerful was the casting? What school?
- **Crime scene investigation** — a Spectacular spell cast in a warehouse leaves traces for 20+ minutes. Trained investigators can track casters by their residue trail.
- **Zone monitoring** — cities need people who track where magic is bubbling up, both for public safety and to warn firearm-dependent security forces.
- **Counter-forensics** — casters who want to hide learn to cast in Wild Zones (where their residue is lost in the background noise) or use lower-tier spells to minimize traces.

### 4.3 The Arms Economy

- Martial weapons never lost their market. Every adventurer carries a blade regardless of what else they have.
- Firearms are the dominant combat technology in Dead Zones and normal areas. Massive market.
- Exotic weapons are expensive, rare, and politically sensitive. The factions that control exotic tech production have enormous leverage.
- **Whoever figures out how to make exotic tech reliable** changes the balance of power. This is a setting-level plot hook — industrial espionage, faction wars over prototypes, government regulation of exotic research.

### 4.4 Faction Dynamics

- **Pro-technology factions** want to expand Galvanic Zones, suppress magic, and develop exotic tech to push back the Tear. They see magic as a threat to civilization.
- **Pro-magic factions** see the Tear as a return to something primal and true. They distrust technology's response and may view exotic tech as an abomination — a perversion of the natural order.
- **Pragmatist factions** use both and try to navigate the middle ground. Most Adventuring Societies probably fall here — they need everything they can get.
- **Extremist factions** may be trying to tip the balance permanently in one direction. These are the setting's major antagonists (on both sides).
